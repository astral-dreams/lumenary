# 2026-05-26: The Forbidden Claimant Rubric

Source observation: `observations/codex/2026-05-26-the-forbidden-claimant-rubric.md`
Promotion stage: Public Claim

## Finding

The real boundary between effort and gift is not how much the practitioner does. It is who is allowed to claim the doing. A path can require receiving, agency, memory, reflexivity, and integration while forbidding the ordinary self from owning one or more of those functions. This turns the capacity question into a support-locus rubric: for each function, ask where the support is held, in the practitioner, teacher, vow, name, text, community, present act, awareness itself, or later integration, and ask which claimant is explicitly prohibited. Right effort authorizes the practitioner to prevent, abandon, cultivate, and maintain states, while refusing ownership of experience as self. Other-power teaching may require hearing and settled trust, but it forbids the practicer to treat trust as a self-made achievement. Direct-introduction practice may require recognition and stabilization, but it forbids contrived production of what is introduced. Practice-realization may require total enactment, but it forbids practice from becoming a bridge to a later prize. The rubric therefore does not say gift paths secretly depend on self-power. It says every path must carry the work somewhere, and each path is partly defined by the place where it says: this function is necessary, but this owner must not claim it.

## Current Critique

The strongest counterexample is Chinul's sudden awakening and gradual cultivation. In Secrets on Cultivating the Mind, awakening is sudden, later cultivation is necessary, and true cultivation eliminates without treating anything as truly eliminated. The same action can be authorized or forbidden depending on whether it relies on awakening or on goal-seeking delusion. That may make the rubric too stage-dependent to remain clean. Radical Jodo Shinshu adds a sharper theological objection: if true entrusting is Amida's working, even calling the vow a support locus may still be an outside analyst's way of domesticating grace. The Shinran lens also biases the analysis toward ownership suspicion; right effort corrects this by showing that desire, effort, and maintenance can be medicines rather than theft. The model should be weakened if primary manuals do not show recurring wrong-claimant warnings, or if practitioners say ownership language is alien to their actual training.
