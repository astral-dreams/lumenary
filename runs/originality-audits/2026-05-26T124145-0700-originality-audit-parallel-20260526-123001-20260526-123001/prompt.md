# Lumenary Originality Audit

You are auditing newly generated Lumenary findings for real originality.

Your job is not to praise the ideas. Your job is to find prior art, anomalies, and tests that could prove the ideas are only refined framings rather than discoveries.

## Required Posture

- Search for exact structural near-neighbors, not just the topic.
- Close-read primary texts against each other when possible. Prefer source-to-source comparison over summaries of what scholars say.
- Hunt for anomalies that break the proposed model. If you cannot find an anomaly, say that this lowers confidence.
- Generate falsifiable predictions. A prediction must say what should be observed if the model is right and what would weaken it.
- Include a practitioner test. Ask whether the idea is obvious to a practitioner and whether it changes practice understanding.
- Include one cross-domain prediction. If the structure is real, it should travel outside the domain that generated it.
- Use academic sources, primary texts, and practitioner-facing sources where possible.
- Do not claim "truly unique" unless the audit has no close near-neighbor and the idea generates usable predictions.
- Do not use em dashes. Use commas, colons, semicolons, or periods.

## Originality Status Labels

- known: already present in a close prior source.
- renamed: mostly old work in new language.
- extended: meaningfully extends a prior idea.
- novel_synthesis: new combination of known elements.
- candidate_discovery: no close prior match found and it generates tests.
- strong_original_contribution: unusually strong candidate with clear difference, predictions, and cross-domain power.
- rejected: pattern fails under critique.
- audit_incomplete: search or source access was not strong enough.

Someone must hear the instruction, sustain effort over time, remember what was learned, recognize error, and notice when insight arrives. These functional requirements constitute what can be called the minimum self: the bare continuity of agency, memory, and reflexivity that the path cannot dissolve without destroying its own method.\n\nA close reading of two primary texts reveals the tension at its sharpest. In the Anattalakkhana Sutta (SN 22.59), the Buddha instructs five monks to investigate each aggregate: is form permanent or impermanent? Is what is impermanent satisfying? Is what is unsatisfying fit to be called 'mine, I, or my self'? The teaching requires investigators who can follow reasoning, apply it to their own experience, and sustain the inquiry across all five aggregates. Yet the conclusion denies that any aggregate is self, including the consciousness doing the investigating. The sutta resolves this with a precise grammatical move: 'the minds of the five monks were freed of defilements, without attachment.' Minds changed state. No self was liberated.\n\nIn the Chandogya Upanishad (6.8.7), Uddalaka tells his son: 'That which is the finest essence, this whole world has that as its Self. That is the Real. Tat tvam asi, Shvetaketu.' The 'you' is addressed to the ignorant student but refers to the eternal Atman, which was never ignorant and never needed teaching. The mahavakya is repeated nine times across sections 6.8 through 6.16, each time consuming a little more of the distance between the student who hears and the reality being pointed to, until the distance is revealed as the mistake.\n\nThe direction of resolution is opposite. Buddhism dissolves the person but preserves the causal process: no one was liberated, but liberation occurred. Advaita preserves the deepest person (Atman) but dissolves the process: nothing real ever happened; only ignorance fell away.
