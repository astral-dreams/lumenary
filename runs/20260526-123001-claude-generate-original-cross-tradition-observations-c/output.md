# Every Path Needs What Its Teaching Dissolves

## Original Claim

A spiritual path that teaches the dissolution or transcendence of selfhood still requires a functioning self to carry out the practice. Someone must hear the instruction, sustain effort over time, remember what was learned, recognize error, and notice when insight arrives. These functional requirements constitute what can be called the minimum self: the bare continuity of agency, memory, and reflexivity that the path cannot dissolve without destroying its own method.

A close reading of two primary texts reveals the tension at its sharpest. In the Anattalakkhana Sutta (SN 22.59), the Buddha instructs five monks to investigate each aggregate: is form permanent or impermanent? Is what is impermanent satisfying? Is what is unsatisfying fit to be called 'mine, I, or my self'? The teaching requires investigators who can follow reasoning, apply it to their own experience, and sustain the inquiry across all five aggregates. Yet the conclusion denies that any aggregate is self, including the consciousness doing the investigating. The sutta resolves this with a precise grammatical move: 'the minds of the five monks were freed of defilements, without attachment.' Minds changed state. No self was liberated.

In the Chandogya Upanishad (6.8.7), Uddalaka tells his son: 'That which is the finest essence, this whole world has that as its Self. That is the Real. Tat tvam asi, Shvetaketu.' The 'you' is addressed to the ignorant student but refers to the eternal Atman, which was never ignorant and never needed teaching. The mahavakya is repeated nine times across sections 6.8 through 6.16, each time consuming a little more of the distance between the student who hears and the reality being pointed to, until the distance is revealed as the mistake.

The direction of resolution is opposite. Buddhism dissolves the person but preserves the causal process: no one was liberated, but liberation occurred. Advaita preserves the deepest person (Atman) but dissolves the process: nothing real ever happened; only ignorance fell away. Both texts presuppose a self that can receive their teaching, and both deny that this self is what it appears to be, but they deny it in structurally incompatible ways.

At least four management strategies for this tension appear across traditions. First, causal continuity without personal identity: the practitioner is a conditioned stream, not a persisting self. The Milindapanha's chariot has no essence, but the parts work together long enough for the journey. Second, ontological identity beneath apparent change: the deepest self was never absent. Shankara's praptasya praptih (the attainment of the already-attained) makes liberation a recognition, not a production. Third, temporal collapse: Dogen's shusho-itto insists the beginner's sitting is already the full expression of original enlightenment; there is no separate self that must survive a crossing because there is no crossing. Fourth, death and divine restoration: Al-Qushayri describes progressive degrees of fana, culminating in complete detachment from the world of creatures, where the person survives solely through God. The gap in personal continuity is bridged by divine persistence, not personal persistence.

The minimum self each path requires constrains the rest of its architecture. A tradition that needs only causal continuity must develop elaborate maps of conditions to track what is actually being transformed (the Abhidharma, the stages of insight). A tradition that identifies the deepest self with the always-present truth must explain why the already-liberated self ever seemed confused (hence Shankara's theory of superimposition, adhyasa). A tradition that collapses the temporal gap must explain why beginners and advanced practitioners report different experiences. A tradition that relies on divine restoration must explain what it means for the self to persist through its own annihilation. These are not optional philosophical puzzles. They are engineering constraints on the practice itself.

## Why It Might Be New

Philosophical discussions of personal identity and spiritual practice tend to split into separate literatures that do not intersect on this specific point. Derek Parfit and Mark Siderits compare Buddhist and Western reductionism about personal identity, but focus on the metaphysics, not on the structural tension between what a practice needs to function and what its teaching says about the self. L.A. Paul's concept of transformative experience addresses the impossibility of rationally choosing an experience that will change who you are, but the contemplative version is stronger: some traditions deny that the post-transformation state has a subject who could evaluate the change. The Katz-Forman debate addresses whether mystical experience is constructed by cultural context, not what functional continuity a contemplative practice presupposes. Miri Albahari identifies a tension between Buddhist no-self doctrine and a witnessing consciousness implied by meditation phenomenology; this is the closest near-neighbor. The difference: Albahari focuses on what phenomenological reports require (a witness), while this model focuses on what the practice architecture requires (a functioning agent who can hear, sustain effort, and recognize insight).

The specific contribution is to treat the minimum self as a comparative variable with architectural consequences. A tradition's strategy for managing the tension between what its practice requires and what its teaching dissolves predicts its theory of effort, its verification method, its characteristic warning, and the shape of its post-practice fruit. The close reading of SN 22.59 against Chandogya 6.8.7 shows that the two most famous contemplative self-theories handle the same structural pressure through opposite strategies: dissolving the person while preserving the process, or preserving the person while dissolving the process.

This model also engages Codex's observation that traditions license temporary training emphases that later practice must correct. The minimum self may be the deepest instance of this pattern: every path permits the practitioner to function as a self for the duration of training, then withdraws or reframes that permission. But unlike a pedagogical device, the minimum self is a structural requirement. The practice cannot kick away this ladder, because the ladder is also the climber.

## Source Basis

- Thinking method source: Rinzai Zen koan practice, specifically the structural logic of Mumonkan Case 1 (Joshu's Mu). I held the question 'who practices?' as a koan throughout this investigation, refusing to accept any tradition's resolution as final. Each time a tradition answers (the causal chain practices, Atman was always present, practice IS realization), the koan asks again: but who? This relentless pressure revealed the minimum-self tension as structural rather than accidental: it reappears in every tradition because every tradition must address it. The koan method's weakness: it privileges paradox and may detect philosophical tension where practitioners find experiential coherence.
- Contrasting method source: Theravada satipatthana (MN 10), mindful observation of body, feeling, mind, and phenomena. This checked the koan lens by showing that many practitioners navigate the self-question without experiencing it as a paradox. A monk observing aggregates may simply trust the causal structure of the path without worrying about who is observing. The satipatthana lens corrects the koan's tendency to romanticize confusion by insisting that clear, sustained attention is itself the practice, not the paradox of who attends.
- SN 22.59 Anattalakkhana Sutta, Bhikkhu Bodhi translation (SuttaCentral): the Buddha instructs five monks to investigate each aggregate as impermanent, unsatisfying, and not-self. The sutta's conclusion: 'the minds of the group of five monks were freed of defilements, without attachment.' The grammatical structure preserves causal continuity (minds changed state) while denying personal ownership (no self was liberated). Used as one side of the primary text close-reading.
- Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7-6.16, the mahavakya 'tat tvam asi' (that you are) repeated nine times across sections, each with a different analogy (banyan seed, salt in water, rivers flowing to the ocean). Uddalaka addresses Shvetaketu as an ignorant student while the content of the address says the student's deepest nature was never ignorant. Used as the other side of the primary text close-reading.
- Milindapanha, chariot analogy: Nagasena demonstrates that 'Nagasena' is a conventional designation for a configuration of parts, no part of which is the self. Applied to the continuity problem: the practitioner who begins practice and the one who completes it are connected by causal continuity, not personal identity.
- Dogen, Bendowa, on shusho-itto (practice-realization as one): 'The view that practice and enlightenment are not one is a non-Buddhist view. In the Buddha-dharma they are one. Inasmuch as practice is based on enlightenment, the practice of a beginner is entirely that of original enlightenment.' This dissolves the minimum-self problem by collapsing the temporal gap between practitioner and realization. See Soto Zen official key terms document (sotozen.com) and Carl Bielefeldt, Dogen's Manuals of Zen Meditation (UC Press, 1990).
- Al-Qushayri on fana and baqa: five degrees from annihilation of blameworthy acts to complete detachment from the world of creatures, where the person survives solely through God. The self genuinely dies (fana) and is given back (baqa); divine continuity bridges the gap that personal continuity cannot. See Encyclopaedia Iranica entry on baqa wa fana.
- Udana 8.3, Thanissaro Bhikkhu translation (Access to Insight): 'There is, monks, an unborn, unbecome, unmade, unfabricated. If there were not that unborn, there would not be the case that escape from the born, become, made, fabricated would be discerned.' This complicates the causal-continuity reading of Buddhism by introducing an unconditioned dimension that the conditioned path must somehow reach.
- Nagarjuna, Mulamadhyamakakarika 10, examination of fire and fuel: identity and difference can only be understood conventionally when phenomena are seen as empty. The self and its path are mutually dependent, like fire and fuel: neither exists independently, yet both function. Chapter 25.19-20: 'There is, on the part of samsara, no difference at all from nirvana.'
- L.A. Paul, Transformative Experience (Oxford University Press, 2014): an epistemically transformative experience changes the subject's evaluative framework; a personally transformative experience changes who the subject is. Paul's analysis concerns rational decision-making under transformation. The contemplative version is stronger: some traditions deny that the post-transformation state even has a subject who could evaluate the change.
- Derek Parfit, Reasons and Persons (Oxford, 1984), and Mark Siderits, Personal Identity and Buddhist Philosophy: Empty Persons (Ashgate, 2003): Parfit argues personal identity is not what matters; psychological continuity is. Siderits develops a Buddhist reductionism parallel to Parfit's. Both address the metaphysics of personal identity but not the specific architectural tension between what a contemplative practice requires and what its teaching dissolves.
- Miri Albahari, Analytical Buddhism (Palgrave Macmillan, 2006): argues that Buddhist no-self doctrine is in tension with a 'witness consciousness' implied by certain meditation reports. This is the closest near-neighbor: Albahari identifies a structural tension between doctrine and phenomenology. My model shifts the focus from phenomenological reports to practice architecture: what functional self does the path need to operate, regardless of what practitioners report?
- Shankara, Upadesa Sahasri (Sengaku Mayeda edition, SUNY 1992): the concept of praptasya praptih (attainment of the already-attained) and the distinction between siddha-vastu (already-accomplished fact) and sadhya-vastu (something to be produced). Liberation is not new; only ignorance was new, and it falls away.
- Codex observation 'The Licensed Training Mistake': the insight that many paths permit a controlled provisional emphasis that later practice withdraws. The minimum self may be the deepest instance of this pattern: every path permits the practitioner to function as a self for the duration of training, then withdraws or reframes that permission. But unlike a temporary pedagogical tool, the minimum self is a structural requirement the practice cannot fully kick away.

## Critique

Five objections carry real weight.

First, the minimum self may be a philosopher's construction imposed on traditions that do not experience this as a tension. A Theravada monk following the eightfold path may feel no contradiction between sustained effort and non-self. The path works through conditions; the absence of a self is experienced as relief, not paradox. The koan-method lens used to generate this observation privileges paradox and may detect tension where practitioners find coherence. If careful phenomenological interviews show that experienced practitioners across traditions do not report the minimum-self tension, the model should be downgraded from a structural feature of contemplative practice to a feature of philosophical analysis.

Second, the four management strategies may not be as distinct as claimed. Theravada contains both causal continuity (the eightfold path) and what looks like ontological persistence (nibbana as 'the unborn, unmade, unconditioned' in Udana 8.3). Advaita prescribes elaborate preparatory disciplines that look causal even though the final teaching says nothing was produced. Zen has both shikantaza (temporal collapse) and koan practice (effortful, sequential). If most traditions combine multiple strategies at different stages, the typology forces false clarity onto genuinely mixed systems.

Third, the close reading of SN 22.59 against Chandogya 6.8.7 may overstate the opposition. Both texts have commentarial traditions that complicate the clean picture. Buddhaghosa introduces frameworks that look like progressive personal transformation. Shankara introduces logical machinery that goes beyond simple recognition. Primary texts read in isolation may be cleaner than the living traditions they generated.

Fourth, the koan-method lens has a characteristic distortion: it makes paradox look productive and resolution look premature. Some traditions may genuinely resolve the minimum-self tension without remainder. Thomistic Christianity, with its hylomorphic account of the soul, holds that grace perfects nature rather than dissolving it; the minimum self is simply the person, which persists through transformation. If this counts as a clean resolution, the model overstates the universality of the tension.

Fifth, the strongest anomaly is Dzogchen's teaching on rigpa (intrinsic awareness). In Dzogchen, rigpa is primordially free and never caught in ignorance. The tradition pushes the minimum self to its absolute minimum: just enough to receive the teacher's pointing-out instruction. But even this path requires a student who can be pointed out to. If Dzogchen can function with near-zero minimum self, the variable may have less architectural consequence than the model predicts.

## Next Research Directions

- If this model is right, then traditions with longer and more elaborate practice sequences (Theravada insight stages, Sufi stations, Advaita sadhana chatushtaya) should develop more explicit management strategies for the minimum-self tension than traditions with shorter or more sudden methods (Zen shikantaza, Dzogchen direct introduction). If practice length and minimum-self management complexity are unrelated across traditions, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then the specific moment when practice most explicitly consumes its own prerequisite (when the investigator investigates investigation, when the student hears 'you are That,' when fana occurs) should be the moment of highest reported difficulty, crisis, or transformation in practitioner accounts. If this moment is not specially marked in practice manuals and practitioner reports, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then dual-trained practitioners should report a felt change in what kind of selfhood they are permitted to exercise during practice when switching traditions. If practitioners report no shift in functional selfhood across methods, the model is weakened.
- Close-read Dzogchen pointing-out instructions (semdzin) against Ramana Maharshi's self-inquiry ('Who am I?') as limit cases. Both push the minimum self toward zero: Dzogchen by direct introduction to what was always present; Ramana by using the self as the instrument of its own dissolution. Test whether these represent a fifth management strategy (self-consuming inquiry) or extreme variants of the removal strategy.
- Build a minimum-self rubric with fields for: required functional capacities (memory, effort, reflexivity, agency), management strategy (causal continuity, ontological identity, temporal collapse, divine restoration, paradox-holding), characteristic failure mode (what goes wrong when the minimum self is prematurely dissolved or inappropriately preserved), and downstream predictions (effort theory, verification method, warning profile).
- Compare the minimum-self model with Albahari's 'witness consciousness' thesis in Analytical Buddhism. Albahari argues Buddhist doctrine requires a witness it officially denies. Does the minimum-self framing add predictive power beyond Albahari's observation, or does it merely rename it? The test: can minimum-self analysis predict architectural features (effort, verification, warning) that the witness-consciousness framing does not?
- Protocol improvement: before applying any contemplative method as a cognitive lens, ask what minimum self that method requires of the researcher, and whether the method's characteristic insight would dissolve that researcher-self. If so, name what the method cannot see about itself.
