## Original Claim

A practice has not earned trust because it produced a powerful state or because its own language says the state is final. The working doctrine should be narrower: after any serious practice, ask what claim the practice permits and what remains able to correct the person who makes that claim. A method may confirm a recognition, undermine every claim, be set down after use, or become embodied as conduct. But if the result removes every teacher, text, community, duty, clinical boundary, or observable change that could correct it, the result is not yet spiritually mature. The anomaly in earlier work is therefore decisive: the raft can be released, but the capacity to be corrected must not be released with it.

## Why It Might Be New

The closest prior argument is Smith's reading of Nagarjuna and Wittgenstein as shared therapeutic methods without theses. Sells also gives strong prior art for language that unsays itself, and MN 22 already teaches a tool that must be used and then released. This finding should not claim novelty for self-canceling method, raft logic, or apophatic unsaying. Its possible contribution is the corrective rule that survived the critique: the important post-practice question is not only whether the method validates or undermines itself, and not only whether it is retained, abandoned, or embodied. The sharper test is whether some correction path remains after the result. That turns a broad typology into a human safety rule and a falsifiable coding task.

## Critique

This idea could smuggle institutional authority back into spiritual life under the friendly word correction. Some communities correct honestly, but others use correction to control, shame, or silence. The model may also overfit isolated modern seekers and miss solitary practitioners whose practice is stable, ethical, and freeing without formal recognition partners. Dogen's practice-realization identity strains the model because correction may not come after a threshold at all; it may be the ongoing act of practice itself. Tannisho-style Other Power also strains it because asking where correction is held can reintroduce the self-calculating frame the text resists. If abusive communities, teacher mismatch, or clinical misreading cause more harm than solo interpretation, the teaching must be narrowed further.

## Teaching Candidate

Status: under_dialogue
Tags: method-authority, critique, correction, solo-practice, meaning-loss, achievement-contingent-self-worth, isolation

### Teaching Line

Set down the tool when it is time; do not set down correction.

### Doctrine Claim

A spiritual result should remain answerable to something beyond the practitioner's first interpretation: trusted people, tested texts, conduct, ordinary duties, clinical boundaries, or the effects of the practice over time.

### Teaching Body

After a strong practice, do not rush to own the meaning. Keep the experience close and keep the judgment open. Ask what could correct you: a teacher you trust, a community that knows you, a text you have agreed to learn from, the effect on your conduct, or the ordinary duties you still owe.

Some tools should be set down after they help. Some should be kept. Some become a way of living. The danger is not release itself. The danger is leaving yourself with no way to be corrected when the result flatters, frightens, or isolates you.

### Human Problem

Isolation, meaning loss, digital comparison, and achievement-contingent spiritual self-worth after solitary or mixed-source practice.

### For

Self-directed practitioners, app meditators, cross-tradition readers, retreatants without continuing teachers, and people who turn spiritual experiences into either status or self-doubt.

### Pressure Survived

Survives the MN 22 anomaly by separating release of the raft from release of correction. Survives the Heart Sutra anomaly by treating no-attainment and reliance as compatible. Survives the Mandukya contrast by allowing confirming methods while requiring a correction path for the claim made from them.

### Falsifying Pressure

The teaching weakens if stable solo practitioners report that internal practice grammar corrects them reliably without community, text, duty, or conduct checks; if correction naming increases shame or dependency; or if primary sources show mature release of method and correction with no substitute.



## Practice Candidate

Status: seed
Risk level: low
Tags: correction-check, solo-practice, recognition-gap, low-risk, post-practice, modern-seeker

### Practice Line

After an unusual practice, name what could correct your interpretation.

### Purpose

To test whether a spiritual experience becomes steadier when its interpretation remains answerable to correction rather than being claimed, dismissed, or turned into identity.

### Human Problem

Solo interpretation pressure, isolation, meaning loss, and achievement-contingent self-worth after meaningful practice experiences.

### For

Stable self-directed practitioners, app meditators, cross-tradition seekers, and retreatants without a continuing teacher who have had a meaningful or unusual practice session.

### Not For

Not for acute psychological crisis, psychosis, mania, dissociation, addiction withdrawal, trauma activation, or medical distress. Not for people in abusive communities where correction is used for control. Not needed for practitioners already in regular contact with a trustworthy teacher or care team.

### Time

- Duration: 10 minutes
- Frequency: Only after a practice session that feels meaningful or unusual, no more than twice a week.
- Minimum attempt: Try it after three separate practice moments before judging whether it helps.

### Steps

1. Write what happened in plain words without naming it as attainment, failure, grace, breakthrough, or realization.
2. Write the interpretation you most want to believe.
3. List up to three possible correction sources: a trusted person, a source text, the effect on conduct, ordinary duties, or professional care if distress is involved.
4. For each source, write one sentence it might use to challenge your interpretation.
5. Choose one small action that keeps the experience answerable for seven days: ask a trusted person, reread the relevant source, check whether conduct improves, or delay making the experience part of your identity.
6. If no correction source can be named, keep the experience private and provisional. Do not build a public claim on it yet.

### Notice

- Whether the experience remains meaningful when the label is held loosely.
- Whether the urge to claim status or collapse into doubt decreases.
- Whether correction feels like honest steadiness or like shame.
- Whether ordinary conduct, care, work, and relationships improve or degrade over the next week.

### Caution

Stop if the exercise increases paranoia, obsessive self-checking, shame, spiritual dependency, or fear of your own mind. Seek human support if the experience is frightening, impairing, or clinically concerning.

### Weakens If

The practice weakens if users report more isolation, paralysis, or shame; if it drains meaningful experiences without improving conduct; or if simple rest, ordinary conversation, or established teacher check-ins work better.

## Next Research Directions

- If this model is right, then traditions that release a method should still retain explicit correction paths through conduct, teacher, community, vow, text, or ongoing practice. If mature sources release both method and correction without replacement, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then solo practitioners who name a correction path after unusual practice should show less inflation, less shameful doubt, and less identity-claiming than solo practitioners who only journal the experience. If naming correction increases paralysis or isolation, the practice is weakened.
- Close-read Dogen Genjokoan or Bendowa, Tannisho, Dionysius Mystical Theology, Rinzai koan curriculum, and one Dzogchen or Mahamudra source for cases where correction is continuous, refused, grace-held, or embodied rather than post-threshold.
- Protocol improvement: stop coding method completion from one passage. Code claim permission from one source layer and correction holder from another, then test whether the pair predicts warnings, repairs, and teaching permission in held-out passages.
- Run practitioner interviews with dual-trained practitioners. Ask where they would look for correction after Advaita recognition, Heart Sutra no-attainment, Zen kensho, or devotional surrender. If answers track teacher style more than method structure, lower the model.
