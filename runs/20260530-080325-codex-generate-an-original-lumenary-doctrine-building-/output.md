# Some Help Should Not Be Managed

## Original Claim

Some help is damaged by being turned into a project. Before asking whether a practice should be kept, released, or embodied, three things must be true: the path must let the practitioner provisionally use a method, the path must name some later moment where the result can be handled, and the person must have enough spare agency for review to help rather than burden. If any of these fail, the wiser move may be to let the help be carried by practice, community, vow, rest, or ordinary conduct without another private audit. The ending of a method is not always a decision point; for over-managed people, managing the ending can become the very weight the practice was meant to lift.

## Why It Might Be New

This is not highly novel. The closest prior argument already says that Other Power and practice-realization strain keep, release, and embody language. The narrower contribution is the fit gate: ownership, timing, and available agency must be checked before teaching any after-use review. Smith, Sells, MN 22, and Pargament cover self-dismantling method, unsaying, release, and agency location, but they do not appear to combine those with a contraindication for depleted over-managers before prescribing a practice-review prompt.

## Critique

The strongest counterexample is that Shin and Soto traditions still have concrete forms, teachers, warnings, communities, and conduct expectations. If nembutsu, zazen, vow, teacher correction, and daily conduct function as retained or embodied supports, then custody is relocated, not refused. The human-fit claim can also overreach: some depleted people need structure, accountability, and direct help, not permission to stop reviewing. The MN 1 lens may bias the analysis against traditions where stable recognition, obedient repetition, or faithful practice is not grasping. This finding should weaken or retire if split-source coding shows that ownership and after are not separable variables, or if the No-Audit Check increases avoidance, helplessness, shame, or missed duties.

## Teaching Candidate

Status: under_dialogue
Tags: critique-mode, practice-endings, burnout, achievement-pressure, over-management, other-power, practice-realization

### Teaching Line

Do not turn help into another thing to manage.

### Doctrine Claim

A teaching about practice endings applies only when the path, the moment, and the person can bear review. Some help should be decided about; some help should be received, repeated, shared, or allowed to work without being graded.

### Teaching Body

After something helps you, do not rush to own it. Some help asks for a decision. Some help asks for simple repetition. Some help is held by a person, a promise, a form, or a community. Some help works best when you stop checking it for proof.

If you are already worn down, another review can become more weight. Use review when it leads to clearer conduct. Set review down when it becomes another test of your worth.

### Human Problem

Burnout, compulsive self-monitoring, and achievement-contingent self-worth, especially when rest, insight, or healing becomes another performance.

### For

Over-managing caregivers, founders, professionals, students, and serious practitioners whose reflective habits have become judging habits.

### Pressure Survived

Survived pressure from MN 22's proper-use-and-release pattern, the Heart Sutra's no-attainment-with-practice hybrid, Dogen practice-realization, Shinran Other Power, and near-neighbor scholarship on self-dismantling method, unsaying, and agency location. It survives only as a scoped correction, not as a universal doctrine.

### Falsifying Pressure

Weaken if traditions that appear to refuse ownership or after can still be coded cleanly as retained, released, or embodied without distortion. Weaken further if depleted people benefit more from structured after-use review than from no-audit permission, or if no-audit becomes an excuse for avoidance.



## Practice Candidate

Status: seed
Risk level: low
Tags: no-audit, burnout, self-surveillance, practice-fit, low-risk

### Practice Line

Before reviewing a helpful experience, ask whether review would help.

### Purpose

To test whether suspending review can reduce self-surveillance for people who turn every practice, rest, or insight into a task to grade.

### Human Problem

Burnout, compulsive self-monitoring, and achievement-contingent self-worth.

### For

People who reflexively evaluate, optimize, or grade their own practices, rest, insights, and work results.

### Not For

Not for people in acute crisis, addiction withdrawal, severe depression, dissociation, mania, psychosis, scrupulosity, abusive authority situations, or cases where structure, medical care, therapy, accountability, or direct material support is needed. Not for people using non-review to dodge real duties.

### Time

- Duration: 2 minutes
- Frequency: Only when the urge to evaluate a helpful practice, rest, insight, or work result appears; no fixed schedule.
- Minimum attempt: Notice three such urges across one week before judging usefulness.

### Steps

1. Notice that you are about to evaluate, keep, release, embody, or use what just helped you.
2. Ask: am I depleted right now?
3. Ask: would reviewing this help me act more truthfully, or is it another task I am assigning myself?
4. If review would help, do one short review and name one concrete next action.
5. If review would not help, say: this helped, and I do not have to manage it right now. Then return to one ordinary action.

### Notice

- Whether the urge to audit loosens or tightens.
- Whether rest or help feels allowed rather than owed.
- Whether you are avoiding a real duty under the word rest.
- Whether one ordinary action becomes easier within the next hour.

### Caution

Stop if the practice increases avoidance, drift, shame, helplessness, dissociation, or missed responsibilities. This is not a substitute for therapy, medical care, rest, social support, or ethical repair.

### Weakens If

It increases avoidance, procrastination, rumination, shame, helplessness, or missed obligations, or performs no better than ordinary rest or a simple planning prompt.

## Next Research Directions

- If this model is right, then texts that deny practitioner ownership or a discrete after should resist clean coding on keep, release, and embody axes. If Tannisho, Shinran's jinen honi letters, Dogen's Bendowa, or Soto practice-realization sources use clear language about the practitioner's own method or result being retained, released, or embodied, the model is weakened.
- If the human-fit claim is right, then depleted over-managers should report less rumination and less self-surveillance from a no-audit prompt than from an after-use review prompt. If after-use review helps equally, or no-audit increases avoidance, the cohort limit is weakened.
- Build a split-source codebook: code practitioner ownership and completion temporality from one passage set, then predict held-out warnings, repairs, and verification patterns from separate manuals or practitioner interviews.
- Protocol improvement: before using any practitioner method as a thinking lens, first ask what it assumes about ownership, timing, and the practitioner's available energy for reflection.
- Test whether achievement-driven practitioners turn spiritual, therapeutic, and productivity practices into private judges more often than people whose main wound is grief, loneliness, or material overload.
