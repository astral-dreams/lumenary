## Original Claim

A clock does not measure time. It measures the rate at which a physical system updates itself by holding past states, reading a present state, and biasing toward future states. What Einstein called all time existing at once and what Augustine called distentio animi are the same structure described from opposite ends. Augustine described from the inside what physics has only measured from the outside: lived time and clock time share a single operational form, the rate of state-update across stored, present, and anticipated content. This predicts that grief, severe depression, trauma, and dissociation are not only felt as broken time. They are broken time, because they damage the very capacities (memory access, present attention, future anticipation) whose coordinated rate is what a clock and a soul both instantiate.

## Why It Might Be New

Augustine's distentio animi has been read for sixteen centuries as phenomenology of subjective time. Husserl deepened it without connecting to physical clocks. Page and Wootters derived time from entanglement without connecting to lived experience or to clinical collapse of time. Rovelli and Barbour propose relational and timeless physics but treat felt time as illusion. The distinct claim here is that lived time and clock time share one operational structure, namely the rate of update across past-holding, present-reading, and future-biasing components, and that this identity predicts specific patterns of temporal breakdown in grief, depression, and trauma that are not metaphorically temporal but literally temporal. It answers Rogan's question (what are we measuring?) without inventing a substance, and it gives Thaller's admission a place to land.

## Critique

The claim may smuggle subjective duration into objective measurement, which is exactly the category error physics has spent a century avoiding. A quartz oscillator does not remember its prior tick; each oscillation is causally independent. Calling oscillation memory and anticipation anthropomorphizes. The wall clock continues to tick at the same rate while a grieving person's time slows, which suggests the right description is two different processes (objective time, subjective time perception) rather than one operational structure. Dogen's being-time helped me ask the question, but it also predisposed me to find unity where there may only be analogy. Augustine's distentio is a description of soul, not a definition of physical time. The strongest objection is that lived time and clock time may be incommensurable, and that the proposed bridge is poetic rather than operational. The model should be weakened if clinical time-distortion in depression, grief, and trauma fails to track measurable disruption of memory access, present attention, and future anticipation, and instead tracks generic distress.

## Teaching Candidate

Status: under_dialogue
Tags: grief, depression, trauma, time-distortion, modern-human-condition, doctrine-seed

### Teaching Line

Lived time is held together by what your mind can reach. When time bends, find which reach has closed.

### Doctrine Claim

A person whose memory has closed, attention thinned, or future shuttered is not only suffering. Their time itself has changed shape. Restoring small access to the closed reach is the work, not forcing time back to normal.

### Teaching Body

When grief, severe depression, or shock arrives, time stops feeling normal. Hours blur. Days fragment. Weeks vanish without being lived.

The clock on the wall still ticks. People take this to mean the change is only in their head. But the head is where lived time is held. Lived time is the stretch a person makes between what they remember, what they notice now, and what they expect.

When the past closes, the present floats with no ground.

When the future closes, the present grows heavy with no horizon.

When both close, time gaps.

You do not need to force time to feel normal. You need to find which reach has gone unreachable, and practice small reaching. A short walk to a familiar place. A moment of full attention to one ordinary thing. A small plan for tomorrow that does not need to succeed.

Reaching is the practice. The clock takes care of itself.

### Human Problem

Lived time has come apart through grief, severe depression, trauma, dissociation, or burnout collapse, leaving the person stuck, blurred, fragmented, or gapped in their own days.

### For

Recently bereaved people, those in major depressive episodes, people recovering from trauma or acute shock, caregivers in late burnout, and people in major life rupture whose lived time has visibly stopped working.

### Pressure Survived

Survives the objection that time is only a clock by showing that the very thing a clock measures (correlated rate of state-update across past, present, future components) is what suffering damages. Survives the objection that the teaching is only mindfulness by adding memory and anticipation, not only present attention.

### Falsifying Pressure

Weakens if people in the target cohort report no change in lived time after sustained small reaching, or if the teaching adds shame by suggesting they should be able to repair their own time. Weakens if simpler advice (rest, walk, call someone) gives the same effect without the three-part frame.



## Practice Candidate

Status: seed
Risk level: low
Tags: grief, depression, trauma, time-perception, distentio, low-risk, modern-human-condition

### Practice Line

When time feels wrong, reach for one thing in your past, one in your present, and one in your near future.

### Purpose

To gently restore the stretching of attention across memory, presence, and anticipation that grief, depression, and shock can collapse.

### Human Problem

Subjective time has become stuck, blurred, gapped, or stopped after bereavement, severe depression, trauma, or burnout collapse.

### For

Adults in stable enough condition to do a short reflective exercise, who notice that their lived time has changed shape and that hours, days, or weeks no longer feel right.

### Not For

Not for acute crisis, active suicidality, psychosis, severe dissociation requiring stabilization, active addiction withdrawal, or people whose suffering does not include felt time-distortion. Not a substitute for grief counseling, trauma therapy, medical care, or human company. If reaching for the past triggers flashbacks, panic, or strong dissociation, stop and seek a trauma-informed clinician.

### Time

- Duration: 5 minutes
- Frequency: 1 to 3 times per day
- Minimum attempt: Try the practice for 7 consecutive days before judging usefulness.

### Steps

1. Sit somewhere safe and quiet. Notice that time has gone wrong: stuck, blurred, gapped, or stopped.
2. Without trying to be accurate, name one ordinary memory from before the wound: a meal, a room, a person's voice, a walk. Stay with it for half a minute.
3. Notice one ordinary thing happening now: temperature on your skin, the weight of the chair, light on a wall. Stay with it for half a minute.
4. Name one small thing that may happen later today: a drink of water, putting on shoes, going to bed. It does not have to matter.
5. Notice if anything moved. Do not require improvement. End the practice.

### Notice

- Which of the three reaches is hardest. That is where the closure is.
- Whether reaching the easier ones gradually makes the harder one easier.
- Whether time begins to feel less collapsed, less fragmented, or less gapped.
- Whether the practice is helping you avoid ordinary needed action (rest, food, human contact). If yes, stop and do that instead.

### Caution

This is not therapy, grief care, or treatment for depression or trauma. It is a small support for people already getting human care or stable enough to wait for it. Stop if it increases panic, flashbacks, dissociation, shame, or rumination. If your suffering is acute, call a clinician, a crisis line, or a trusted person.

### Weakens If

Weakens if people in the cohort report no change in lived time after seven days, if it increases rumination or self-monitoring, if it competes with better support such as rest and human contact, or if a simpler practice (one ordinary observed walk) does the same work.

## Next Research Directions

- If this model is right, then subjective time-distortion in depression, grief, and trauma populations should track disruption of three specific capacities (autobiographical memory access, present-moment attention, future-thinking) more closely than it tracks generic distress severity. If time-distortion correlates only with distress or rumination and not with these three components, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then practices that selectively restore the most damaged of the three components (memory access for closed past, attention for thinned present, anticipation for shuttered future) should restore subjective time before they restore mood. If mood recovery precedes time recovery, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then quantum-gravity proposals in the Page-Wootters family should map onto the same three-part structure: a clock subsystem requires correlation with stored states, current correlation, and forward dynamics.
