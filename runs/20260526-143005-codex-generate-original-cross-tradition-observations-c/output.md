# The Forbidden Claimant Rubric

## Original Claim

The real boundary between effort and gift is not how much the practitioner does. It is who is allowed to claim the doing. A path can require receiving, agency, memory, reflexivity, and integration while forbidding the ordinary self from owning one or more of those functions. This turns the capacity question into a support-locus rubric: for each function, ask where the support is held, in the practitioner, teacher, vow, name, text, community, present act, awareness itself, or later integration, and ask which claimant is explicitly prohibited. Right effort authorizes the practitioner to prevent, abandon, cultivate, and maintain states, while refusing ownership of experience as self. Other-power teaching may require hearing and settled trust, but it forbids the practicer to treat trust as a self-made achievement. Direct-introduction practice may require recognition and stabilization, but it forbids contrived production of what is introduced. Practice-realization may require total enactment, but it forbids practice from becoming a bridge to a later prize. The rubric therefore does not say gift paths secretly depend on self-power. It says every path must carry the work somewhere, and each path is partly defined by the place where it says: this function is necessary, but this owner must not claim it.

## Why It Might Be New

The closest prior argument is Engler's healthy-ego-before-no-self thesis, joined by Buddhist skillful-means literature, Advaita eligibility teaching, and sudden-gradual debates. These already show that practices need prepared people, capacity-sensitive teaching, and stage-aware safeguards. The different move here is the double code. Existing capacity accounts mostly ask what the practitioner must have; this model asks where each required function is held and who must not claim it. That distinction lets radical gift traditions be compared without translating their gift into a hidden psychological skill. It also makes anomalies more visible: a tradition may not reduce the amount of support, but change the authorized bearer of support from self to vow, teacher, present act, awareness, community, or post-insight discipline.

## Critique

The strongest counterexample is Chinul's sudden awakening and gradual cultivation. In Secrets on Cultivating the Mind, awakening is sudden, later cultivation is necessary, and true cultivation eliminates without treating anything as truly eliminated. The same action can be authorized or forbidden depending on whether it relies on awakening or on goal-seeking delusion. That may make the rubric too stage-dependent to remain clean. Radical Jodo Shinshu adds a sharper theological objection: if true entrusting is Amida's working, even calling the vow a support locus may still be an outside analyst's way of domesticating grace. The Shinran lens also biases the analysis toward ownership suspicion; right effort corrects this by showing that desire, effort, and maintenance can be medicines rather than theft. The model should be weakened if primary manuals do not show recurring wrong-claimant warnings, or if practitioners say ownership language is alien to their actual training.

## Next Research Directions

- Build a coding sheet with five functions: receiving, agency, memory, reflexivity, and integration. For each cell, code support locus, forbidden claimant, stage, safeguard, and failure mode.
- If this model is right, then practice manuals should warn not only against missing capacity but against the wrong owner claiming a necessary function. If warnings track only general ethics, institutional caution, or teacher personality, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then dual-trained practitioners should report a shift in permitted first-person grammar when switching methods: I cultivate, I entrust, I recognize, I just sit, or I do not claim. If they report only generic calm or concentration differences, the model is weakened.
- If this model is right, then adverse contemplative events should cluster around wrong-claimant failures: self-power anxiety in gift practice, achievement seeking in practice-realization, reification after direct introduction, and mechanical striving in effort practice. If adverse reports do not vary by claimant pattern after controlling for trauma, intensity, and supervision, the model is weakened.
- Cross-domain prediction: if identity-transforming therapies relocate support from client agency to therapist, group, protocol, medicine, or integration practice, then their safeguards should name who may claim the decisive change. If ordinary skills training has the same ownership safeguards, the analogy is weakened.
- Protocol improvement: before using any practitioner method as a reasoning lens, name what that method forbids the thinker to claim. Then pair it with a contrasting method that restores the capacity the first lens tends to distrust.
