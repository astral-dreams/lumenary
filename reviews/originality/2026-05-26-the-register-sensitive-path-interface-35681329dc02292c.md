## Exact Claim

A path should be compared in two ordered layers: first identify the tradition's own register for decisive transformation, then map only the vulnerabilities and safeguards the tradition itself names at the human boundary of that transformation, including a marker for cases where the mapping stance itself becomes an obstacle.

## Claim Units

- Comparison must first preserve the transformation register, such as human effort, Other Power, primordial awareness, practice-realization, divine restoration, or causal continuity.
- Comparison should then code only tradition-recognized vulnerabilities and safeguards around the transformation event or condition.
- Relevant vulnerabilities include hearing, doubt, calculation, misrecognition, false trust, teacher relation, ethical misuse, memory, discernment, community correction, and integration.
- The rubric needs a self-limitation marker for traditions that say analysis, conceptualization, or mapping can become part of the obstacle.
- The model rejects a universal human-capacity ledger and instead asks whose act transformation is said to be, what can obscure or counterfeit it, and where analysis must yield to practice.

Structural observation: The Tannisho strongly supports the register-first move. Its decisive register is Other Power, and it names boundary failures such as self-power, calculation, doubt, wrong teacher ownership, and confusing nembutsu with the practitioner's own good act. Bendowa is the anomaly case. It names wrong understanding, false teachers, verbal clinging, and inadequate practice, but its register is practice-realization, where training and enlightenment are not two. The proposed model reads Shinran well, but its interface language strains Dogen because an interface can imply the very separation between practitioner, practice, and realization that Bendowa rejects.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike Freiberger, Clooney, Hedges, Katz, Sharf, Lindbeck, Pargament, Shinran, Dogen, and MN 22, this idea claims that comparative work should use a specific two-layer rubric: preserve the transformation register first, then code only tradition-named vulnerabilities, safeguards, and warnings against the rubric itself. Its contribution is operational and predictive, not the discovery that traditions have different registers.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: Dogen's practice-realization and other no-boundary traditions where decisive transformation is not approached through a separable practitioner-event interface.

Why it strains the model: If practice and realization are one, then an interface between practitioner and transformation may be an imposed dualism. A self-limitation marker may not be enough because the problematic category is not only the map, it is the boundary implied by interface.

Confidence effect: This lowers originality and model confidence. The idea remains useful as a careful comparative heuristic, but it should be classed as extended unless it replaces interface with coding values such as no-boundary, collapsed-boundary, practice-realization, vow-mediated, teacher-mediated, and analyst-frame-refused.

## Falsifiable Predictions

- If: Other Power texts should show a distinctive warning profile after the register is identified. Then: Tannisho, Shinran letters, and Jodo Shinshu teaching materials should warn more against self-power, calculation, pride in practice, and self-assessment than against laxity as the primary danger. Weakens if: Other Power materials do not warn against calculation more than effort-centered manuals, or the warnings track genre, polemic, or institution better than transformation register. Test surface: Blind coding of Jodo Shinshu primary texts and manuals against Theravada, Yogic, and monastic effort manuals.
- If: Direct-introduction traditions should show a warning profile distinct from Other Power traditions. Then: Dzogchen and Mahamudra materials should warn especially against misrecognition, mistaking an experience for recognition, reifying awareness, inadequate teacher relation, and failure to integrate conduct. Weakens if: Their warnings are generic morality or effort warnings, or the same profile appears regardless of claimed transformation register. Test surface: Longchenpa, pointing-out manuals, Mahamudra manuals, Tergar or other teacher-facing materials, and practitioner interviews.
- If: Practice-realization traditions should resist interface grammar more than causal-cultivation or divine-gift traditions. Then: Dogen and Soto materials should correct before-after, means-end, and practitioner-event separations more often than texts framed by gradual effort, vow, grace, or recognition. Weakens if: Practice-realization texts freely use interface-like causal language without corrective pressure, or their corrections are no stronger than in unrelated traditions. Test surface: Bendowa, Genjokoan, selected Shobogenzo fascicles, Soto practice manuals, and teacher interviews.
- If: A register-sensitive rubric should predict warning profiles better than direct summary or agency-locus models. Then: Independent coders should infer later manual warnings from early register statements at above-chance rates, for example calculation in Shinran, misrecognition in Dzogchen, wrong effort in gradual paths, and conceptual reification in apophatic or raft-like traditions. Weakens if: Predictions are vague, post hoc, low agreement, or no better than Pargament-style agency categories, direct textual summary, or broad tradition labels. Test surface: Inter-rater manual-coding trial across at least six traditions, with prediction scoring before coders read later warning sections.

Structural translation: First identify the method's claimed change register, such as client skill acquisition, therapeutic relationship, unconscious integration, nervous-system regulation, medication effect, group accountability, surrender, or acceptance. Prediction: Different change registers should produce different warning profiles: CBT should warn about distorted appraisal and homework nonuse, psychoanalysis about resistance and enactment, somatic therapy about dysregulation and bypassing body signals, twelve-step work about self-will and isolation, ACT about experiential avoidance and fusion.
