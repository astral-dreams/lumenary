## Exact Claim

Predictive-processing accounts of contemplative self-negation do not neutrally reproduce the atman versus anatta dispute. By treating the self as an inferred or constructed model whose precision can be relaxed, they already favor the not-self side and cannot express Advaita self-luminous awareness without reducing it to a model. The apparent convergence is translation strain across both vocabulary and explanatory level.

## Claim Units

- Predictive processing is not neutral between atman and anatta.
- Self as relaxable generative posit aligns structurally with Buddhist not-self or no-self framings.
- The framework cannot represent Advaita svaprakasha as non-inferred self-disclosure without changing the claim into a model, prior, state, or latent cause.
- The Free Energy Principle often speaks at a subpersonal or deflationary as-if level, while the religious dispute concerns person-level warrant.
- The frontier should be retired as a formal reproduction claim and retained only as a one-sided scientific redescription plus translation-strain warning.
- Human-condition fit is narrow: it helps stable practitioners avoid letting neuroscience settle identity after quiet states, but it does not directly address addiction, grief, severe anxiety, or loneliness except through overinterpretation and meaning loss.

Structural observation: Shankara's side treats consciousness as the presupposed, self-disclosing condition of knowing, not as an object found inside cognition. SN 22.59 places consciousness among the aggregates to be regarded as not-self, and Bahiya removes any authorized claimant in the seen, heard, sensed, or cognized. Laukkonen and Slagter's predictive account, https://researchportal.scu.edu.au/esploro/outputs/journalArticle/From-many-to-none-Meditation-and/991013062213702368, directly models the Buddhist operation by describing meditation as deconstructing predictions, including the predicted self. It can model Advaita only by translating the witness into a prior, latent state, fixed point, or reportable pure-awareness condition, which is not Shankara's non-objectifiable self-luminosity.

Secondary literature gap: Before raising novelty, search the exact target in Metzinger self-model theory, Letheby and Gerrans on predictive self-binding, Seth on self as controlled hallucination, Sebastian and Limanowski on predictive selfhood, Mann's Free Energy user guide, Bruineberg and Rietveld, Hohwy, Thompson, Albahari, Fasching, Ram-Prasad, and recent mindfulness no-self mechanism critiques such as https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11097-025-10081-8.

Risk of overread: High. Laukkonen and Slagter do not claim to adjudicate Advaita versus Buddhism, and FEP theorists can say the model is only a heuristic or mathematical description. The phrase took a side may confuse a modeling assumption with a metaphysical conclusion. It is safer to say the vocabulary has a Buddhist-compatible affordance and an Advaita distortion risk.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike Laukkonen and Slagter, Metzinger-style self-model theory, and general FEP critiques, this idea specifically says that a predictive-processing model of self-negation cannot be a neutral higher-order mirror of the atman and anatta dispute because its self-as-model vocabulary already rules out Shankara's non-inferred witness unless extra metaphysical assumptions are added. That is an extension, not a discovery.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: A formal active-inference account that represents non-inferred self-luminous awareness without reducing it to a hidden state, prior, precision parameter, global workspace, or reportable phenomenological content.

Why it strains the model: If such a model works and is accepted by both active-inference theorists and Advaita scholars, the claim that predictive processing structurally cannot express svaprakasha becomes too strong. A second anomaly would be Advaita practitioners finding the predictive-processing explanation more faithful to witness recognition than Buddhist practitioners do.

Confidence effect: Lowers confidence from strong to moderate. The audit supports vocabulary bias and level mismatch, but not the stronger claim that no possible non-reductive formalization can exist.
