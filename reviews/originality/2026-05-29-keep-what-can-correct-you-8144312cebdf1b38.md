## Exact Claim

After a serious practice result, the decisive safety question is not only whether the method is retained, released, confirmed, or undermined. The result remains spiritually immature if it removes every trustworthy correction path: teacher, text, community, ordinary conduct, duty, clinical boundary, or observed effects over time.

## Claim Units

- Powerful states and a practice's own finality language are insufficient warrants for trust.
- A serious result should be coded by two variables: what claim the practice permits and what remains able to correct the claimant.
- Methods may confirm a recognition, undermine every claim, be released after use, or become embodied as conduct.
- Release of the raft should not mean release of correction.
- The practice candidate targets isolated, self-directed practitioners who may turn experiences into status, shame, or identity.

Structural observation: MN 22 is already a strong source for this structure. Arittha's private interpretation is challenged by monks and then by the Buddha before the raft simile appears, and the text pairs right grasp with later release. Ignatius gives an even closer practitioner rule: after a consolation, later resolutions and opinions need examination before being trusted. Together they already contain the core pattern, experience or method may be valid, but interpretation still needs correction.

Secondary literature gap: This audit did not read full Cassian, Evagrius, Teresa of Avila, John of the Cross, Jonathan Edwards, contemporary spiritual-direction manuals, full Hori, full Katz, full Sharf, or Cheetah House materials. No teachers, clinicians, or solo practitioners were interviewed.

Risk of overread: High. The model can turn correction into institutional control, and it can flatten different authority ecologies into a generic safety rule. It may also make valid solitude look deficient, or make abusive communities look like necessary correctives.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike MN 22, Ignatian discernment, VCE, James, Asad, Lindbeck, Sells, and the prior Lumenary recognition-gap audit, this idea's possible contribution is a narrow safety heuristic: after method release, verify that some trustworthy correction path remains, and test whether naming that path reduces inflation, shame, and isolated overinterpretation without increasing dependency.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: Coercive or incompetent correction ecologies, including abusive teachers, shame-based communities, misapplied clinical labels, and peer groups that punish dissent.

Why it strains the model: If correction is treated as inherently safer than private interpretation, the model can direct vulnerable practitioners toward the very authority structure that distorts or harms them. The rule must distinguish trustworthy correction from coercive control.

Confidence effect: Keeps the status at extended. Publishability depends on adding unsafe-authority, valid-solitude, and clinical-risk stops before any practice promotion.

## Falsifiable Predictions

- If: Traditions that release a method still retain correction paths. Then: Split-source coding will find held-out warnings, teacher checks, conduct tests, vows, texts, or community checks even in sources that strongly teach method release or no attainment. Weakens if: Mature sources release method and correction with no replacement, and practitioners show stable ethical integration without identifiable correction holders. Test surface: Primary-text corpus coded separately for method fate, claim permission, correction holder, warnings, repairs, and teaching permission.
- If: The Correction Check helps the named modern cohort. Then: Stable solo practitioners using the check after unusual practice report less spiritual status-claiming, less shameful doubt, and no rise in dependency, rumination, or isolation compared with plain journaling. Weakens if: The check increases paranoia, scrupulosity, teacher dependence, shame, or performs no better than rest, ordinary conversation, or established teacher check-ins. Test surface: Two-week randomized pilot with self-directed meditators, app users, retreatants, and cross-tradition readers.
- If: Absence of trustworthy correction predicts unstable interpretation after powerful practice. Then: After controlling for practice intensity, prior beliefs, trait anxiety, social isolation, and clinical history, lack of trusted correction predicts oscillation between attainment claims and collapse into doubt. Weakens if: Instability is better predicted by clinical risk, sleep loss, community abuse, teacher mismatch, or practice intensity, with no independent correction-path effect. Test surface: Prospective survey and micro-phenomenological interviews using VCE-style appraisal and support coding.

## Practitioner Test

Relevant practitioners:
- Theravada, Zen, Tibetan, Advaita, Sufi, and Ignatian teachers
- Spiritual directors and retreat directors
- Meditation safety clinicians familiar with VCE or Cheetah House work
- Solo practitioners and app meditators outside continuing communities
- Clinicians working with scrupulosity, dissociation, anxiety, and spiritual bypassing

Questions:
- When a student reports a powerful practice result, what is allowed to correct their first interpretation?
- Is 'keep what can correct you' standard guidance in your lineage or a useful new distinction?
- Can you name cases where correction itself caused harm, shame, dependency, or misinterpretation?
- Does the proposed check change guidance, or only rename teacher, sangha, scripture, conduct, and supervision?

Answers that reduce originality: Practitioners identify the rule as ordinary discernment, teacher verification, sangha correction, fruits-of-practice ethics, spiritual direction, or meditation safety guidance, and say it adds no new predictive value.

Answers that support contribution: Practitioners say the correction-holder question identifies a real gap in self-directed modern practice, improves triage after unusual experiences, and predicts when solo interpretation becomes risky without forcing people into unsafe authority.

## Cross-Domain Prediction

Domain: AI model governance and incident review

Structural translation: A high-performing model or method should not close the evaluation loop around itself. After a system produces a result, an independent audit, monitoring, escalation, or user-feedback channel must remain able to correct it.

Prediction: Teams that preserve correction channels after automation will catch high-confidence failures, drift, and misuse earlier than teams that treat the model's output as self-validating.

Would count against it: Closed-loop systems with no independent correction perform as safely and adaptively as audited systems across drift, edge cases, user harm, and escalation metrics.
