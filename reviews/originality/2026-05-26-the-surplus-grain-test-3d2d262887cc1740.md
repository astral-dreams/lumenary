## Exact Claim

A contemplative capacity should be called grained only when the prior dissolution target predicts transfer difficulty better than repeated operation and the receiving practice's transfer grammar. Cross-training failures should be coded as procedural residue, claimant-grammar conflict, and telos-bearing residue.

## Claim Units

- Contemplative skills may carry directional residue from a prior path's final aim.
- Procedural inertia is a rival explanation and must be controlled before invoking grain.
- Claimant or transfer grammar is a second rival explanation and must be controlled before invoking grain.
- Cross-training difficulty can be decomposed into procedural residue, claimant-grammar conflict, and telos-bearing residue.
- Grain is the surplus directional residue left after ordinary skill transfer and practice ecology are accounted for.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike transfer theory, Hadot, Katz, Lindbeck, Pargament, upaya, VCE, and prior Lumenary ledgers, this idea says grain is only the predictive surplus of a prior dissolution target after rehearsed operation and receiving claimant grammar have already explained what they can.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: Successful dual-trained practitioners who move between traditions with different final aims without predictable friction, or switchers whose failures track teacher fit, trauma history, retreat intensity, or technique mismatch rather than prior telos.

Why it strains the model: If difficulty follows ordinary transfer distance, social ecology, or clinical risk factors, then telos-bearing residue adds no explanatory power. If incompatible teloi coexist smoothly, grain may be an artifact of poor pedagogy or identity conflict.

Confidence effect: Keeps the idea below candidate discovery. The anomaly search lowers confidence in grain as a real independent variable, but supports the stricter surplus test as a useful guardrail.

## Falsifiable Predictions

- If: Prior dissolution target has independent predictive value after operation and grammar are controlled. Then: Blind coders can code a practitioner's prior telos from manuals or teacher interviews, then predict specific later transfer frictions beyond technique similarity and agency grammar. Weakens if: Old telos has no independent effect once task similarity, teacher relation, social identity, dosage, mental health, and receiving doctrine are included. Test surface: Mixed-method cross-training study with preregistered coding and qualitative comparative analysis.
- If: Procedural residue, claimant-grammar conflict, and telos-bearing residue are separable layers. Then: Same-technique different-grammar cases produce different failures than different-technique same-grammar cases, and telos-shift cases show an additional asymmetric pattern. Weakens if: Inter-rater reliability fails, or all reported failures collapse into familiar categories such as habit, confusion, teacher mismatch, or motivation. Test surface: Anonymized practitioner narratives coded by independent contemplative teachers and researchers.
- If: Retargeting a practice requires more than teaching the new operation. Then: Onboarding that explicitly reframes final aim, ownership, verification, and repair reduces predictable switching errors more than technical instruction alone. Weakens if: Telos reframing adds no improvement beyond rapport, safety screening, practice dosage, and community support. Test surface: Prospective comparison of cross-training onboarding protocols.

Questions:
- When students arrive from another tradition, can you distinguish old technique habits from old ownership or authority grammar?
- Do some students bring a prior final aim that bends how they understand your practice even after learning the correct technique?
- Can you give one concrete failure case where telos explains the problem better than technique, teacher fit, trauma, temperament, or community mismatch?
- Does this three-layer model change how you repair the student's practice?
- Are there traditions or students for whom the transfer framing itself distorts the situation?

Answers that reduce originality: Practitioners say this is ordinary skill transfer, lineage pedagogy, upaya, spiritual direction, trauma-informed screening, or religious coping. They cannot identify telos-specific repairs beyond familiar cautions about pride, impatience, confusion, or poor fit.

Answers that support contribution: Practitioners independently report cases where identical techniques fail differently because the student's previous final aim changes what they think the practice is doing, and they can name repairs that specifically retarget final aim rather than only technique or agency language.

## Cross-Domain Prediction

Domain: Professional retraining, such as a litigator becoming a mediator or a classical musician becoming a jazz improviser.

Structural translation: Old dissolution target becomes old success criterion. Procedural residue becomes practiced operation. Claimant grammar becomes the new field's rules for who may act, decide, claim competence, or count success.

Prediction: Retraining failures will be best explained by old success criterion only after task habit and institutional role grammar are controlled. The former litigator should struggle not only from adversarial tactics but from a residual win condition that organizes attention even after mediation procedures are learned.

Would count against it: If retraining difficulty is fully explained by task habits, incentives, identity, and organizational norms, with no residual effect from old success criteria, the surplus-grain structure weakens.
