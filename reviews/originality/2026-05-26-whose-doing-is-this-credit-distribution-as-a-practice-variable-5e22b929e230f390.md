## Exact Claim

Credit distribution is a separable practice variable. Traditions distribute the power behind transformation across practitioner-centered, source-centered, split-credit, activity-centered, and ground-centered patterns, and those patterns shape the felt quality of effort, failure meanings, safeguards, and practice dangers.

## Claim Units

- The question whose power drives transformation is a practice instruction, not only a doctrine.
- Self-power versus other-power is too narrow, because traditions distribute credit across multiple agents and stages.
- Five patterns can be distinguished: practitioner-centered, source-centered, split-credit, activity-centered, and ground-centered.
- SN 45.8 and Bhagavad Gita 3.27 show similar outward diligence with different agency orientation.
- Each credit type should predict a characteristic failure mode and matching safeguard.
- Credit distribution is separable from the capacity ledger, because two paths can require similar functions but credit their source differently.

## Primary Text Close Read

Sources:
- SN 45.8 Magga-vibhanga Sutta, https://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/sn/sn45/sn45.008.than.html
- Bhagavad Gita 3.27 and 18.14, https://www.bhagavad-gita.us/bhagavad-gita-3-27/ and https://www.bhagavad-gita.us/bhagavad-gita-18-14/

Structural observation: The SN 45.8 comparison is strong but slightly overstated. SN 45.8 makes effort operationally direct: the practitioner generates desire, endeavors, activates persistence, upholds, and exerts intent. Bhagavad Gita 3.27 denies egoic sole-doership by assigning actions to prakriti's gunas, but Bhagavad Gita 18.14 complicates this by listing five causes of action, including body, doer, senses, efforts, and divine factor. The better close-read is not that the Gita simply disowns effort, but that it embeds effort in a multi-causal anti-ego framework. This supports credit distribution, but weakens the clean split-credit classification.

Secondary literature gap: The strongest prior art is Pargament's self-directing, deferring, and collaborative religious coping; Christian monergism and synergism; Japanese Buddhist jiriki and tariki; Sri Vaishnava markata and marjara; Sufi maqam and hal; and religious-attribution or God-control psychology. The searched literature did not show the exact five-type typology with activity-centered and ground-centered categories plus failure-mode predictions, but the main agency-allocation variable is well established.

Risk of overread: High. The five types may classify traditions at too coarse a level. Many traditions shift credit by stage, genre, audience, and commentator. The Gita is internally multi-causal; Sufism distinguishes stations and states; Advaita distinguishes preparation from recognition; Buddhism authorizes effort conventionally while undermining self-ownership analytically.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike Pargament, monergism and synergism, self-power and other-power, and markata and marjara debates, this idea adds activity-centered and ground-centered patterns, treats credit attribution as a felt practice variable, and predicts matching failure modes and safeguards across multiple traditions.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: The Bhagavad Gita itself is the strongest anomaly to the clean typology.

Why it strains the model: The model uses BG 3.27 to classify the Gita as disowning doership, but BG 18.14 gives a five-cause account that includes doer, effort, instruments, body, and divine factor. BG 2.47 also commands action while refusing claim on fruits. This means the same text can be practitioner-active, anti-ego, split-credit, and divine-inclusive depending on verse and commentary.

Confidence effect: Lowers confidence in tradition-level categories. The model should become a stage, verse, and commentary-level coding scheme before public use.

## Falsifiable Predictions

- If: Manuals grouped by credit type should show matching safeguard profiles. Then: Practitioner-centered texts emphasize warnings against pride and attainment ownership; source-centered texts emphasize warnings against false surrender, passivity, or antinomianism; split-credit texts warn against outcome attachment; activity-centered texts warn against gaining idea and formalism; ground-centered texts warn against premature realization claims. Weakens if: Each tradition warns equally against all failure modes, or safeguard profiles track institution, teacher temperament, ethics, or historical polemic better than credit type. Test surface: Blind coding of at least three manuals or teaching corpora per proposed type.
- If: Dual-trained practitioners should experience different agency orientations when switching practices. Then: A practitioner trained in Theravada right effort and Jodo Shinshu entrusting, or Advaita inquiry and Soto just-sitting, can describe different felt meanings of effort, failure, and credit without being prompted with the typology. Weakens if: Practitioners report no felt agency shift, or the shift is fully explained by vocabulary, teacher style, devotional temperament, or institutional identity. Test surface: Micro-phenomenological interviews with dual-trained practitioners and teachers.
- If: Credit distribution should be separable from the capacity ledger. Then: Two traditions can require similar receiving, agency, memory, reflexivity, and integration functions while crediting the power behind those functions differently, and this difference predicts different safeguards. Weakens if: Credit distribution always collapses into support locus, or support-locus coding predicts safeguards and failures without a separate credit variable. Test surface: Paired coding of Jodo Shinshu and Christian grace traditions, Soto Zen and Dzogchen, Theravada and Gita karma-yoga, with independent raters.
- If: Stage transitions should be high-risk points when credit distribution changes. Then: Manuals and teachers mark special danger when a practitioner moves from effortful preparation to recognition, surrender, no-gaining practice, or already-accomplished realization. Weakens if: Transitions do not show distinctive warnings, or reported difficulties are better predicted by retreat intensity, trauma, doctrine learning, or social pressure. Test surface: Stage-coded readings of Theravada, Sufi, Advaita, Dzogchen, Soto, and Pure Land training sequences.

## Cross-Domain Prediction

Domain: Organizational change and clinical recovery programs

Structural translation: Change programs distribute credit among individual effort, facilitator, group, protocol, medication, higher power, emergent process, or already-present capacity.

Prediction: Programs with similar tasks but different credit stories should show different failure patterns: burnout from self-credit, passivity from source-credit, performative compliance from activity-credit, and premature completion claims from ground-credit.

Would count against it: Outcomes and failures are explained by resources, relationship quality, severity, dosage, incentives, and social support, with no independent effect from the credit story.
