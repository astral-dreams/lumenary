## Exact Claim

Credit distribution should not function as a master taxonomy. First code the native claimant grammar, meaning what may count as agent, action, transformation, and causal attribution. Then score translation strain between traditions. Only after that should stage-level credit distribution be tested for added prediction of safeguards beyond claimant grammar, institution, teacher authority, initiation structure, and existing agency-allocation models. Low-strain cases may support taxonomy; high-strain cases should count as boundary cases or analogies, not confirming evidence.

## Claim Units

- Credit doctrine is a dependent diagnostic variable, not a master taxonomy.
- Native claimant grammar must be coded before credit distribution.
- Translation strain must be scored before using a cross-tradition comparison as evidence.
- Credit distribution should be stage-level, not only tradition-level.
- Credit distribution must add predictive power beyond claimant grammar, institution, teacher authority, initiation, and existing religious agency models.
- Low-strain comparisons may support taxonomy, while high-strain comparisons should be treated as boundary cases or analogical extensions.

Structural observation: The Gita is a strong test case for why credit needs claimant grammar first. It preserves obligation to act, denies simple ego-doership, separates action from fruits, and lists multiple causes of action, including bodily basis, instruments, effort, and divine factor. A simple self versus God credit scale misses that action, fruit, ego, duty, and causation are differently distributed. Tannisho creates a sharper strain case. It relocates efficacy to Amida's Vow, rejects self-powered merit, denies Shinran ownership over disciples, and describes nembutsu as neither ordinary practice nor good act because it arises through Other Power. Coding both texts with the same deferring or collaborative category would flatten native rules about what kind of action is even claimable. The close read supports the candidate's sequence, but it also shows that the primary traditions already force much of the insight.

Secondary literature gap: The audit used accessible translations and open secondary sources but did not read Sanskrit, Japanese, Tamil, Arabic, Pali, or Tibetan originals. It did not fully reread Lindbeck, Katz, Clooney, J. Z. Smith, Sandmel, Rambo, or Pargament's full article corpus. It also lacks practitioner interviews and statistical comparison against existing models.

Risk of overread: High. The protocol could become circular if the same passage codes claimant grammar, credit distribution, and predicted safeguards. It can also impose an analyst vocabulary of claimant, credit, and strain on traditions that reject agency accounting, especially Dogen practice-realization, Madhyamaka non-positioning, apophatic theology, Dzogchen direct recognition, and inherited devotional practice.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike Pargament's agency styles, attribution theory, Lindbeck's grammar, Asad's discursive tradition, Freiberger's comparative method, and prior Lumenary credit and strain ledgers, this idea's distinct claim is a narrow testing order: code native claimant grammar and translation strain first, then ask whether stage-level credit distribution adds independent prediction of safeguards, warnings, repairs, or transition vulnerabilities. Without that added predictive gain, the idea is mostly a renamed consolidation of existing comparison and agency-allocation methods.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: Native credit-first traditions, especially Shinran's self-power and Other Power, Sri Vaishnava effort and surrender debates, and Sufi maqam and hal distinctions.

Why it strains the model: The candidate says credit is dependent, but these traditions may make credit grammar itself a native master distinction that organizes entry, practice, failure, and verification. If credit-first coding predicts safeguards better than claimant grammar, institution, or translation strain, the candidate's demotion of credit is too strong.

Confidence effect: This lowers confidence from strong originality to extended synthesis. The model survives only if it treats native credit-first cases as admissible low-strain cases rather than forcing credit to be secondary everywhere.

## Falsifiable Predictions

- If: Split-source coding of claimant grammar and translation strain should improve prediction before credit distribution is added. Then: In blind coding, credit distribution adds clear predictive value mainly in low-strain cases where credit categories are native or near-native, such as self-power and Other Power, grace and effort, or maqam and hal. Weakens if: A generic self, divine, collaborative credit scale predicts safeguards, warnings, and repairs equally well across low-strain and high-strain cases. Test surface: Manual coding across Tannisho, Bhagavad Gita, Srivaishnava prapatti sources, Sufi manuals, Dogen practice-realization texts, and Dzogchen pointing-out materials.
- If: High-strain comparisons should show lower inter-rater reliability and weaker prediction when credit is coded before claimant grammar. Then: Coders disagree more often on Dogen, Dzogchen, Madhyamaka, apophatic, and inherited-practice cases unless analyst-frame refusal or no-credit-frame values are allowed. Weakens if: Coders reliably assign stage-level credit categories in high-strain cases and those categories predict held-out safeguards without claimant grammar controls. Test surface: Inter-rater trial with held-out warning and repair passages.
- If: Transitions between credit grammars should be heightened vulnerability points. Then: Practitioner manuals and interviews identify failures such as pride in surrender, quietism after grace, self-powered misuse of Other Power language, antinomian readings of non-doership, or passivity after direct recognition. Weakens if: Reported failure cases track intensity, teacher style, institution, trauma history, or general misunderstanding without relation to credit transition. Test surface: Practitioner interviews and adverse-event case coding.
- If: Low-strain matched cases should show credit-specific warning profiles after controlling for institution and teacher authority. Then: Matched cases such as Jodo Shu and Jodo Shinshu, Vedantic action and non-doership teachings, or Srivaishnava surrender schools show different repair instructions tied to native credit grammar. Weakens if: The differences disappear when socialization, institution, teacher authority, or initiation structure are controlled. Test surface: Matched corpus comparison plus practitioner interviews.

## Cross-Domain Prediction

Domain: Psychotherapy and behavior change modalities

Structural translation: Before crediting change to client effort, therapist skill, medication, group support, unconscious process, or surrender, code what each modality permits to count as agent, action, transformation, and cause. Then score translation strain between modalities before comparing credit allocation.

Prediction: High-strain comparisons, such as CBT skill practice, psychoanalytic insight, 12-step surrender, and biomedical medication, will produce misleading responsibility taxonomies unless modality grammar is coded first. Stage-level credit should add predictive value mainly where the modality already admits that credit language.

Would count against it: General locus-of-control or self-efficacy measures predict adherence, relapse, and repair across modalities without needing modality grammar or translation-strain controls.
