## Exact Claim

A contemplative practice does not name what happens through it. Its result is named by trusted recognition partners, traditions, teachers, communities, or other accepted authorities. Modern self-directed practitioners often inherit methods without the partners who once interpreted those methods, so after a meaningful shift they should ask whom they would actually trust to tell them what happened, and if no one can be named, carry that absence as part of the experience.

## Claim Units

- A method can produce or occasion an experience but cannot by itself authorize the meaning of that experience.
- The same instruction changes meaning when the recognizing community changes.
- Recognition is performed by trusted persons, lineages, texts, institutions, or communities, not by the method as an isolated object.
- Modern self-directed practitioners face a recognition gap when they borrow practices without continuing interpretive partners.
- The human-condition target is isolation, meaning loss, and achievement-contingent self-worth among solo seekers, app meditators, cross-tradition readers, and retreatants without continuing guidance.
- Non-fit cases include acute psychiatric destabilization, scrupulosity, grief needing ordinary company, abusive or false recognition communities, and practitioners already inside stable teacher relationships.

Structural observation: MN 22 does not leave Arittha's interpretation to private experience. The community reports the misreading, the Buddha cross-examines it, and the raft simile then teaches right use and release of the Dhamma. The method is not self-naming because wrong grasp is corrected by teacher and sangha. Bendowa strains the model. Dogen collapses practice and realization so strongly that there is no simple later event to label, yet the text still frames zazen through transmitted Buddha-dharma, trust, and instruction. The comparison supports the recognition claim, but it also shows the claim is already native to many practice ecologies rather than newly discovered.

Secondary literature gap: The search found strong structural neighbors in Talal Asad's discursive tradition, George Lindbeck's cultural-linguistic grammar, Robert Sharf's critique of Buddhist modernism, Ignatian discernment, and the VCE meditation-challenges literature. The audit did not close-read Japanese, Pali, Sanskrit, or Catholic primary-language sources. It also lacks interviews with solo practitioners and lineage teachers.

Risk of overread: High. The idea may overcorrect from method grammar to community authorization. Some methods do constrain interpretation internally, and some practitioners deliberately leave communities because the available recognition is false, coercive, or unsafe. The model should not make loneliness look like doctrinal failure, and it should not imply that any community recognition is better than no recognition.

## Unlike Statement

Unlike Asad, Lindbeck, Sharf, Ignatian discernment, and VCE, this idea's distinct contribution is not that communities shape religious meaning. Its possible contribution is a narrow practitioner diagnostic: after a contemplative shift, ask who could correct the interpretation, and treat the inability to name such a person as data about the experience rather than as proof or failure.

## Anomaly Probe

Candidate: Self-luminous recognition traditions and deliberate solitary practice, such as Advaita svaprakasha language, some hermit lineages, Quaker inner-light language, and practitioners who leave abusive communities because communal recognition is less trustworthy than disciplined solitude.

Why it strains the model: These cases suggest that some traditions authorize recognition through intrinsic self-evidence, conduct over time, scripture, or inward confirmation rather than a named recognition partner. They also show that community can misname experience and that the absence of a trusted corrector may be protective rather than pathological.

Confidence effect: Lowers confidence from candidate discovery to extended. The model remains useful only if it distinguishes trustworthy recognition, unsafe recognition, valid solitude, and pathological isolation.

## Falsifiable Predictions

- If: Recognition ecology materially changes interpretation after similar practice experiences. Then: Dual-trained practitioners will report different completion judgments, warnings, and integration duties for similar phenomenology depending on which teacher, lineage, text, or community they treat as authoritative. Weakens if: Practitioners report stable interpretation and integration regardless of recognition community, after controlling for practice type, intensity, prior beliefs, and mental-health history. Test surface: Micro-phenomenological interviews with dual-trained practitioners and split-source coding of teacher responses.
- If: Modern self-directed practice produces distinctive recognition-gap patterns. Then: Solo practitioners without trusted correction will show higher rates of interpretation oscillation, premature identity claims, or collapse into doubt than practitioners with stable, non-coercive guidance. Weakens if: These patterns occur at similar rates in well-supervised settings, or are better predicted by trait anxiety, social isolation, or adverse meditation effects than by recognition ecology. Test surface: Prospective survey of app meditators, retreatants, and lineage practitioners using VCE-style appraisal and support measures.
- If: The Recognition Gap exercise helps its target cohort by loosening interpretation without negating experience. Then: After three uses, stable solo practitioners report less status-claiming and less self-suspicion, while preserving clear memory of the experience and ordinary functioning. Weakens if: The exercise increases shame, obsessive doubt, withdrawal, or dependence on imagined authorities, or performs no better than a generic journaling prompt. Test surface: Two-week low-risk pilot with pre-post measures of rumination, isolation, inflation, and grounded action.

## Practitioner Test

Relevant practitioners:
- Theravada, Zen, Tibetan, Advaita, Sufi, Ignatian, and Quaker teachers
- Spiritual directors and retreat directors
- Meditation safety researchers familiar with VCE and Cheetah House work
- Solo practitioners and app meditators outside continuing communities

Questions:
- When a student reports a meaningful shift, who or what is authorized to name it in your setting?
- Is the statement that a practice cannot name itself obvious, false, or practically useful from within your tradition?
- Have you seen concrete harms from students lacking recognition partners, and are those harms different from ordinary anxiety, pride, or confusion?

Answers that reduce originality: Teachers say this is standard spiritual direction, lineage confirmation, satsang, sangha, confession, or discernment vocabulary, and the proposed prompt adds no new sorting power.

Answers that support contribution: Practitioners say the prompt identifies a real modern gap they had not named, especially in self-directed practice, and it changes how they handle post-experience interpretation without increasing dependence or shame.

## Cross-Domain Prediction

Domain: Clinical psychotherapy and coaching supervision

Structural translation: A technique does not certify the meaning of the change it produces. A client or coach needs a supervision or interpretive ecology to distinguish insight, avoidance, transference, skill acquisition, and identity claim.

Prediction: Self-guided users of powerful therapeutic or coaching techniques will show more unstable self-labeling and overclaiming when they lack trusted review, even when the technique itself is evidence-based.

Would count against it: Outcomes and interpretation stability are fully explained by technique quality, symptom severity, and user motivation, with no independent effect from recognition or supervision ecology.
