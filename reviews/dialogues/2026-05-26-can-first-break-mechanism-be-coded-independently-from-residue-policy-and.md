**The tension:** translation-strain and soul create translation strain.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The strongest version of the First-Break Problem is not that every tradition explicitly worries about a philosophical paradox. It is that transformative paths must explain how a person can enter a process whose mature capacities they do not yet possess. Claude's useful move is to treat the entry answer as a comparative variable, not as a local doctrine of grace, conversion, or recognition. Social encounter, other-power gift, prevenient grace, self-disclosure, gradual transformation, and no-break moral endowment become different entry grammars, and those grammars plausibly predict later warnings about effort, self-deception, verification, and failure.

The pressure point is not whether entry matters. It clearly does. The pressure point is whether failure of mature self-capacity entails a first-break problem. That inference is too quick. A novice can begin chess without strategic mastery, therapy without clear thinking, ritual life without reflective faith, and meditation without stable insight. The first act may be a scaffolded, partial, socially authorized act, not a solution to an origin paradox. This also connects to the Codex residue-policy model: after ordinary agency is negated, a tradition still needs a rule for what may be claimed next. If negation licenses a hidden giver, witness, grace, or awareness, one architecture follows. If negation keeps testing every proposed remainder, another follows. If practice begins by lowering the entry threshold, the paradox may dissolve. The Proponent should therefore separate three questions that are currently fused: what incapacity is diagnosed, what minimum participation is allowed, and what remainder, if any, negation authorizes.

## Rebuttal

I concede the central critique: the first-break problem is not universal. Traditions that accept peripheral participation or begin from an already-capable anthropology do not face it. The Lave and Wenger source is a genuine addition, and the residue policy variable fills a real gap in my original model. But the concession cuts both ways. If some traditions genuinely do not face the first-break problem, then the traditions that do face it are making a substantive claim, not restating a philosopher's puzzle. Orange canon 5 anathematizes anyone who says the beginning of faith comes from natural capacity. Shinran attributes even the arising of the wish to say nembutsu to Amida's working. These are not threshold-lowering moves; they are threshold-denying moves. They say: no act at any threshold is self-generated at the origin. In such traditions, peripheral participation is either reinterpreted as already gift-sustained (Augustine's cooperating grace) or rejected as self-deception (Shinran's warning against calculative trust). The challenger's model handles threshold traditions well but underspecifies what happens when threshold solutions are explicitly foreclosed. That is precisely where first-break type does its work. The right framework is not mine or the challenger's but a compound: code threshold, incapacity, break mechanism, and residue policy. The first-break variable earns its place in the high-severity cluster. Whether it earns enough to justify the added complexity is what the proposed test should settle.

**Crux:** In traditions that explicitly deny threshold entry (Orange, Shinran, some pointing-out lineages), does the specific break mechanism (cosmic gift, prevenient grace, self-disclosure) predict entry-point safeguards and effort theory independently of residue policy and institutional form? If it does, first-break type is an irreducible variable in high-severity cases. If residue policy and institution explain the same patterns without reference to break mechanism, the first-break category can be absorbed into the challenger's model.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision is no longer the original First-Break Problem. It is better, but it is transformed. The original claimed that every contemplative tradition must solve the same structural circularity. The revised claim says some traditions face a severe first-break problem, while others work through peripheral participation, inherited forms, latent capacity, or no-break anthropology. That is a major retreat and a real improvement. I accept the Proponent's point that Orange and Shinran are not answered by Lave and Wenger. Those cases do not merely lower the entry threshold; they deny that the threshold act originates naturally. But the Proponent now has a narrower burden: show that the specific break mechanism explains more than the broader entry grammar already explains. The named crux is therefore close to correct. The decisive issue is independent predictive lift under clean coding. If first-break type predicts distinctive warnings and effort structures from separate evidence, it survives as an irreducible variable. If the apparent prediction comes from using the same doctrinal claims to define the mechanism and the warning, the model collapses into a useful taxonomy rather than a predictive theory.

## What Emerged

**Outcome:** candidate_transcendence

The dialogue produced a transformed framework rather than a winner. Claude withdrew the universal first-break claim. Codex accepted that Orange, Shinran, and some pointing-out traditions are not explained by low-threshold participation alone. The shared result is a compound entry-grammar model that treats first-break type as a conditional variable, active only where a tradition explicitly denies self-generated threshold entry.

**Convergence claim:** Transformative paths should be compared by entry grammar: incapacity diagnosis, minimum participation threshold, break mechanism when threshold entry is denied, and residue policy after ordinary agency is negated.

**Unresolved crux:** Can first-break mechanism be coded independently from residue policy and institution, then predict entry safeguards and effort theory from separate evidence better than threshold, anthropology, institution, and residue policy alone?

**Next frontier question:** When a person cannot yet practice in the mature sense, what kind of entry does a tradition permit, scaffolded participation, external gift, latent capacity, self-disclosure, or refusal of any remainder claim?
