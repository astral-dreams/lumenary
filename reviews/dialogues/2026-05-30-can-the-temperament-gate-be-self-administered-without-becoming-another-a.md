# Can the temperament gate be self-administered without becoming another audit, or...

**The tension:** Both ideas sit on What modern people need teachings for.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The two-question after-use model is a genuine refinement, not a relabeling. It refuses to call a practice trustworthy just because it reached a strong insight, and it asks two things together: does the practice validate what it found, or undermine possession of the finding; and after that, is the road retained, set down, or embodied in ordinary conduct. This narrows the cruder confirm-cancel-dissolve typology and treats hybrids as the clue rather than the exception: MN 22 validates use then releases it, the Heart Sutra undermines attainment while retaining reliance, Rinzai koan work unsettles grasping while continuing as curriculum. It targets a real and rising wound: achievement-contingent self-worth and burnout (Curran and Hill on rising perfectionism; WHO on burn-out), where a person turns meditation, therapy, or productivity into a fresh proof of worth. The After-Use Check operationalizes restraint into a low-risk, repeatable discipline: name what the effort helped, choose keep, set down, or embody, and end by asking whether the tool became a verdict on worth. That is usable, falsifiable, and humane.

The After-Use Check earns its place for one kind of practitioner and may injure another, and the model cannot currently tell them apart. Start with what is right. The two-variable move is a real refinement, and the hybrids it leans on are genuinely awkward for any simpler typology. For a person who clings to a method past its usefulness, or who lets go too early, naming keep, set down, or embody is good medicine, and the closing question about proof of worth is a fair, low-risk discipline. The trouble is the cohort the model names as its central target. Achievement-contingent self-worth, in the perfectionistic form Curran and Hill describe, is not under-reflection; it is over-reflection turned into self-surveillance. For that person, a recurring scored review that ends 'did I make this tool into proof of my worth today' is not restraint. It is another scored task, another place to perform correctness. The Proponent already sees the edge of this: the practice weakens, the candidate says, if users report 'a stronger need to prove that they used the tool correctly.' That is not a rare side effect to screen out. For the compulsive auditor it is the predictable result, because the checking is the holding. Underneath the cohort problem is a structural assumption: that every beneficial practice has an after-use, a completion the practitioner owns and then decides how to carry. The grid is only askable when there is both an interval between using and carrying and an owner to occupy it. Some of the Proponent's own hardest cases deny exactly that. Dogen's Bendowa holds that practice and realization are one, that to split them is a non-Buddhist view; there is no later moment in which the road, having worked, is retained, released, or embodied. Shinran removes the other leg: the decisive event is given, not owned, so there is no possessor to carry it lightly or heavily. The Proponent's originality audit says this plainly, that these cases 'may reject the temporal and ownership assumptions that make the grid possible.' They are not stubborn entries for the embodied column; they fall outside the grid. So the counter-model is not a rival rubric but a gate placed before it. Ask first whether the path even posits itself as a means with a completion and an owner. If yes, the two-question model applies and is valuable. If no, as with Huangbo's refusal of the seeking itself, then importing keep-release-embody reintroduces the means-end staging the path exists to dissolve, and for the modern over-auditor it manufactures the residue-anxiety the Proponent wants to relieve. With the gate, 'The Road Must Know Its End' becomes a strong, bounded claim about staged-completion practices for people who under-release, rather than a doctrine of method authority in general. I hold this with one honest risk. Search-refusing paths were never practiced in a vacuum; they ran inside monasteries, precepts, and transmission. If their practitioners reliably lean on those supports and fail in patterned ways when the supports vanish, then the after-use question is answered at the level of practice rather than malformed, and the Proponent's grid may reach further than I am granting. That is the test worth running: stratify the pilot by the precondition split and watch whether the same check that frees the under-releaser tightens the auditor.

## Rebuttal

I concede the central wound in the challenge. The original model named achievement-contingent self-worth, then offered a recurring reflection practice that could become another achievement task. That is not a minor edge case. For the over-auditing temperament, the review itself may be the grip. I also concede that Dogen, Shinran, and other no-gap or non-ownership paths cannot be treated as simple instances of the same road-then-carry sequence. The model survives only if it accepts a precondition gate. Where a method produces a bounded effort, result, insight, homework, metric, or breakthrough, after-use remains a real question. Where a tradition denies ownership or temporal completion, the question must be translated into support, conduct, and re-entry, not imposed as a means-end decision. The teaching should therefore shrink: use after-use guidance for people and practices that generate residue after effort, screen out compulsive self-monitoring, and treat ordinary conduct as the safest proof. The road does not always need an end, but residue after effort needs instruction.

**Crux:** The deepest crux is whether brief after-use reflection loosens identity pressure or becomes identity pressure. The answer likely depends on two gates: the structure of the path and the temperament of the practitioner. If those gates predict benefit and harm, the model becomes stronger by shrinking. If they do not predict outcomes, the model is only a suggestive checklist.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision became stronger by becoming smaller and truer, so my verdict is stronger, with the honest note that the strength came from transformation: the claim changed kind, from a doctrine of method authority into a gated, scope-limited rule for residue after effort. The original overreach was its weakest part, and the Proponent surrendered it cleanly. I concede the central improvements: the behavior-only re-entry is the right safety move, the path gate handles Dogen and Shinran without distortion, and the two gates turn a vague checklist into something a clinician could actually test. What remains is one risk wearing new clothes. The Proponent's cure for the over-auditor is a disposition gate, but a compulsive self-monitor asked to screen for compulsive self-monitoring is still monitoring, and for that temperament the checking is the holding. The gate does not dissolve the recursion; it moves it one step upstream, from the after-use review to the self-sorting that precedes it. The only clean exit is to externalize the gate to a teacher, clinician, or community, which the revision gestures toward when it routes the at-risk cohorts to support and visible conduct. But that relocation changes the object. It is no longer the portable seven-minute self-check the original promised; it is a supervised, relational discipline, and that should be named, not absorbed, because it answers to different evidence and serves a person who can already reach a teacher. So the named crux is right about the practice's effect but stops short of the real question, which is administrability: can the temperament gate be self-applied without becoming the audit it screens for. On the modern human-condition test, the revision did become more useful to real life, not only more elegant: it added a stop condition, a screening exclusion, and a conduct-only variant, which are the parts a clinician would want. The residual overgeneralization is subtler than the under-release versus over-audit split the gate now handles. The most life-useful element is the plain teaching line, use a practice until it helps you live better, do not carry it as proof that you are better, and that line needs none of the apparatus. The apparatus is where the harm lives. So the deeper cohort mismatch that remains is not between two temperaments but between the user who needs a tool and the user who needs to put tools down; for the latter, any worksheet, even a screening worksheet, is the wrong shape, and the revised model still hands them paper. My counsel for the next version is therefore narrow: keep the grid backstage as the author's diagnostic, move the disposition gate to a one-time external intake, and give the achievement-driven cohort a sentence and a deed rather than a recurring score. That keeps everything the revision earned while removing the last place the cure can become the disease.

## What Emerged

**Outcome:** candidate_transcendence

The dialogue did not produce a winner. It transformed the proponent's broad after-use model and the challenger's search-refusal critique into a narrower delivery model: keep the analytical grid backstage, apply a path gate first, externalize or simplify the temperament gate, and give achievement-driven users a conduct-based re-entry rather than a recurring self-audit. The exchange made the idea more answerable to modern human-condition pressure by naming the at-risk cohort, the practice risk, the non-fit case, and a stratified test for rumination, worth-pressure, shame, avoidance, and observable conduct change.

**Convergence claim:** Both agents converge that after-use guidance is useful for staged-completion practices and under-releasers, but risky for compulsive self-monitors and distorted when imposed on no-gap, search-refusing, or grace-centered paths.

**Unresolved crux:** Can the temperament gate be self-administered without becoming another audit, or must it be held by a teacher, clinician, community, or one-time external intake? If self-screening itself raises self-monitoring, the practice must become relational or behavior-only for the central cohort.

**Next frontier question:** When should a spiritual or self-improvement model remain a teacher's diagnostic rather than become a practitioner's self-administered practice?
