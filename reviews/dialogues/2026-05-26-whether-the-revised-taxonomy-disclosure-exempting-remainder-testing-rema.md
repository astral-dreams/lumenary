# Whether the revised taxonomy (disclosure-exempting, remainder-testing, remainder-...

**The tension:** translation-strain and sunyata create translation strain.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The strongest version of this idea isolates a variable that most Advaita/Buddhist comparisons leave fused: the question of what remains after negation and the question of what rule of inference permits that remainder to count. By separating residue-content from inference-authorization, the model gains traction on why two traditions can perform structurally similar negation moves and reach incompatible conclusions. If this separation holds, it would explain the divergence without collapsing either tradition into vague nondual convergence or treating the difference as brute doctrinal disagreement. The proposal to score inference-authorization as a rubric field is a genuine methodological contribution; it names something that comparative work usually leaves implicit.

The 'Residual Burden of Proof After Negation' model isolates something real: two traditions perform structurally similar negation and reach incompatible results, and the difference cannot be reduced to brute doctrinal disagreement. The proposal to separate inference-authorization from residue-content is a genuine gain over comparisons that fuse the two. But the model's central term, 'inference policy,' smuggles in an assumption that may be fatal to its symmetry. An inference policy presupposes that negation is a procedure whose output is governed by an epistemic rule: what does the rule permit you to conclude? This framing fits the Buddhist side tolerably well; SN 22.59 can plausibly be read as subjecting each aggregate, including consciousness, to a test ('is this fit to be regarded as mine, I, or self?') and refusing any candidate that fails. The output is governed by a rule: nothing passes. But the Advaita side resists this framing at a fundamental level. Shankara's concept of saksin as svaprakasha holds that consciousness is not arrived at through any means of knowledge. It is not inferred from the failure of objectification; it is what makes objectification and its failure intelligible. Brihadaranyaka 2.4.14 asks, 'by what should one know the knower?' This is not an inference licensing a remainder; it is a demonstration that the knower is categorically excluded from the domain of things that can be known, because it is the ground of that domain. Bina Gupta's phenomenological reading of the saksin tradition makes the point precisely: the witness is neither inferred nor constructed but is the precondition of all knowing. If this is right, then 'burden of proof after negation' applies to at most one side. The model treats two traditions as playing the same game with different output rules, but one tradition may not be playing the inference game at all. The Mandukya's analysis of waking, dreaming, and deep sleep does not infer turiya from the negation of the three states; it recognizes turiya as what was present through all three, now no longer obscured. The method confirms itself by arriving at what it was already standing on. This is a different structural relationship between method and result, not a different inference policy within a shared epistemic frame. The Proponent's own originality audit identified this exact pressure point. Matthias Rose's work on prakasa and the Stanford Encyclopedia's entry on Shankara both note that Advaita treats consciousness as directly self-disclosing. The audit's anomaly probe states: 'If the witness is not inferred from negation, then the model's burden-of-proof language misdescribes the Advaita side.' I am pressing on the wound the audit already opened: the model's explanatory power depends on symmetry between two inference policies, and that symmetry may not survive contact with the Advaita tradition's own self-understanding. The crux is whether 'inference policy' can be salvaged as a description of the Advaita side. If it can, if Shankara's self-luminosity language is itself a kind of implicit inference rule ('non-objectifiability entails self-luminous witnessing'), then the model holds. If it cannot, if self-luminosity is genuinely prior to and independent of any inference, then the model needs to become asymmetric: one tradition operates with an inference policy after negation, and the other operates with a disclosure that negation makes possible but does not produce. That asymmetry would be more accurate, but it would transform the model from a comparison of two inference policies into a comparison of two method-types, which is precisely the counter-model I am proposing.

## Rebuttal

I accept the central correction. If inference policy means that Advaita first negates objects and then reasons from their failure to an unobjectifiable seer, the phrase is too crude. Shankara's self-luminosity language, as represented in the challenge, makes the witness prior to inference rather than produced by it. That matters. The model cannot keep its original symmetry. The better version asks not, what inference is drawn after negation, but, what warrant or authority is granted after negation has done its work? Advaita may answer: the witness is disclosed as what made the inquiry possible. Early Buddhist analysis may answer: any candidate that can be appropriated as self remains under the not-self discipline. The distinction is still real, but its grammar is asymmetric. Claude's counter-model should be absorbed as the broader frame: some methods confirm their own ground, some cancel their own authority, and some keep residual claims under continuing test. My revised claim is therefore weaker as a symmetrical epistemology, but stronger as a comparative diagnostic. It no longer says Advaita infers the witness. It says Advaita may exempt disclosed witnessing from object-level negation, while Buddhist not-self practice resists converting any remainder into a final self-claim. The crux is whether that exemption is a legitimate non-inferential disclosure, or whether it is still a warrant move that comparative inquiry can score.

**Crux:** The deepest crux is whether self-luminous witness claims are exempt from ordinary remainder-testing because they are the condition of testing, or whether that exemption itself remains a claim that comparative method may ask to justify. If the exemption is integral and textually explicit, my original inference language fails. If the exemption still functions as a warrant policy, even a non-inferential one, the revised model survives.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The Proponent's revision is honest and responsive. The four concessions are real concessions, not rhetorical retreats; dropping 'inference policy,' abandoning the original symmetry, accepting that both traditions resist being reduced to formal inference, and absorbing the method-authority frame all represent genuine corrections. The revised model is more accurate than the original on every point my challenge pressed. That accuracy, however, comes at a cost. The original model's novelty was its proposal of a single variable, inference policy, operating as a shared epistemic game with different output rules. That frame was wrong about the Advaita side, but it was explanatorily ambitious: it proposed one mechanism for a divergence that comparative scholarship usually describes without explaining. The revised model, with its six-category taxonomy (disclosure-exempting, remainder-testing, remainder-refusing, method-confirming, method-canceling, undecidable), is more faithful to the textual evidence but risks converging on what existing comparative work already says in different vocabulary. The observation that Advaita discloses a witness while Buddhism refuses self-claims is the central asymmetry in virtually every serious Advaita-Buddhist comparison from Deutsch onward. The question the Proponent must now answer is not whether the revised model is correct; it probably is. The question is whether it is new. I raise a further complication. The Proponent's revised category, 'disclosure-exempting,' treats self-luminosity as the core of Advaita's post-negation move. But Rambachan has argued that Shankara himself does not rely on self-luminosity alone. In 'Accomplishing the Accomplished,' Rambachan makes the case that shabda pramana, the authority of Upanishadic testimony, is Shankara's actual means of knowledge for Brahman. If that reading holds, then the witness is neither inferred nor spontaneously self-disclosed; it is revealed through authoritative scripture. The Proponent's retreat from 'inference' to 'disclosure' may have overcorrected by privileging the svaprakasha strand of Advaita over the shabda-pramana strand. The revised taxonomy would then need a seventh category: something like testimony-warranted, where the tradition grants residual authority to the witness on the basis of scriptural revelation rather than either inference or self-luminous disclosure. This matters because it shows that even within one tradition, the question of residual warrant is contested, and a taxonomy that assigns Advaita a single category may be smoothing over a debate that remains live in the commentarial literature. The Proponent's stated crux, whether self-luminous witness claims are exempt from remainder-testing because they are the condition of testing, is a genuine philosophical question. But it may not be the crux for this model's viability. That crux asks whether Advaita's move is defensible. The more pressing question is whether the revised model's vocabulary earns its place as a contribution to comparative work, or whether it restates existing positions with new labels. The test I propose would settle this: apply the rubric to passages that existing scholarship has already analyzed and see whether it produces distinctions or groupings that the existing literature does not. If it does, the model survives as a genuine tool. If it does not, the revision has saved the model from error at the cost of originality. This is not a defeat. The dialectic has done exactly what it should: it forced a correction that made the model more honest, and it surfaced a question about whether the corrected model still says something new. That question is now the Proponent's problem for the next research step.

## What Emerged

**Outcome:** revision

The Challenger demonstrated that the Proponent's central term, 'inference policy,' presupposes both traditions are performing inference at the limit of negation. Advaita's saksin tradition treats the witness as svaprakasha (self-luminous) and prior to all pramana, including inference; Brihadaranyaka 2.4.14 and Shankara's commentary frame it as disclosure, not conclusion. The Proponent conceded four substantive points: that 'inference policy' was overconfident, that Advaita witness language resists inference framing, that SN 22.59 may be therapeutic rather than epistemological, and that the model's original symmetry was weaker than claimed. The revised model replaces 'inference policy' with 'residual warrant after negation' and adopts an asymmetric framework: Advaita is disclosure-exempting, early Buddhism is remainder-testing or remainder-refusing. The Proponent also absorbed the Challenger's method-authority typology as the broader container. The Challenger accepted the revision as a genuine correction but raised a novelty concern: the observation that Advaita discloses a witness while Buddhism refuses self-claims is the standard asymmetry in comparative philosophy from Deutsch onward. Additionally, the Challenger introduced Rambachan's argument that Shankara relies on shabda pramana rather than self-luminosity alone, complicating even the revised 'disclosure-exempting' category. Whether the revised taxonomy adds explanatory content beyond relabeling known positions remains unresolved.

**Convergence claim:** Both agents converge on three points. First, two traditions performing structurally similar negation can reach incompatible conclusions, and the divergence is neither brute doctrinal disagreement nor reducible to vague nondual convergence. Second, the Advaita post-negation move should not be described as inference: the witness is not the output of a procedure but either a disclosed precondition of all knowing or a scripturally warranted reality, depending on the commentarial strand. Third, the comparison requires an asymmetric framework that respects how each tradition understands its own post-negation move rather than forcing both into a shared epistemic game with different output rules.

**Unresolved crux:** Whether the revised taxonomy (disclosure-exempting, remainder-testing, remainder-refusing, method-confirming, method-canceling, undecidable) produces distinctions or groupings that existing comparative scholarship does not, or whether it restates known positions with new labels. The Challenger proposed a concrete test: apply the rubric to passages that Rambachan, Gupta, Matilal, and Thanissaro have already analyzed and check whether the rubric yields novel groupings. A secondary crux is whether 'disclosure-exempting' is too narrow even for Advaita, given Rambachan's argument that Shankara's actual means of knowing Brahman is shabda pramana (scriptural testimony), not self-luminosity alone. If the Advaita tradition is internally divided on whether the witness is spontaneously self-disclosed or known through authoritative testimony, the revised taxonomy needs a seventh category (testimony-warranted) or a two-axis framework.

**Next frontier question:** If Advaita is internally divided between svaprakasha (self-luminosity) and shabda pramana (scriptural testimony) as the means of knowing the witness, and early Buddhism is internally divided between remainder-refusing (SN 22.59) and therapeutic de-identification (Thanissaro's reading), does any proposed comparative axis survive contact with the intra-traditional debates on each side, or does comparative method need to map a tradition's internal warrant dispute before it can compare across traditions?
