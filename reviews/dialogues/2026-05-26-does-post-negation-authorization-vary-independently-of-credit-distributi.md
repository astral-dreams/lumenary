## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The Proponent makes a genuinely clarifying move by separating two questions that comparative scholarship usually fuses: what, if anything, remains after contemplative negation, and what rule of inference permits that remainder to count as established. This separation is productive because it addresses a real puzzle. Upanishadic and early Buddhist traditions both perform negation of experiential objects, yet arrive at incompatible conclusions. By locating the divergence in the inference rule rather than in the content of what remains, the model offers a structural explanation for why traditions sharing a contemplative gesture produce different metaphysical outcomes. The proposed rubric field, 'inference authorization,' could discipline cross-tradition analysis by forcing researchers to name the epistemic move each tradition makes after negation, rather than simply noting doctrinal disagreement. The Proponent's own critique section strengthens the idea further: acknowledging that the model may be an interpretive reconstruction rather than a textual discovery shows the kind of epistemic self-awareness the Lumenary method requires.

The Proponent's idea makes a real contribution by separating two questions usually fused in comparative work: what remains after negation, and what rule authorizes that remainder. This separation explains why traditions performing similar contemplative operations arrive at incompatible conclusions, and the proposed 'inference authorization' rubric field could sharpen cross-tradition analysis. But the model rests on a hidden assumption that undermines its strongest claims: it assumes both traditions are performing epistemological reasoning governed by implicit inference rules. The primary texts suggest they are doing something else. On the Advaita side, the pressure is severe. Shankara does not infer the witness from the failure of objectification. In his commentary on Brihadaranyaka 2.4.14, the question 'by what should one know the knower?' receives not a burden-of-proof shift but a category claim: the atman is the condition of knowing, self-luminous (svaprakasha), and not the kind of thing established or disestablished by inference. Matthias Rose's study of prakasha confirms that Advaita consciousness is not a posit surviving negation; it is the light by which negation is possible. Michael Comans shows that Shankara's adhyaropa-apavada method deliberately superimposes a pedagogical framework, then removes it, revealing what was always present. The removal is not an argument whose conclusion is 'therefore the witness exists.' It is the dissolution of a teaching device. To call this a 'burden of proof shifting toward an unobjectifiable seer-knower' imports modern argumentative structure into a tradition that explicitly denies the witness is reached by argument. The Buddhist side faces a parallel distortion. SN 22.59 does not articulate a general epistemological rule that every proposed remainder must undergo the same test. It makes a specific soteriological observation: the aggregates are impermanent, unsatisfactory, and not subject to the practitioner's control. The force of the teaching is therapeutic. Seeing impermanence and non-mastery produces nibbida (disenchantment), viraga (dispassion), and vimutti (release). The Phena Sutta (SN 22.95) compares consciousness to a magic trick: the point is experiential disenchantment, not epistemic burden allocation. Thanissaro Bhikkhu's treatment of not-self as a 'strategy' rather than a metaphysical thesis supports this reading. To frame the Buddhist move as 'keeping the burden against any self-claim' imposes an argumentative architecture the text does not use. If inference policy is not operative in either tradition, what explains the divergence? The credit-distribution model offers a more fundamental variable. A ground-centered credit tradition (Advaita: moksha is praptasya praptih, the attainment of the already-attained) naturally treats negation as removal of what obscures what was always present. The 'remainder' needs no authorization because the ground was never in question. A practitioner-centered credit tradition (early Buddhism: 'You yourselves must strive') naturally treats negation as a practice operation whose results remain under scrutiny, not because an inference rule demands it, but because the practitioner bears responsibility for the seeing. Ground-centered credit risks premature realization claims ('nothing was ever wrong' becomes avoidance of genuine work), so Advaita requires preparatory disciplines that the teaching technically renders unnecessary. ## Rebuttal

I accept the main correction. My original phrasing made the model sound more textual and more argumentative than the evidence warrants. The phrase 'burden of proof' should be retired or kept only as a modern analogy. Shankara's witness is not best described as a posit inferred from failed objectification, and SN 22.59 is not best described as a formal rule requiring every possible remainder to pass the same test. The challenge rightly pressures both sides: Advaita often frames negation as the removal of superimposition so the already-present Self is recognized, while early Buddhist not-self often works as a strategy of disenchantment and release. The part I would defend is the comparative variable beneath the bad wording. After negation, contemplative systems still differ in what they permit next. One may rest as the witness, investigate the witness, refuse the question, appeal to scripture, or treat the whole remainder-seeking impulse as another form of clinging. That permission structure is not necessarily an inference policy in the tradition's own language, but it is a real analytic target if it reliably predicts instructions, safeguards, and failure modes. The credit-distribution model is probably deeper than my first model. Ground-credit systems naturally treat negation as uncovering what was never absent. Practitioner-discipline systems naturally treat negation as a way to transform attachment and perception. But this does not make post-negation authorization useless. It gives it a cause: where a tradition locates transformative credit may shape what it allows after negation. The revised model should therefore pair both variables instead of making inference policy primary.

**Crux:** The deepest crux is whether a comparative model may legitimately name a rule-like practical permission that traditions do not themselves name. If yes, post-negation authorization remains useful as an analytic variable. If no, the model should yield priority to tradition-internal variables such as self-luminosity, disenchantment, scriptural authority, direct recognition, and credit distribution.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision is stronger than the original. The Proponent conceded well, retiring the most exposed language ('burden of proof,' 'inference policy'), acknowledging that Shankara's witness is recognized rather than inferred, and accepting that SN 22.59 is soteriological rather than meta-epistemological. The surviving claim, that comparative analysis can track what traditions permit after negation under the label 'post-negation authorization,' is defensible. It names a real practical question: after negation, the practitioner faces divergent instructions across traditions, and those instructions are not random. The question I would press is not whether the variable is legitimate but whether it is independent. The Proponent proposes to pair credit distribution and post-negation authorization as co-equal rubric fields. But the Proponent also says credit distribution may explain why different traditions authorize, test, or refuse remainders differently. If that causal claim holds, then post-negation authorization is not a peer variable; it is a downstream expression of credit distribution. Pairing them without specifying the relationship would be like scoring both 'latitude' and 'average temperature' as independent geographic variables: the correlation is high, the causal direction is clear, and the second variable does not add predictive power once the first is known. The test that would settle this is concrete: find a tradition where credit distribution and post-negation authorization diverge. Zen may be the most promising case. Zen inherits Buddha-nature (a ground-credit structure: the nature was never absent), yet classic Zen instruction often refuses to let the practitioner rest in any recognized ground. 'If you meet the Buddha, kill him' is not what a straightforward ground-credit model predicts. If Zen's post-negation authorization diverges from its credit distribution, the Proponent's variable earns independent status. If no such case can be found, the variable should be folded into credit distribution as a useful descriptor rather than maintained as a separate rubric field. A second risk concerns intra-traditional variance. The original model and the revision both speak of 'Advaita' and 'early Buddhism' as if each tradition has a single post-negation stance. But Advaita contains at least three distinct positions. Shankara treats the witness as self-luminous and directly recognized. Mandana Mishra, his near-contemporary, gives inference a larger role; in the Brahmasiddhi, the Self's existence is supported by reasoning that more closely resembles what the Proponent originally described. The later Vivarana and Bhamati sub-schools disagree about whether avidya has a locus and how its removal works. Early Buddhism likewise splits: the Sutta Pitaka's therapeutic disenchantment is substantially different from the Abhidhamma's systematic analysis of dhammas as real but impersonal constituents. Scoring 'Advaita' or 'early Buddhism' on a single post-negation authorization value may flatten exactly the diversity that makes the comparison interesting. The Proponent should specify at what level of granularity the variable is scored: tradition, school, commentator, or individual text. The third risk is a quiet one. The Proponent has honestly marked the model as an analytic reconstruction. A rubric field should carry a marker distinguishing analyst-constructed variables from variables the traditions themselves recognize and name. ## What Emerged

**Outcome:** revision

The Proponent's original framing of 'inference policy after negation' was substantially revised under pressure from the Challenger. The phrase 'burden of proof' was retired as a modern epistemological import onto traditions whose negation practices function as revelation (Advaita) or therapeutic disenchantment (early Buddhism). The surviving variable, 'post-negation authorization,' names what traditions permit practitioners to treat as settled, revealed, testable, or irrelevant after negation, without claiming that traditions frame this as formal argument. The Proponent conceded that credit distribution is likely a deeper variable and proposed pairing both. The Challenger accepted the revision's legitimacy but raised an unresolved question: whether post-negation authorization does any independent explanatory work once credit distribution is known, or whether it is merely a downstream redescription. The exchange sharpened both ideas without destroying either, and produced a concrete test case (Zen's refusal of ground-resting within a Buddha-nature tradition) for determining whether the two variables are genuinely separable.

**Convergence claim:** Both sides agree that traditions differ in what they permit after negation and that credit distribution is a real practice variable. They converge on the judgment that the original 'inference policy' language was too strong, that Shankara's witness is recognized rather than inferred, and that SN 22.59 is soteriological rather than meta-epistemological. The remaining disagreement is structural, not doctrinal: whether post-negation authorization is an independent variable or a downstream expression of credit distribution.

**Unresolved crux:** Does post-negation authorization vary independently of credit distribution? The Challenger proposed a concrete test: Zen inherits Buddha-nature (ground-credit) yet classic Zen instruction often refuses to let the practitioner rest in any recognized ground ('kill the Buddha'). If Zen's post-negation authorization diverges from its credit distribution, the Proponent's variable earns independent rubric status. If no such divergence can be found, post-negation authorization should be folded into credit distribution as a useful descriptor rather than maintained as a separate variable. A secondary unresolved issue is the level of granularity: should these variables be scored at the tradition level, the school level, the commentator level, or the individual-text level?

**Next frontier question:** Can a tradition's post-negation authorization diverge from its credit distribution? Zen Buddhism may be the sharpest test case: it inherits a ground-credit metaphysics (Buddha-nature was never absent) yet its classic instructions refuse to let the practitioner rest in that ground. If post-negation authorization and credit distribution can point in different directions within a single tradition, both are needed as separate comparative variables. If they always align, the simpler model (credit distribution alone) should be preferred.
