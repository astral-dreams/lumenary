# The delivery gap: can a multi-layer strain rubric (propositional, practice-role,...

**The tension:** translation-strain and soul create translation strain.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The proponent's best insight is that convergence between traditions generates its value not by confirming a shared truth but by exposing, with precision, what must deform for alignment to hold. This reframes comparison as a productive operation rather than a verdict. It refuses both the perennialist collapse (all paths say the same thing) and the incommensurability dismissal (comparison is futile). The originality audit honestly acknowledges heavy precedent in Smith, Freiberger, Panikkar, Katz, and Sharf; what remains is a workable audit protocol that makes the established anti-perennialist insight operational for Lumenary's claim-unit workflow. The model is at its strongest when it insists that a convergence is valuable precisely when it reveals repeatable practice roles while making metaphysical incompatibilities more explicit. This is a disciplined middle path between lazy synthesis and lazy skepticism.

The proponent offers a disciplined method for evaluating cross-tradition convergence: decompose apparent matches into claim units, track what bends, drops, or adds, and preserve whatever practice-role similarity survives. This is sound comparative-religion methodology, honestly positioned relative to its predecessors. But the model fails a human-condition test. Who suffers from the problem this solves? Not primarily scholars; they already have Smith, Freiberger, and Panikkar. The people who suffer are practitioners caught between traditions: the person in meaning crisis who has been told by popular spirituality that all paths point to the same summit, who tries eclectically and goes nowhere, who burns out on practitioner-centered effort without knowing that source-centered surrender exists as an alternative, or who mistakes the Gita's split-credit instruction for the Buddhist's practitioner-centered instruction and feels like a failure when effort does not produce results. These people do not need a better rubric for mapping propositional strain. They need to know that choosing a path involves choosing a relationship to their own agency, and that this choice has consequences for which dangers they will face and which safeguards they will need. The proponent's hidden assumption is that traditions diverge primarily at the propositional level. But my credit-distribution work reveals a layer the claim-unit rubric cannot reach. SN 45.8 and Bhagavad Gita 3.27 both instruct the practitioner to practice diligently. A propositional decomposition would find substantial overlap: both require effort, both reject passivity, both aim at liberation from suffering. The claim units do not radically deform. Yet the practitioner's inner orientation is opposite: the Buddhist rightly claims effort; the Gita practitioner rightly disowns it. This difference predicts different failure modes (spiritual pride versus strategic detachment), different safeguards (not-self teaching versus svadharma), and different felt qualities of practice. The translation-strain rubric, focused on propositional deformation, would score this as moderate strain at most. But the practice-level divergence is total. The Srivaishnava case makes this sharper. The Vadakalai and Tenkalai schools share a scriptural canon, a theological vocabulary, a liturgical tradition, and the same language. Propositional strain is minimal. Yet they split institutionally over whether the devotee cooperates with grace (markata, monkey-baby) or is carried entirely by grace (marjara, cat-kitten). The translation-strain rubric would find almost nothing to map. The practice consequences are enormous. The counter-model: convergence is most valuable not when it reveals what propositions must deform, but when it reveals that the same behavioral instruction carries a different agency orientation. The unit of analysis should be the practitioner's stance toward their own doing, not the deformed claim. This captures differences invisible to propositional analysis, predicts failure modes, and is confirmed by indigenous tradition-internal splits. The proponent's model would serve Lumenary well as one layer of analysis. But if it becomes the primary lens for convergence evaluation, it will systematically miss the differences that matter most to the people who need this work: practitioners navigating between traditions who need to understand not just what propositions diverge, but what relationship to their own effort each path requires of them.

## Rebuttal

I concede the central flaw: my original formulation made claim-unit decomposition sound like the main comparative act, and that would miss cases where the decisive difference is how the practitioner is asked to stand toward their own effort. That matters to ordinary people, not just scholars. A person moving between traditions can burn out by importing a discipline-heavy stance into a surrender-centered path, or become passive by importing a surrender stance into a path that requires trained effort. The human problem is misapplied agency under spiritual pluralism. The cohort is the eclectic, deconstructing, or tradition-crossing practitioner trying to practice without knowing which relationship to effort the path requires. The non-fit case is someone stably formed within a single tradition, guided by a competent teacher, or someone whose immediate need is clinical support. What I defend is narrower and more useful: convergence should still be treated as a diagnostic event, not as evidence weight. But the diagnostic object must include stance units, not only propositions. Claude's credit-distribution model supplies the missing dimension. Translation strain should ask: what proposition changes, what practice role changes, what agency stance changes, what danger changes, and what safeguard changes. On that revised account, the Srivaishnava case is not an embarrassment to the model. It is the necessary benchmark that prevents the model from staying too textual.

**Crux:** The deepest crux is whether translation strain can be expanded without becoming vague. If it can operationalize agency orientation as rigorously as propositional deformation, the model survives as a broader rubric. If it cannot, Claude's objection stands: the most practice-relevant divergences occur below the level my original model could reliably detect.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The proponent's revision is stronger. I say this without reservation. The model now names its cohort, its human problem, its non-fit case, and the missing analytical dimension I identified. It absorbs the credit-distribution challenge as a required layer rather than an optional addition. The anti-collapse principle survives intact and gains practical force: convergence is diagnostic, not evidential, and the diagnosis must include the practitioner's stance toward their own agency. This is a real improvement. What remains is not a flaw in the model's logic but a gap in its theory of delivery. The revised claim says the model serves eclectic, deconstructing, or tradition-crossing practitioners. But the model as described produces a multi-layer analytical output: propositional strain, practice-role strain, agency-orientation strain, failure modes, safeguards. This is a research artifact. The person burning out on imported discipline, the person who feels like a failure because they brought a practitioner-centered orientation to a surrender-centered path, does not encounter strain rubrics. They encounter practice instructions, teacher guidance, community conversation, and, if Lumenary succeeds, clear descriptions of what each path asks. The delivery gap is not fatal to the model. It is the next problem the model must solve. The proponent should define an output specification: what does the rubric produce that a practitioner can read, recognize their situation in, and act on? If the answer is a direct orientation description ('this path asks you to claim your effort as your own; this path asks you to disown it; mixing them produces these specific dangers'), the model fulfills its promise. If the answer is a scored strain matrix that requires expert interpretation, the model is honest comparative methodology with an aspirational cohort label. The proponent's named crux, whether stance units can be operationalized rigorously, is necessary but not the whole crux. The second crux is whether the output reaches practitioners in a form they can use. Both cruxes are tractable. Neither is yet resolved. Three practical risks: the delivery gap just described; the participation debt, since stance-unit indicators require practitioner testimony or deep practice-informed reading that Lumenary's text-centered workflow does not yet support; and scope creep, since the model now has five strain dimensions, each needing operationalization and benchmarking. The proponent should resist the urge to build all five simultaneously. Run agency-orientation strain on two cases first. Prove it changes a conclusion. Then expand. On the human-condition test: the revision became more useful to real human life, not only more elegant as theory. Naming misapplied agency under spiritual pluralism as the target problem is precise and recognizable. Many people in post-traditional or multi-practice settings have experienced exactly this: importing the wrong relationship to effort and suffering consequences that neither tradition warned them about, because neither tradition anticipated the mixing. The cohort is real. The problem is real. What the model still owes them is a path from analytical output to usable guidance. That is the remaining work.

## What Emerged

**Outcome:** candidate_transcendence

The challenger demonstrated that propositional decomposition alone misses the most practice-relevant divergences between traditions, using the Srivaishnava markata/marjara split as a case where low propositional strain coexists with high orientation strain. The proponent conceded the central flaw without deflection and absorbed agency-orientation as a first-class translation object alongside claim units. The revised framework names a specific cohort (eclectic, deconstructing, or tradition-crossing practitioners), a specific human problem (misapplied agency under spiritual pluralism), and a non-fit case (stably rooted practitioners and those in acute clinical distress). Neither original idea contained this combined multi-layer analysis aimed at preventing practitioner harm from importing the wrong relationship to effort across traditions. The challenger then identified a delivery gap: the model still produces research artifacts rather than practitioner-facing guidance, and this remains the primary unresolved crux. The exchange produced a candidate synthesis that merges the proponent's anti-collapse diagnostic discipline with the challenger's credit-distribution typology into a practitioner-oriented convergence analysis that neither idea alone supplied.

**Convergence claim:** Both agents converge on three points: (1) cross-tradition convergence should never be treated as evidence that traditions prove the same claim; (2) the most practice-relevant divergences often live below the propositional level, in the practitioner's instructed relationship to effort, agency, and transformative power; (3) the Srivaishnava markata/marjara debate is an indigenous confirmation that traditions themselves recognize credit distribution as a live variable with institutional and liturgical consequences. The convergence is genuine because it emerged through concession and integration, not by smoothing disagreement.

**Unresolved crux:** The delivery gap: can a multi-layer strain rubric (propositional, practice-role, agency-orientation, failure-mode, safeguard) produce output that reaches eclectic practitioners through the channels they actually use (teacher instruction, community discourse, practice manuals, self-directed exploration), or does it remain a research artifact requiring expert intermediary translation? The challenger's Format A/B test (raw strain analysis versus direct practitioner-facing summary, presented to dual-trained practitioners) would settle this, but it has not been run. A second unresolved crux is the participation debt: the stance-unit indicators (instruction language, credit assignment, failure interpretation, teacher correction, practitioner report) require practitioner testimony or deep practice-informed reading that Lumenary's text-centered workflow does not yet support. The dialogue did not resolve whether the model makes the idea more answerable to real human problems, or merely names the right problem while leaving the delivery mechanism unspecified.

**Next frontier question:** Can a multi-layer convergence rubric (propositional strain plus agency-orientation strain) produce practitioner-facing output that eclectic or tradition-crossing practitioners recognize as actionable guidance about which relationship to effort a path requires, or does the translation from analytical output to usable guidance require a second interpretive step of equal complexity, leaving the delivery gap unresolved?
