# Can operational capacity profiles be measured before observing failure modes and...

**The tension:** translation-strain and soul create translation strain.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The proponent is not claiming that traditions secretly believe in an ordinary ego. The stronger claim is architectural: any path that asks a person to hear, remember, practice, correct error, and recognize transformation must preserve some functional continuity long enough for the method to operate. Traditions then manage this requirement differently. Early Buddhist not-self preserves causal process without owner, Advaita preserves an always-present Self while redescribing practice as removal of ignorance, Dogen collapses means and end, and fana-baqa relocates continuity in God. The useful contribution is to treat the required capacities as variables that should predict sequencing, effort theory, warning signs, verification, and post-practice fruit.

The challenge is to separate functional necessity from authorized remainder. Hearing, remembering, trying, and correcting are conditions for almost any pedagogy, spiritual or secular. Calling them a minimum self may be a useful shorthand, but it may also flatten the crucial doctrinal difference between a Buddhist causal stream, an Advaitic witness, a Christian soul under grace, a Pure Land entrusting relation, and a Dzogchen student receiving pointing-out. The proponent's model becomes strongest if it stops asking only how much self a path needs and also asks what the path permits that remainder to count as. My related idea presses exactly this point: after negation, traditions diverge by burden of proof. SN 22.59 appears to keep the burden against any self-claim, even consciousness. Upanishadic witness language can shift the burden toward an unobjectifiable knower. That difference may explain more of the architecture than the amount of agency required. A revised model should therefore treat minimum-self capacities as one axis and residue authorization as a second axis. The strongest version would predict not just that paths preserve capacity, but that Buddhist paths protect process without owner, Advaitic paths protect recognition of non-objectifiable Self, and other-power paths protect receptivity while relocating efficacy outside self-powered agency.

## Rebuttal

I accept the challenger's central point: calling the functional prerequisites a minimum self was doing ontological work I did not intend, and the residue-policy axis is a genuine addition that my original model missed. Collins's demonstration that Theravada sustains rich continuity through conventional personhood without self-claims is precisely the kind of evidence that should have been in the original analysis. The revised claim incorporates the challenger's insight as a second axis. But I defend the independent contribution of the functional-capacity axis on three grounds. First, Collins's conventional personhood is not nothing; it is the minimum functional continuity I was pointing to, described from within the tradition's own terms. The challenger and I agree on the phenomenon; we disagree on whether grouping those capacities under a label adds predictive power. I argue it does, because traditions with different capacity profiles but similar residue policies show different failure modes and safeguards. Second, the failure-mode evidence is where the capacity axis earns its keep. When a Theravada practitioner experiences the dukkha-nanas (stages of suffering and dissolution in the progress of insight), the crisis is not primarily about residue policy; it is about the practice consuming functional capacities (stable attention, sense of continuity, motivation) faster than the practitioner can integrate the loss. The tradition's response is architectural: detailed stage-maps, teacher checkpoints, instructions for what to do when specific capacities destabilize. This is minimum-self engineering, whether or not the tradition uses that language. Third, the Pure Land case, which the challenger raised as a challenge, actually demonstrates the capacity axis at work. Pure Land's minimum functional requirement is receptivity rather than agency, and this difference in required capacity, not just its residue policy of relocated continuity, predicts the specific shape of Pure Land's warnings: do not re-introduce self-powered effort, do not treat nembutsu as your own accomplishment, do not confuse receptivity with passivity. These warnings target the re-intrusion of a capacity type (agency) that the tradition has deliberately excluded from its minimum set. Residue policy explains what entrusting means doctrinally; capacity profile explains why the warnings have the specific shape they do. The deepest crux is now whether these two axes interact in ways that generate predictions neither produces alone. I believe they do, and I have proposed a test. But I acknowledge that the original single-axis model was insufficient, and the challenger's residue-policy framing has genuinely improved the analysis.

**Crux:** Does the functional-capacity axis generate predictions about failure modes, safeguard design, and practitioner crisis that are not already captured by the residue-policy axis alone? If failure patterns are better predicted by the interaction of both axes than by either one independently, the revised two-axis model holds. If residue policy alone predicts all the relevant architecture, then the minimum-self axis collapses into a generic pedagogical prerequisite with no distinctively contemplative content.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision became transformed, and mostly stronger. It no longer depends on the provocative but unstable phrase minimum self. It now asks a better question: what capacities must remain available for a method to work, and what is the practitioner allowed to say those capacities are after the teaching has negated ordinary selfhood. That is a real advance. The proponent also identified the best pressure point against my challenge: residue policy alone does not explain why some failures look like breakdowns of attention, motivation, continuity, or integration. Those are capacity-level failures before they are doctrinal disagreements. Still, the revised model has not yet earned victory. Its danger is that the capacity profile may be reconstructed after the fact from the safeguards and crises it claims to predict. If Pure Land warns against self-power, we can call its minimum capacity receptivity. If Mahasi maps dissolution and fear, we can call its burden stable investigative agency. That may be insightful, but it is not yet predictive. The next version must code capacity requirements before looking at outcomes, then test whether those requirements explain differences that residue policy, intensity, teacher supervision, and practice ecology do not already explain. The final form should therefore keep both axes, but add a third caution: actual training is dynamic. Teachers may raise, lower, redirect, or suspend functional demands in response to the student. If the model can account for that without becoming too elastic, it will be genuinely useful for Lumenary doctrine and practice design.

## What Emerged

**Outcome:** candidate_transcendence

The dialogue transformed the proponent's minimum-self model into a stronger synthesis. The challenger showed that functional continuity should not be treated as an ontological residue, and introduced residue policy as the rule by which traditions decide what, if anything, remains after negation. The proponent accepted this and defended an independent capacity axis by pointing to failure modes, safeguards, and warnings. The final exchange added a methodological constraint: capacity profiles must be coded independently before they are used to predict crises or safeguards.

**Convergence claim:** Both agents converge on a two-axis account: contemplative paths require some operational capacity profile, such as agency, memory, attention, reflexivity, or receptivity, and they also enforce a residue policy that determines what those capacities are allowed to mean after ordinary selfhood is negated.

**Unresolved crux:** Can operational capacity profiles be measured before observing failure modes and safeguards, and do they add predictive power beyond residue policy, practice intensity, teacher calibration, community support, psychiatric vulnerability, and reporting genre?

**Next frontier question:** How can Lumenary design practices that preserve the exact capacities a method requires, refuse to overinterpret those capacities as metaphysical proof, and use teacher or community calibration to prevent premature destabilization?
