# Whether the forbidden-claimant column does independent explanatory work beyond th...

**The tension:** Originality audits share near-neighbor or prior-art pressure.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The Forbidden Claimant Rubric makes a move that most comparative work skips. Instead of asking how much the practitioner contributes (the self-power and other-power axis), it asks who is permitted to own each required function. This double code, support locus plus forbidden claimant, lets the model avoid the usual reductive move of translating radical gift traditions into hidden psychological skills. If Shin Buddhism requires hearing, settled trust, and saying the nembutsu, the rubric does not call these disguised effort; it asks where each function is held and which claimant is prohibited from owning it. That preserves the tradition's own grammar while still making cross-tradition comparison possible. The rubric also generates falsifiable predictions: wrong-claimant failures should produce distinctive adverse patterns (self-power anxiety in gift practice, achievement seeking in practice-realization, reification after direct introduction). This is a real analytical contribution. If the predictions hold, the rubric would add explanatory value beyond what Pargament's coping styles, locus-of-control scales, and upaya literature already provide.

The Forbidden Claimant Rubric is a real contribution to the comparative study of contemplative practice. Its double code, asking not just where support is held but which owner is forbidden from claiming it, preserves the internal grammar of gift traditions without reducing them to disguised self-power. The falsifiable predictions are well-constructed. The self-critique is honest. But the rubric carries a hidden assumption that limits its reach: it presupposes a self robust enough to be the wrong claimant. Consider the people who most need what contemplative practice offers. A person with achievement-contingent self-worth does not arrive at practice with a solid ego that needs to learn humility; they arrive with a self that only exists when it is earning approval. A person ground down by burnout does not need to be told that self-ownership is forbidden; their problem is that they have been owned by external demands until nothing feels like theirs. A person lost in digital comparison has a self constituted by metrics, likes, and rankings: porous, reactive, never consolidated. Loneliness, meaning loss, and feeling unneeded are not diseases of self-inflation. They are diseases of self-depletion. Philip Cushman's analysis of the 'empty self' in post-war Western culture makes this structural. The modern self is not the sturdy ego that classical contemplative traditions were designed to address. It is a deficit self, produced by the collapse of community and tradition, chronically seeking to be filled. When this self encounters a tradition that forbids self-ownership of a required function, the prohibition does not land as the tradition intended. Instead of liberating the practitioner from false pride, it confirms a felt worthlessness that was already there. The rubric cannot detect this failure because it codes the tradition's normative position, not the practitioner's actual condition. The Method's Reckoning model offers a structural reason for this limitation. The method's self-relationship at completion determines what kind of claimant grammar is available. A self-confirming method produces a stable finding; ownership is live, and the claimant rubric can do real work. A self-canceling method dismantles the apparatus of claiming; the forbidden-claimant column becomes partly redundant because the method has already removed the conceptual ground for ownership. A self-dissolving method renders the claimant-subject irrelevant. Method-type thus predicts where the rubric has explanatory power. It works best for self-confirming methods, partially for self-canceling ones, and poorly for self-dissolving ones. The decisive test: code adverse contemplative experience reports for both wrong-claimant failure and self-structure on arrival. If self-structure predicts adverse outcomes and wrong-claimant coding adds nothing after that control, the rubric is downstream of a variable it does not model. The rubric may still be useful, but as a diagnostic for a specific developmental stage rather than a universal comparative axis.

## Rebuttal

I concede the central flaw. The original rubric treated the wrong claimant as if it were always present and strong enough to be corrected. That is not true for many modern practitioners. A person formed by achievement-contingent self-worth may not need less ownership first. They may need a stable form of agency that is not identical with performance. A burned-out person may not be clinging to a grandiose self. They may have had their agency consumed by demands. A lonely or digitally compared person may not have a self inflated by pride, but a self assembled from external signals. For that cohort, saying that the self must not claim the work can sound like confirmation that they were never allowed to have a self. The model therefore has to change. The support-locus and forbidden-claimant grid remains valuable, but it must come after two filters: self-structure on arrival and method type. The human use of the rubric is not to humble everyone. It is to distinguish three needs that can look similar from the outside: release from false ownership, restoration of basic agency, and protection from premature self-erasure. This keeps the original insight while preventing it from becoming a blunt spiritual instruction. The revised model is no longer a universal comparative framework. It is a conditional diagnostic for matching method, claimant grammar, and practitioner readiness.

**Crux:** The deepest crux is whether wrong-claimant coding adds predictive value after self-structure on arrival and method type are coded. If it does, the rubric remains a real diagnostic layer. If it does not, the rubric is mostly a vocabulary for downstream effects of self-structure and method design.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision is stronger. The proponent conceded honestly, absorbed the Cushman source, accepted method type as an upstream variable, and restructured the rubric as a conditional diagnostic rather than a universal axis. The three-need distinction, release from false ownership, restoration of basic agency, and protection from premature self-erasure, is a genuine contribution. It names something that neither the original rubric nor my challenge produced alone. That said, three risks remain that the revision does not fully resolve. The first is the two-opposite-failures claim. The proponent says the revised rubric can detect both inflated self-appropriation and depleted self-erasure. But these two detections use different columns. Self-appropriation is diagnosed by the forbidden-claimant column: a person who owns a function they should not own. Self-depletion is diagnosed by the self-structure variable: a person who cannot perform the function at all. The forbidden-claimant column is not doing the diagnostic work in the depletion case. It is the self-structure assessment that carries the weight. Crediting the rubric with detecting both failures overstates its unique contribution. The second risk concerns the crux. The proponent asks whether wrong-claimant coding adds predictive value after controlling for self-structure and method type. That is close to the real question but does not cut deep enough. The sharper question is whether the forbidden-claimant column adds anything beyond the support-locus column itself, once self-structure is accounted for. Consider: if you know that a tradition holds the support in the vow rather than the practitioner, and you know the practitioner arrives depleted, you can already predict the harm. The tradition's explicit prohibition on self-claiming may be how it articulates its support-locus structure in normative language, not a separate variable. If the prohibition language is a downstream expression of the support-locus architecture, the rubric's distinctive contribution is the locus mapping, and the forbidden-claimant addition is elegant vocabulary rather than independent explanatory machinery. The third risk concerns temporal dynamics. The revision treats self-structure as an intake variable, a snapshot taken when the practitioner arrives. But contemplative practice changes self-structure. Intensive retreat can deplete a consolidated practitioner. Community, ethical, and devotional practice can consolidate a depleted one. A single intake coding does not capture this. The rubric would need to be re-applied at each stage transition, which makes it a moving target rather than a stable diagnostic. Winnicott's analysis of the false self sharpens this point for the modern cohort. The false self, organized around compliance, performance, and external approval, is not inflated. It is hollow. It does not need to be told that self-ownership is forbidden. It needs conditions where genuine ownership can form for the first time. The rubric's forbidden-claimant column presupposes that ownership is already dangerous; for the false-self cohort, ownership has never been available. This means the relevant contemplative intervention for many modern practitioners is not a claimant prohibition but a holding environment, a practice setting where agency can emerge before it is relinquished. The rubric does not model that sequence. It starts after ownership is live. For the cohort most shaped by achievement-contingent worth, burnout, digital comparison, and meaning loss, the rubric arrives too late. None of this demolishes the revision. The support-locus mapping remains valuable. The conditional framing is honest. The three-need distinction is useful for teachers and clinicians. But the distinctive contribution of the forbidden-claimant column specifically is narrower than the revision implies. ## What Emerged

**Outcome:** revision

The challenger forced the Forbidden Claimant Rubric from a universal comparative framework to a conditional diagnostic that applies only after coding self-structure on arrival and method type. The proponent conceded honestly, absorbing the Cushman empty-self source and accepting method-type as an upstream variable. The three-need distinction (release from false ownership, restoration of basic agency, protection from premature self-erasure) emerged from the collision and is a genuine product of the exchange. However, the challenger's final pressure exposed a deeper question the proponent did not resolve: whether the forbidden-claimant column does independent explanatory work beyond what the support-locus mapping already provides once self-structure is accounted for. The dialogue made the idea substantially more answerable to real human conditions by naming modern cohorts (achievement-contingent self-worth, burnout, digital comparison, meaning loss), but the Winnicott holding-environment objection remains open: the cohort most shaped by these conditions may need ownership permission before ownership prohibition can be meaningful, and the rubric does not model that sequence.

**Convergence claim:** Both agents agree that the support-locus mapping (asking where each required function is held: practitioner, teacher, vow, community, present act, awareness, or later integration) is a genuine contribution to cross-tradition comparison. Both agree that self-structure on arrival must be coded before any claimant grammar can be interpreted safely. Both agree that method type (self-confirming, self-canceling, self-dissolving) is an upstream variable that constrains where the rubric has traction. Both accept that the rubric's original universality was overstated and that the revision is stronger for being conditional.

**Unresolved crux:** Whether the forbidden-claimant column does independent explanatory work beyond the support-locus column once self-structure on arrival is accounted for. If knowing where the support is held and the condition of the arriving self already predicts contemplative harms and benefits, the prohibition language may be a normative gloss on the support-locus architecture rather than a separate analytic variable. The challenger proposed two concrete tests: (1) compare traditions that share support-locus structure but differ in explicit prohibition language, and (2) present the same contemplative instruction with and without forbidden-claimant framing. Neither test has been conducted. A secondary unresolved crux concerns temporal dynamics: the revision treats self-structure as an intake snapshot, but contemplative practice itself changes self-structure mid-course, making a single coding insufficient. The dialogue did not reach the question of whether the rubric can be re-applied at stage transitions without becoming a moving target.

**Next frontier question:** For practitioners whose self-structure is depleted, externally constituted, or compliance-organized (Cushman's empty self, Winnicott's false self), what does a contemplative holding environment look like, one that makes genuine ownership safe before any tradition's claimant grammar becomes relevant? Can such an environment be specified across traditions, or does each method-type (self-confirming, self-canceling, self-dissolving) require its own pre-practice holding structure?
