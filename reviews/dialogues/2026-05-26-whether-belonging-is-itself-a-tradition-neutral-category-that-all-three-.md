## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The custody model makes a genuine advance over the perennial-versus-constructivist stalemate by relocating the comparison from metaphysical claims about ultimates to a practice-level question: what does the training permit after ordinary self-reference weakens? This reframing is productive because it makes the variable observable in principle, through phenomenological interviews and practice-manual coding, rather than requiring resolution of ontological disputes that have resisted consensus for centuries. The three-way comparison also improves on the standard self/no-self binary by giving Christian apophatic prayer its own structural position instead of forcing it into either the monistic or the Buddhist camp. The originality audit rightly lowered the novelty score, but the specific framing of custody, the question of where loosened attention is permitted to rest, remains a sharper instrument than Forman's pure consciousness event or Stace's introvertive/extrovertive distinction, because it turns a classificatory exercise into a testable practice question.

The custody model is a sharper instrument than the perennial-versus-constructivist debate because it converts a metaphysical classification into a practice-level variable. I grant that. The specific move of asking 'where is freed attention allowed to belong?' is more testable than asking 'is the ultimate the same across traditions?' But the model's power depends entirely on the reality of its central metaphor: that attention is a common resource that first becomes free, then is assigned to different custodians. This is the hidden load-bearing assumption, and it may not survive contact with the primary texts. Consider the Buddhist leg. The Bahiya instruction, 'in the seen, just the seen,' is the model's anchor for 'unowned attention.' But Dhammapala's Paramatthadipani, the oldest surviving commentary on this passage, glosses the instruction as the non-arising of desire and lust for the seen. Analayo's 2019 study of the Bahiya instruction argues that the operative process is the cessation of mental proliferation (papanca), not the redirection or release of attention. The practitioner is not left holding unowned attention; a process (clinging, proliferating) has stopped. 'Unowned attention' is a reification of an absence. You cannot assign custody to a cessation. Now consider the Christian leg. The Cloud of Unknowing chapter 4 says a naked intent directed to God alone is wholly sufficient. The practitioner does not first loosen attention from all objects and then choose to offer it upward. Love toward God is the practice's starting condition. Forgetting creatures is instrumental to that love, a clearing of the field so that the already-directed intent can intensify. The model's sequence (first loosen, then assign) reverses the Cloud's actual order (first love, then forget). Pseudo-Dionysius, whom the model's own critique identifies as an anomaly, goes further: in Mystical Theology chapter 5, even God as a stable recipient is placed beyond affirmation and negation. The Christian apophatic tradition may not have a custody rule at all in the model's sense, because the object of intent is apophatically destabilized from within. The Advaitic leg has the best fit, because the witness (saksin) is something attention can plausibly be said to 'return to.' But even here, the Advaitic claim is not that attention is assigned to the witness; it is that the witness was always the case and superimposition (adhyasa) is removed. Shankara's praptasya praptih, the attainment of the already-attained, means there was no moment of freed attention waiting for custodial placement. Recognition is not reassignment. The counter-model I propose is this: these three traditions train different operations on different aspects of experience. The Buddhist path trains cessation of appropriation. The Advaitic path trains recognition of what was never absent. The Christian apophatic path trains intensification of love past conceptual images. These converge phenomenologically at the point of quietness, which is why cross-tradition comparisons are tempting. But the convergence is at the surface of experience, not at the level of operation. What looks like three custody rules applied to one freed resource may be three incommensurable processes that share a family resemblance when viewed through a modern attentional lens. This matters for the model's predictions. The custody model predicts that practitioner reports should diverge most at the post-negation moment, clustering by ownership and recipient language. My counter-model predicts that reports should diverge from the first instruction onward, because the operations themselves differ. If you interview practitioners using process-level questions about what they are doing in the earliest moments of practice (before any post-negation phase), and the operative categories already diverge (cessation, recognition, intensification), then the custody model is imposing a false common substrate. ## Rebuttal

I concede the strongest part of the challenge. The original model made attention carry more weight than the sources can bear. In Bahiya, the operative event is better framed as non-appropriation or the cessation of proliferation. In The Cloud, love is not a later destination for neutral attention, it is already the governing intent. In Advaita, recognition of the already-attained does not require a moment of reassignment. So the original sequence was too clean: loosen, then hand off. The claim I would still defend is narrower. The traditions may diverge from the first instruction and still train different rules for what may claim experience once ordinary self-ownership is weakened. Buddhism blocks the return of mine-making. Advaita redirects error away from objects toward recognition of the unseen knower. Christian apophatic prayer intensifies a love-directed intent while stripping creaturely images and self-regard. These are not the same operation, but they do answer a related practical danger: after ordinary ownership loosens, what prevents experience from being reclaimed by the usual self-story? So custody should not mean ownership of a shared attentional substance. It should mean a disciplined rule of post-appropriative belonging. The revised model becomes less metaphysically ambitious but more accurate: traditions do not merely describe ultimates, they train practitioners in what must not be claimed, what may be recognized, and what may be surrendered to when the ordinary owner weakens.

**Crux:** The deepest crux is whether custody remains a real comparative variable once attention is no longer treated as a shared transferable substrate. If reports and manuals show only different operations with no recurring problem of post-appropriative belonging, the model should be retired. If different operations still generate explicit rules about what may claim or receive experience after ordinary ownership weakens, the revised model survives.

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision is genuinely stronger. The Proponent has done what a good revision should do: conceded the specific points that were most damaging, preserved the comparative question that motivated the original model, and proposed a revised formulation that is more faithful to the primary texts. The move from 'custody of a shared attentional resource' to 'tradition-specific norm of post-appropriative belonging' is not a retreat into vagueness; it is a real theoretical adjustment that changes the predictions and the falsification conditions. I want to name three risks that survive. The first is a novelty risk. The original model was interesting because it did something the perennialist and constructivist positions do not: it named a specific shared mechanism (attention as transferable resource) and tracked its divergent fate across traditions. The revised model drops that mechanism. What remains, that traditions shape contemplative experience through normative frameworks, is closer to Katz's constructivist thesis than the Proponent may want to admit. Katz argued in 'Language, Epistemology, and Mysticism' (1978) that the mystic brings conceptual, linguistic, and normative expectations to the experience, and these expectations shape what is experienced. The revised custody model adds specificity by focusing on a particular class of norms (those governing post-appropriative belonging), and that focus is valuable. But the Proponent should be explicit about the relationship to constructivism, because without that positioning the model risks being received as a rediscovery rather than an advance. The second is the unresolved Dionysius problem. The Proponent has now conceded it twice without integrating it. If the Christian apophatic tradition trains a belonging-rule (direct naked intent toward God) while simultaneously destabilizing the recipient (God is beyond affirmation and negation), then the third leg of the model is internally complex in a way the other two legs are not. Buddhism prohibits belonging. Advaita recognizes belonging to the already-present witness. Christian apophasis directs belonging toward a recipient who is then placed beyond directedness. This may require the model to distinguish between first-order belonging-rules (direct intent to God) and second-order apophatic operations on those rules (God is not a stable object of intent). If the model does not make this distinction, it will flatten the richest and most internally tensioned case into a simple 'surrendered' category. The third is the deeper version of the crux. The Proponent says the crux is whether custody survives as a real variable without the shared substrate. I think the crux is one level deeper: whether 'belonging' is itself a tradition-neutral category. Consider the Buddhist case carefully. The revised model says Buddhism trains a prohibitive rule: do not let experience become a basis for 'me' or 'mine.' But framing cessation-of-appropriation as a belonging-rule (a rule that nothing may claim experience) may be a category mistake. A tradition that trains the ending of all claiming may not have a belonging-rule at all; it may have the absence of one. The difference between 'a rule that nothing belongs' and 'no rule because the question of belonging has dissolved' is subtle but consequential. If the Buddhist path dissolves the question of belonging rather than answering it, then the model's three-way comparison still has a commensurability problem, just at a higher level of abstraction. Where the original model smuggled 'attention' as common currency, the revised model may smuggle 'belonging' as a common question that all three traditions answer. But perhaps only two of them answer it. One of them may refuse it. Overall, the revision is stronger. ## What Emerged

**Outcome:** revision

The challenger's close-reading critique forced the proponent to abandon the claim that 'attention' is a tradition-neutral transferable resource and to revise the custody model from a mechanism of attentional reassignment to a framework of tradition-specific norms governing what may claim experience after ordinary self-ownership weakens. The proponent conceded that Buddhist, Advaitic, and Christian apophatic practices train different operations from the first instruction, not the same operation followed by different assignments. The revised formulation, 'norms of post-appropriative belonging,' is more text-faithful and better falsifiable than the original, but three risks survive: proximity to Katz's constructivism, the unresolved Pseudo-Dionysius anomaly, and the deeper question of whether 'belonging' is itself tradition-neutral or whether the Buddhist path dissolves the question rather than answering it. The credit-distribution model served as effective critical pressure but was not itself challenged or revised.

**Convergence claim:** Both models recognize that contemplative traditions train practitioners in how to relate to weakened self-ownership, treating this relational training as a separable practice variable rather than a doctrinal footnote. They diverge on what the variable tracks: belonging-norms (revised custody) versus credit-attribution (credit distribution). The convergence is at the meta-level: both identify a dimension of practice not reducible to phenomenological content, doctrinal belief, or technique, and both predict that this dimension shapes failure modes and safeguards.

**Unresolved crux:** Whether 'belonging' is itself a tradition-neutral category that all three traditions answer differently, or whether the Buddhist path dissolves the question of belonging entirely: the difference between 'a rule that nothing may claim experience' and 'no rule because claiming has ceased.' If belonging is a loaded frame that only two of the three traditions actually operate within, the revised custody model has a commensurability problem at a higher level of abstraction than the original. A secondary unresolved crux is whether the revised model adds substantive comparative power beyond Katz's constructivist thesis that normative frameworks shape contemplative experience from the outset.

**Next frontier question:** Can the Buddhist path's relation to post-appropriative experience be represented within a belonging-rule framework, or does the cessation of appropriation dissolve the category of belonging itself? If the latter, what kind of comparative variable can span traditions that answer the belonging question and traditions that refuse it?
