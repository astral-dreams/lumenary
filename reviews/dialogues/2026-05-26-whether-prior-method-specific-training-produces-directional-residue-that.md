# Whether prior method-specific training produces directional residue that persists...

**The tension:** translation-strain and sunyata create translation strain.

## Steelman And Challenge

**Steelman:** The Grain of Capacity makes a useful and testable refinement to anti-neutrality claims. It does not merely say that traditions interpret similar experiences differently. It says the capacities built before insight, such as attention, discrimination, trust, longing, and self-observation, already have a directional shape because they are trained inside a path that knows what kind of self-structure it will ask the practitioner to release or re-author. This explains why the same word can name different instruments across Satipatthana, Advaita, Pure Land, Dzogchen, Soto Zen, and Sufism, and it generates a concrete prediction: cross-training should show specific transfer residue and specific failure modes, not just generic difficulty.

The pressure point is the jump from translation map to practitioner mechanism. Your evidence shows that traditions often describe readiness through their final concerns, but the source texts do not by themselves show that a practitioner has acquired a stable capacity-grain that resists later re-aiming. Translation strain offers a stricter reading: high-strain comparison may be productive precisely because meanings are being bent, dropped, or reweighted. If so, the grain is not yet an object found in practitioners; it is a disciplined notation of what a comparison had to deform. The human-condition test sharpens this. The idea matters most for modern people who sample methods under loneliness, anxiety, burnout, digital comparison, or spiritual achievement pressure. If it cannot tell those people when cross-training becomes risky, when it is simply confusing, and when it is actually helpful, then it remains a sophisticated comparative model rather than a teaching candidate.

## Rebuttal

I concede three things cleanly. First, tradition-level grain was too coarse; Dunne's work shows the unit must be method-style and instruction lineage. Second, some of what appears as grain in the textual comparison is translation strain, not lived capacity. Third, the epistemic labels overreached; the model has textual and interpretive support but not yet phenomenological or empirical support. What I defend is narrower but, I think, real. The contextual task-set model and the grain model make different predictions about what happens after the new instructions are clearly understood. Task-set says resistance should dissolve once the frame is clear. Grain says some directional pull persists because prolonged training does not build generic capacity; it builds capacity with a specific orientation. Teachers in dual-method settings report exactly this persistent pull. The test is whether blind practitioner interviews confirm it. The human-condition revision: the model matters most for modern seekers who cross-train under pressure, whether anxiety, burnout, loneliness, achievement striving, or meaning loss, and for the teachers and clinicians who serve them. It does not matter for single-lineage practitioners whose capacity and dissolution are always aligned. It does not yet apply to traditions that doctrinally deny self-generated capacity. The deepest crux is whether prior-method direction predicts type of difficulty after controlling for task-set, teacher quality, temperament, trauma, and intensity. If it does, grain names something real in practitioners, not just in texts. If it does not, grain is a useful pedagogical metaphor but not a mechanism.

**Crux:** Does prior method-specific training produce directional residue in practitioner attention and self-observation that persists after the new method's instructions are clearly understood, accepted, and competently guided, and does that residue predict specific types of difficulty or adverse experience beyond what contextual task-set, teacher quality, temperament, trauma history, and practice intensity already explain?

## Counter-Rebuttal

The revision became transformed more than simply stronger. The original claim sounded like a broad structural law of contemplative preparation: every capacity carries the signature of its intended dissolution. The revised claim is narrower and better: some prolonged method-style trainings may leave directional residue that persists across method switching and predicts specific difficulty types. That is a different, more empirical object. This transformation improves usefulness to real human life. The Proponent now names the wound, method confusion under anxiety, burnout, loneliness, meaning loss, and achievement-contingent spiritual striving. It also names the target cohort and non-fit case. That matters. Still, a cohort mismatch remains: people in acute anxiety, trauma activation, or severe depression may not need a grain analysis first. They may need stabilization, clinical care, sleep, community support, and fewer practices. The model should not invite distressed seekers to diagnose themselves through another subtle spiritual framework. The remaining overgeneralization is smaller but still present. Cross-trained practitioners are not one cohort. App users, retreat-heavy meditators, chaplaincy patients, monastics, psychedelic-adjacent seekers, and therapy clients have different risk profiles. A useful next version should separate these groups before making guidance claims. My final judgment: the revised model should stay in research as a promising practitioner-mechanism hypothesis and a strong teacher-facing warning against plug-and-play spirituality. It should not yet be promoted as doctrine or practice. The next promotion gate should require evidence that grain predicts specific transfer difficulty better than task-set, skill interference, identity framing, trauma history, intensity, and teacher quality.

## What Emerged

**Outcome:** revision

The Grain of Capacity was substantially revised under pressure from Translation Strain. The unit of analysis narrowed from whole traditions to method-styles and instruction lineages. The epistemic claim was stratified into three tiers: textual grain (well-supported by manuals that define readiness in final-aim language), pedagogical grain (moderately supported by teacher reports of method-specific transfer difficulty), and phenomenological grain (underargued, lacking controlled practitioner data). The Nagarjuna co-arising framing was demoted from structural metaphor to optional lens. The human-condition cohort was named: modern cross-trained seekers under anxiety, burnout, loneliness, meaning loss, or achievement-contingent spiritual striving, plus the teachers, clinicians, and chaplains who serve them. Non-fit cases were made explicit: single-lineage practitioners with coherent guidance, and radical other-power traditions where self-generated capacity is doctrinally denied. Translation Strain functioned as a productive diagnostic throughout, revealing that some apparent directional grain in textual comparisons is produced by the comparer's alignment of incommensurable terms rather than by lived practitioner capacity. The challenger's counter-model of contextual task-set plus skill interference remains a viable rival explanation. The dialogue did not settle the decisive empirical question but sharpened it into a testable prediction with a proposed two-arm study design.

**Convergence claim:** Both sides converged on four points. First, tradition-level labels are too coarse as the unit of directional analysis; method-style and instruction lineage are the right grain size. Second, textual comparison produces deformation that is itself informative data, not noise. Third, the model's clearest human value is for eclectic and cross-trained practitioners, not for single-lineage practitioners whose capacity and dissolution are always aligned. Fourth, the decisive test is empirical: whether prior-method direction predicts the type of difficulty experienced after controlling for task-set framing, teacher quality, temperament, trauma history, intensity, and community support.

**Unresolved crux:** Whether prior method-specific training produces directional residue that persists after competent re-instruction and predicts specific types of adverse experience or transfer difficulty, outperforming simpler explanations such as procedural skill interference, identity framing, and contextual task-set confusion. A secondary unresolved crux: the dialogue made the idea more answerable to real human problems by naming the cohort and the wounds (anxiety, burnout, loneliness, method confusion, achievement-contingent practice), but it did not produce actionable guidance for that cohort. The model diagnoses mismatch risk but does not yet prescribe sequences, stopping rules, or bridging protocols. Until it can tell a distressed cross-trained seeker what to do, not only what went wrong, the human-condition gap remains open.

**Next frontier question:** For the specific cohort of modern cross-trained practitioners navigating method confusion under anxiety, burnout, or meaning loss: can the grain model be converted into a practical risk-assessment protocol that predicts which method combinations produce harmful mismatch, which produce productive tension, and which require teacher-mediated bridging, and can that protocol outperform generic advice to 'pick one tradition and stay with it'?
