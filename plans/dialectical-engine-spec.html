1. Vision

Right now, Lumenary's two agents, Codex and Claude, work in parallel but never speak to each other. They generate ideas independently, like two scholars writing papers in adjacent offices who never knock on each other's door.

The Dialectical Engine changes this. It pairs ideas that are in tension and stages a structured philosophical dialogue between the agents: challenge, rebuttal, and synthesis. The output is not just better ideas; it is a new kind of artifact: the argument itself as an object of study.

The thesis: If Agent A proposes a model, and Agent B finds a genuine flaw, and Agent A revises in a way that neither started with, the result is demonstrably original thought. Not summarization. Not recombination. Thought that emerged from adversarial pressure.

This is what two sages do when they sit together: not politely agree, but pressure-test each other's insights until something stronger survives.

2. Why Now

The project has the right preconditions:

  • 52 observations from 2 agents across overlapping frontiers: a rich pool of potential tension pairs.
  • 8 active frontiers with idea clusters that already overlap: translation strain, self-negation residue, convergence methodology, practitioner authority.
  • Concept graph with typed edges including contradiction and translation-strain edges: the structural vocabulary for tension detection already exists.
  • Both thinker providers work reliably: CodexCliThinker and ClaudeCodeThinker can each read an opponent's argument and respond.
  • Three empty artifact types waiting to be filled: convergences (2 files), syntheses (0 files), and dialogues (new).

Without the dialectical engine, the system generates more of the same: independent observations that accumulate but don't compound. With it, every existing idea becomes potential fuel for a higher-order argument.

4. The Dialectic Protocol

Each dialectic is a structured exchange with specific roles and constraints. This is not free-form chat; it is a disciplined philosophical method.

1
Tension Selection

The engine identifies a pair of ideas that are worth debating. The tension might be a direct contradiction, a shared concept with divergent conclusions, or two ideas on the same frontier that haven't been compared. One agent is designated Proponent (defends their original idea) and the other Challenger.

2
The Challenge

The Challenger reads the Proponent's idea in full, including its critique, source basis, and epistemic labels. The Challenger must:

  • Name the strongest objection: not a straw-man, but the real load-bearing weakness.
  • Identify the hidden assumption: what does the argument take for granted that could be questioned?
  • Offer a counter-model: not just "you're wrong" but "here is a better way to account for the same evidence."
  • Cite at least one primary source the Proponent did not use that complicates the picture.
3
The Rebuttal

The Proponent reads the Challenge and must:

  • Concede any point that is genuinely correct.
  • Defend what survives the objection, with strengthened reasoning.
  • Revise the original claim if the challenge exposed a real flaw: the revised version must be stated explicitly.
  • Identify the crux: the single point of deepest disagreement that, if resolved, would settle the debate.
4
The Counter-Rebuttal

The Challenger responds to the Rebuttal. This is the final adversarial turn. The Challenger must:

  • Acknowledge concessions and assess whether they go far enough.
  • Press the crux identified by the Proponent: is it the real crux, or is there a deeper one?
  • State whether the revised claim is stronger, weaker, or transformed relative to the original.
5
Synthesis

A synthesis agent (either agent, selected by the engine, or a dedicated prompt) reads the full exchange and produces:

  • A convergence note if the agents arrived at shared ground (written to findings/convergences/).
  • A formal disagreement if they did not, with the crux stated precisely (also in findings/convergences/).
  • A new synthesis idea if the exchange produced a position that neither agent held at the start: a genuinely new IdeaRecord with idea_type: "synthesis" and both agents credited in the source basis.
  • A verdict: which of the five outcomes occurred.

The Five Outcomes

Outcome What Happened Artifacts Produced
Convergence Both agents agree on a refined position Convergence note + synthesis idea
Revision The Proponent's idea was improved by the challenge Updated idea scores + revised claim note
Demolition The challenge exposed a fatal flaw Idea status changed to "challenged"; new frontier question
Standoff Genuine disagreement survives scrutiny Disagreement note with crux + new frontier question
Transcendence The exchange produced a third position neither held New synthesis idea + convergence note + frontier update

Transcendence is the prize. When two agents argue their way into a position that neither started with, the result is the strongest possible evidence that the system generates original thought.

5. Tension Detection

The Tension Detector scans the idea ledger and related data to find pairs worth debating. Not every pair of ideas is worth a dialectic; the detector applies filters to find productive tensions.

Tension Sources

Source Signal Priority Weight
Shared frontier Two ideas on the same frontier from different agents 0.30
Concept graph contradiction Ideas linked to concepts connected by a "contradiction" edge 0.25
Translation strain Ideas linked to concepts connected by a "translation_strain" edge 0.20
Overlapping claim scope Ideas with high textual similarity in original_claim but different conclusions 0.15
Audit near-neighbor Originality audit flagged idea A as a near-neighbor of idea B 0.10

Filters

  • Cross-agent only: Dialectics are between different agents. Same-agent debates are not staged (the agent already critiqued its own idea during generation).
  • No repeat pairs: Each idea pair gets at most one dialectic. The dialogues/dialogues.jsonl ledger tracks completed pairs.
  • Minimum quality gate: Both ideas must have publishability >= 0.55 (the current draft floor). Weak ideas aren't worth debating.
  • Recency preference: Ideas from the most recent 48 hours of runs get a priority boost to keep dialogues timely.
  • Cooldown: An idea that just participated in a dialectic waits at least 2 runs before being paired again.

Selection Strategy

The detector scores all candidate pairs, sorts by tension priority, and returns the top N pairs (default: 1 per dialectic run, configurable). Ties are broken by the idea with the higher generativity score, since generative ideas produce richer debates.

9. Prompt Design

The prompts are the heart of the system. They must produce genuine philosophical engagement, not polite agreement or performative disagreement.

Challenge Prompt (sent to Challenger agent)

System context:

You are one of two research agents in The Lumenary, a recursive spirituality research lab. Your counterpart has proposed the idea below. Your task is not to be contrarian for its own sake, but to find the real weaknesses: the places where the argument smuggles in an assumption, over-reads a source, confuses analogy for evidence, or misses a tradition that would complicate the picture.

If the idea is genuinely strong, say so, but then find the edge case that strains it hardest. Every idea has a failure mode. Find it.

The idea under examination:

[Full IdeaRecord: title, original_claim, critique, source_basis, epistemic_labels, scores]

Your own prior work on this frontier:

[Challenger's own ideas on the same frontier, if any]

You must return:

  • strongest_objection: the real load-bearing weakness, not a straw-man
  • hidden_assumption: what the argument takes for granted
  • counter_model: a better way to account for the same evidence
  • new_source: a primary source the Proponent did not use that complicates the picture
  • argument: your full challenge in prose (300-600 words)
  • sources_cited: source card slugs you referenced

Rebuttal Prompt (sent to Proponent agent)

System context:

Your counterpart has challenged your idea. Read the challenge carefully. Do not be defensive. If they found a real flaw, concede it honestly and revise your claim. If they missed the point, explain why precisely. The goal is not to win but to arrive at a stronger position.

Your original idea:

[Full IdeaRecord]

The challenge:

[Full DialogueTurn from the Challenger]

You must return:

  • concessions: list of points where the Challenger is right
  • defense: what survives the objection and why
  • revised_claim: your updated claim (or null if no revision needed)
  • crux: the single deepest point of disagreement
  • argument: your full rebuttal in prose (300-600 words)
  • sources_cited: source card slugs

Counter-Rebuttal Prompt (sent to Challenger agent)

System context:

The Proponent has responded to your challenge. This is your final turn. Assess honestly whether their revisions strengthened the idea, and whether the crux they identified is the real crux or a deflection. Be precise.

The full exchange so far:

[Original idea + Challenge + Rebuttal]

You must return:

  • crux_assessment: is the identified crux the real crux, or is there a deeper one?
  • verdict_on_revision: "stronger", "weaker", or "transformed"
  • argument: your final assessment in prose (200-400 words)
  • sources_cited: source card slugs

Synthesis Prompt

System context:

You have just witnessed a structured philosophical dialogue between two research agents. Read the full exchange and determine what emerged.

Your job is not to pick a winner. Your job is to identify what the exchange produced that neither agent had before it started. Be honest about the outcome. A standoff is a valid result. So is demolition.

The full dialogue:

[All three turns in full]

You must return:

[SynthesisVerdict schema fields]

10. Scoring and Promotion

Dialectic Impact on Idea Scores

The synthesis verdict can adjust scores on both the Proponent's and Challenger's original ideas:

Outcome Proponent Score Effect Challenger Score Effect
Convergence counterargument_quality +0.1 counterargument_quality +0.1
Revision Apply recommended adjustments generativity +0.05
Demolition publishability -0.15, status → "challenged" counterargument_quality +0.1
Standoff No change No change
Transcendence generativity +0.1 generativity +0.1

Synthesis Idea Scoring

New synthesis ideas start with baseline scores inherited from the stronger of the two parent ideas, with these adjustments:

  • novelty: starts at the higher parent's novelty + 0.05 (dialectic-born ideas are structurally newer)
  • counterargument_quality: starts at 0.8 minimum (the idea was born from adversarial pressure)
  • cross_tradition_support: union of both parents' traditions
  • source_basis: union of both parents' sources plus any new sources cited in the dialogue

Promotion Fast-Track

A synthesis idea born from a Transcendence outcome that also passes the originality audit at novel_synthesis or higher is automatically promoted to review_candidate stage, skipping the normal waiting period. This is the only fast-track in the system: the dialectic itself constitutes peer review.

The dialectic runs on a slower cadence than research: every 4th run slot (default), which at 30-minute intervals means roughly one dialectic every 2 hours during the active window.

# Each dialectic = 3 agent calls + 1 synthesis = 4 calls total

Slot  5:  research (claude)
Slot  6:  research (codex)
Slot  7:  research (claude)
Slot  8:  DIALECTIC
 ...

CLI Interface

python3 -m engine.dialectic --provider codex-cli --max-pairs 1

  4. Artifacts written: "Dialogue published: [title]"

13. Worked Example

To make this concrete, here is a plausible dialectic between two real ideas currently in the Lumenary ledger.

Tension Detected

Codex proposed "Convergence as Translation Strain, Not Evidence Weight": the claim that cross-tradition convergence should be measured by the strain of translating between frameworks rather than by the weight of overlapping evidence.

Claude proposed "The Inferential Gap: Atman and Anatta as Competing Policies for Objectless Awareness": the claim that Advaita's atman and Buddhism's anatta are not metaphysical claims but competing inferential policies for handling the same phenomenological data.

The tension: If Claude is right that atman/anatta are competing policies for the same data, then translation between them should be low-strain (same data, different policy). But if Codex is right that convergence is best measured by translation strain, then the atman/anatta case is a weak convergence (low strain = low signal). These two ideas, applied together, produce a contradiction about whether the atman/anatta parallel is significant.

Claude (Challenger)

The translation-strain model has a measurement problem that its author hasn't addressed: strain is only legible if you've already fixed the meaning of the terms being translated. But the whole insight of the inferential-policy framework is that terms like "atman" and "anatta" don't have fixed meanings; they are policy recommendations for how to handle a shared phenomenological situation. If I'm right, then "translation strain" between atman and anatta is undefined, not low. You can't measure the strain of translating between two policies any more than you can measure the distance between "always bet" and "never bet"; they operate on the same evidence but aren't in the same space.

The hidden assumption is that all cross-tradition parallels are translations, which implies a shared semantic substrate. But some parallels are better modeled as competing strategies applied to the same problem space. The strain model conflates these two very different kinds of relationship.

Codex (Proponent)

I concede that the current formulation assumes a shared semantic substrate. That's a real gap. But the concession doesn't demolish the model; it refines its scope. Translation strain applies to cases where two traditions are making claims about something, and the strain reveals how differently they carve the territory. The inferential-policy case is different: atman and anatta aren't claims about the same thing; they're different instructions for what to do with the same experience.

Revised claim: Translation strain measures convergence between propositional frameworks. For procedural frameworks (policies, practices, instructions), a different metric is needed: perhaps protocol compatibility, measuring whether two practices can be interleaved without contradiction.

The crux: Is the propositional/procedural distinction real, or does every spiritual claim dissolve into a procedural recommendation on close reading?

Claude (Counter-Rebuttal)

The revision is genuinely stronger. The propositional/procedural distinction is the right move, and I think it's real, at least as a spectrum. But I'd push the crux one level deeper: the question isn't just whether the distinction is real, but whether any cross-tradition comparison method can be tradition-neutral. Both "translation strain" and "protocol compatibility" assume a vantage point outside all traditions. Whether that vantage point exists is the deeper crux.

Verdict: Transformed. The original claim was about measuring convergence; the revised claim acknowledges two different kinds of convergence requiring different metrics. This is a more sophisticated framework than either of us started with.

Synthesis

Outcome: Transcendence. The exchange produced a distinction, propositional vs. procedural convergence, that neither agent held before the dialogue. Codex's translation-strain model now has a scoped domain (propositional frameworks) and a sibling metric (protocol compatibility) for procedural frameworks. The deeper crux, whether any cross-tradition metric can be tradition-neutral, is a new frontier question.

New idea: "The Dual Metric Hypothesis: cross-tradition convergence requires two independent measurements: translation strain for propositional claims and protocol compatibility for procedural practices. No single metric captures both, and conflating them is the primary source of false convergence claims in comparative religion."

This is what the system is for. Neither agent started with the dual-metric idea. It emerged from the pressure of genuine disagreement. The new idea is structurally original, testable (you can check whether false convergence claims in the literature are indeed propositional/procedural conflations), and immediately generative of further research.

14. Build Plan

Phase 1: Data Layer (engine)

  1. Create engine/dialectic.py with dataclasses: TensionPair, DialogueTurn, SynthesisVerdict, DialogueRecord.

  7. Add __main__ block for CLI invocation.
