# Nagarjuna's Mulamadhyamakakarika (MMK)

Source ID: buddhism-nagarjuna-mmk
Tradition: Madhyamaka Buddhism
Type: text
Agent: claude
Dating: c. 150-250 CE
Length: ~450 verses in 27 chapters

## Core Claims

1. **Non-Origination (MMK 1:1):** "Not from itself, not from another, not from both, nor without cause: never in any way is there any existing thing that has arisen." Refutes four modes of origination using the catuskoti. Formally identical to Gaudapada's Karika 4.22.

2. **Sunyata = Dependent Origination (MMK 24:18):** "Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness. That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way." The foundational three-way identification: things are empty precisely BECAUSE they are dependently originated.

3. **Self as Catuskoti (MMK 18:6):** "The Buddhas have taught that there is a self, taught that there is no-self, and also taught that there is neither self nor non-self." Not a contradiction but a pedagogical strategy: each teaching corrects a different error (nihilism, eternalism, or the attempt to establish a third position).

4. **Cessation of Prapanca (MMK 18:5-7):** Conceptual proliferation (prapanca) produces conceptual thought (vikalpa), which produces karma and defilements. Emptiness stops the chain at its root. "When the domain of thought has ceased, that which can be stated ceases."

## Key Verses

- **1:1**: Four-fold negation of origination (parallel to Gaudapada 4.22)
- **18:6**: Catuskoti applied to self; critical for the inferential gap analysis
- **24:18**: Dependent origination = sunyata = middle way
- **18:7**: "The nature of things is, like nirvana, unarisen and unceased"
- **18:8**: "Everything is real, and not real, both real and not real, neither real nor not real: this is the Buddha's teaching"

## Cross-Tradition Significance

**MMK vs. Gaudapada (the paradigmatic case):** Both use identical four-fold negation of origination. Both conclude "nothing is ever born." But: Nagarjuna means "there is no birth because things lack inherent existence" (no substratum affirmed; sunyata is itself empty). Gaudapada means "there IS an Unborn (Brahman)"; same negation, opposite inference. This is the strongest test case for both CodeX's translation strain model and Claude's inferential gap model.

**MMK 18:6 and the Inferential Gap:** The verse that says the Buddha taught self, no-self, AND neither-self-nor-no-self suggests that the atman/anatta debate is itself a product of conceptual proliferation. Madhyamaka may represent a third inferential policy: refuse to infer in either direction. This is the "Nagarjuna as third policy" question flagged in Claude's first observation.

**Two Types of Negation:** Scholars (Westerhoff, Priest) distinguish paryudasa (implicative, implies something else) from prasajya-pratisedha (non-implicative, merely denies). The catuskoti uses the latter; it does not affirm an alternative when negating each position.
