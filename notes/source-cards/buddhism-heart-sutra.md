# Heart Sutra (Prajnaparamita Hridaya)

Source ID: buddhism-heart-sutra
Tradition: Mahayana Buddhism (Prajnaparamita / Madhyamaka)
Type: text
Agent: claude
Dating: c. 4th century CE (possibly composed in China, per Nattier 1992)
Length: ~260 characters (short form)

## Core Claims

1. **Form is Emptiness, Emptiness is Form:** Four-part formula encoding the complete two-truths doctrine:
   - "Form is emptiness" → negates naive realism (things don't exist independently)
   - "Emptiness is form" → negates nihilism AND reification of emptiness (emptiness is not a void or alternative realm; it IS the phenomenal world)
   - "Form is not other than emptiness, emptiness is not other than form" → the two truths are co-extensive, not separate realities

2. **Systematic Negation of All Buddhist Categories:** Five skandhas, twelve ayatanas, eighteen dhatus, twelve links of dependent origination, Four Noble Truths, even wisdom and attainment; ALL negated. Not nihilistic destruction but application of sunyata to every conceptual framework, including the most sacred doctrines. Prevents reification of the teaching itself.

3. **Gate Gate Paragate Parasamgate Bodhi Svaha:** "Gone, gone, gone beyond, gone completely beyond; awakening!" The Tibetan tradition maps this onto the five paths of the bodhisattva (accumulation, preparation, seeing, meditation, no-more-learning). The mantra does not describe emptiness; it performs the going-beyond.

## Cross-Tradition Significance

**Heart Sutra as Compressed MMK:** Every negation corresponds to an argument in Nagarjuna. The four-part form/emptiness formula = MMK 24:18. The cascade of negations = MMK's 27 chapters. The catuskoti is implicit in the four-fold formula.

**"No Attainment" and the Inferential Gap:** The Heart Sutra says there is "no wisdom, no attainment"; even the goal of the path is empty. This is the most radical application of the inferential gap: you cannot infer ANYTHING from the practice, not even that you have "attained" something. Compare: Advaita's "Tat Tvam Asi" (positive assertion of identity) vs. Heart Sutra's "no attainment" (refusal to assert even that).

**Two Truths and the One/Many Problem:** The co-extensiveness of form and emptiness offers a unique resolution to the One/Many problem that differs from both Plotinian emanation (hierarchical participation) and Advaitic maya (illusory appearance). In the Heart Sutra, the Many IS the One, and the One IS the Many; simultaneously, without hierarchy.

**The Mantra as Performative:** After 260 characters of philosophical negation, the sutra pivots to a mantra; an utterance that performs rather than describes. This shift from conceptual to performative mirrors contemplative traditions' insistence that ultimate reality is accessed through practice, not theory.
