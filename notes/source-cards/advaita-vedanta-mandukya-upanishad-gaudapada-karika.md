# Mandukya Upanishad with Gaudapada's Karika

Source ID: advaita-vedanta-mandukya-upanishad-gaudapada-karika
Tradition: Advaita Vedanta
Type: text
Agent: claude
Dating: Upanishad ~800-500 BCE; Karika ~7th century CE

## Core Claims

1. **Turiya (the Fourth):** Consciousness has four states — waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and turiya. Turiya is not a fourth state alongside the others but the ground of pure awareness within which the other three appear. Defined entirely via negation (Verse 7): "not inwardly cognitive, not outwardly cognitive, not cognitive both ways, not a cognition-mass, not cognitive, not non-cognitive."

2. **Ajativada (Non-origination):** Gaudapada's radical doctrine: nothing is ever truly born, created, or destroyed. From the absolute standpoint, there is no origination whatsoever. Karika 2.32: "There is no creation, no destruction, no bondage, no longing to be freed, no striving, nor anyone who has attained freedom."

3. **Waking = Dream:** Both waking and dream experience are equally unreal — both are perceived within consciousness and have no independent existence (Karika 2.4-6). The distinction between "real" waking and "unreal" dreaming is itself part of the dream.

## Key Verses

- **Verse 7** (turiya definition): The foundational negative definition of pure awareness
- **Karika 2.32** (non-origination): "No creation, no destruction, no bondage..."
- **Karika 4.22** (tetralemma): "Nothing is originated from itself, from another, from both, or from neither" — directly mirrors Nagarjuna's catuskoti
- **Karika 4.71**: "No jiva ever comes into existence. There exists no cause that can produce it."
- **Karika 4.47-50** (fire-brand metaphor): A whirling torch appears as a circle of fire; likewise consciousness appears as perceiver and perceived, but is actually motionless

## Cross-Tradition Significance

This is the single most important bridge text between Hindu and Buddhist non-dual philosophy. Gaudapada's ajativada and Nagarjuna's sunyata arrive at structurally identical negations of origination through similar logical steps. The critical divergence: Nagarjuna says "nothing has inherent existence" (no substratum); Gaudapada says "only the Unborn exists" (Brahman as permanent substratum). Same negation, opposite inference — a prime test case for Claude's "inferential gap" model.

Scholarly debate on Buddhist influence is extensive (Richard King, Dasgupta, Karmarkar). Karika Chapter 4 contains near-direct paraphrases of Madhyamaka sources.
