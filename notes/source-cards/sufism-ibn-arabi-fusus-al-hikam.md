# Ibn Arabi's Fusus al-Hikam (Bezels of Wisdom)

Source ID: sufism-ibn-arabi-fusus-al-hikam
Tradition: Sufism / Islamic Mysticism
Type: text
Agent: claude
Attribution: Muhyi al-Din Ibn al-Arabi (1165-1240 CE), "the Greatest Master"
Composed: 1229 CE, Damascus. Ibn Arabi claimed it was dictated by the Prophet Muhammad in a vision.
Structure: 27 chapters, each devoted to a prophet embodying a specific wisdom

## Core Claims

1. **Wahdat al-Wujud (Unity of Being):** "There is no wujud but God." Not pantheism — creation subsists through God's existence "like a shadow" or "forms in a mirror." CRITICAL: Ibn Arabi never used this term himself; it was coined polemically by Ibn Taymiyya decades later (per Stanford Encyclopedia, Ibn Arabi Society consensus).

2. **Tajalli (Divine Self-Disclosure):** Creation is God's self-disclosure to Himself. "Allah wanted to see His own Essence in one global object." The universe is an unpolished mirror; Adam is the polishing. Each creature represents a specific face of God. "There is no repetition in God's self-disclosure" — each moment is unique.

3. **Al-Insan al-Kamil (The Perfect Human):** The First Entification — primary manifestation through which all others emerge. "He is for God in the same place as the eye of the eye is for the man." Achieves the "Station of No Station" (maqam la maqam), transcending all particular stations while embracing them.

4. **Barzakh (Isthmus):** Ontological principle that simultaneously divides and unites two realities. The mundus imaginalis (alam al-mithal) — where spiritual beings are corporealized and corporeal beings spiritualized. Imagination is not merely human faculty but objective ontological dimension.

5. **A'yan al-Thabita (Immutable Entities):** "The immutable entities have never smelled the scent of existence." Fixed archetypes exist only in Divine Knowledge, never independently. God manifests within their conceptual forms while they remain in the "darkness" of non-existence.

## Cross-Tradition Significance

**Wahdat al-Wujud vs. Advaita Vedanta:** Both affirm non-duality. Both treat multiplicity as derivative of a singular principle. Key differences: Shankara's maya renders multiplicity illusory; Ibn Arabi's tajalli makes multiplicity real AS self-disclosure. The world "borrows" wujud from God like earth borrows light from the sun. Also: Vedanta affirms identity (Atman IS Brahman); Ibn Arabi maintains distinction between Necessary Being and contingent being.

**Barzakh and Tibetan Bardos:** The imaginal realm between spirit and matter has structural parallels to the bardo states — intermediate ontological dimensions where transformation occurs.

**Tajalli and Emanation:** Ibn Arabi's self-disclosure parallels Plotinus's emanation (the One overflows into Nous) but adds intentionality — God WANTS to see Himself. The Neoplatonic transmission through the Liber de Causis and Pseudo-Dionysius is a documented historical influence.

**The One-Many as Theophanic:** Unlike strict Advaita (multiplicity = maya) and strict Madhyamaka (multiplicity = sunyata), Ibn Arabi treats the Many as the One's way of knowing Itself. A distinctive third position on the One-Many problem.
