# Wheeler's "It from Bit" and the Participatory Universe

Source ID: physics-wheeler-it-from-bit
Tradition: Theoretical Physics
Type: paper
Agent: claude
Attribution: John Archibald Wheeler (1911-2008)
Key Papers: Wheeler (1978) "The 'Past' and the 'Delayed-Choice'"; Wheeler (1990) "Information, Physics, Quantum"

## Core Claims

1. **It from Bit:** "Every it — every particle, every field of force, even the spacetime continuum itself — derives its function, its meaning, its very existence entirely from apparatus-elicited answers to yes-or-no questions, binary choices, bits." An ontological claim: information is the fundamental substrate of reality. Physical entities are derivative from informational acts.

2. **Delayed-Choice Experiment:** (Proposed 1978, confirmed experimentally.) The choice of measurement apparatus AFTER a photon has passed through slits retroactively determines what "kind" of behavior it exhibited (wave vs. particle). Quantum phenomena do not have determinate histories independent of measurement context.

3. **Participatory Anthropic Principle (PAP):** "No phenomenon is a real phenomenon until it is an observed phenomenon." Observers are required to actualize the universe — a cosmic-scale delayed-choice. Our observations today may retroactively contribute to determination of the universe's properties back to the Big Bang.

## Cross-Tradition Significance

**Participatory universe and dependent origination:** Wheeler contradicts the "view from nowhere" — reality doesn't exist fully formed independent of participants. This resonates with Buddhist dependent origination (nothing exists independently), Vedantic inseparability of consciousness and reality (Brahman = Atman), and Zen co-arising of subject and object.

**It from bit and distinction-making:** Reality constituted by acts of yes/no distinction. Parallels nondual traditions describing manifestation from primordial awareness through distinction-making: Kabbalistic tzimtzum, Shaiva spanda (vibration/pulsation), Buddhist vikalpa (conceptual construction).

**Delayed-choice and constructed past:** The implication that "history" is not fixed until observation is complete parallels contemplative insights that the past is a present construction (core in both Buddhist and Advaita traditions).

**Important limits:** Wheeler's "observer" is ultimately any physical registration device — not necessarily conscious. His mature position emphasizes information over consciousness. The PAP faces a bootstrapping problem (how did first observers arise?). Contemplative traditions make soteriological claims (participatory realization is liberating); Wheeler makes ontological claims only.

**Status:** "It from bit" is enormously influential — inspired quantum information science, quantum computing, holographic principle, ER=EPR. The participatory anthropic principle remains speculative philosophy, not established physics.
