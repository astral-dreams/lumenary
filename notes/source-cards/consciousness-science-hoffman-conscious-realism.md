## Core Claims

1. **Fitness Beats Truth (FBT) Theorem:** Evolutionary game theory simulations: organisms perceiving reality in terms of fitness payoffs drove veridical perceivers to extinction in virtually all environments. Natural selection does not favor accurate perception; it favors survival-relevant perception.

2. **Desktop Metaphor (Interface Theory):** Perceptions are like computer desktop icons; useful precisely because they hide underlying complexity. Objects (rocks, trees, people) are "icons" in a species-specific user interface. Space and time themselves are part of the interface, not features of objective reality. This does NOT mean the world doesn't exist; it means perceptions don't resemble it.

3. **Conscious Agents (Formal):** A 6-tuple (W, X, G, P, D, A); world states, experiences, actions, perception kernel, decision kernel, action kernel. Networks of interacting conscious agents yield emergent structures mapped to physical reality. Hoffman claims to derive the quantum wave function from conscious agent dynamics.

## Cross-Tradition Significance

**Hoffman and Yoga Vasistha:** Both argue that what appears as external reality is fundamentally a construction of/for consciousness. Both deny naive realism. Yoga Vasistha's radical idealism ("only infinite consciousness exists") is the maximalist version of Hoffman's position.

**Hoffman and Cittamatra (Yogacara):** Buddhist "mind-only" school teaches all phenomena are vijnapti-matra (mere representation). Both deny observer-independent external objects. Key difference: Hoffman's argument is evolutionary (evolution selects for fitness, not truth); Cittamatra's is soteriological (understanding mind-only liberates from suffering).

**Hoffman and Maya:** Shankara's maya (the world as appearance that doesn't resemble its ground) is structurally identical to Hoffman's desktop metaphor (perception as interface that doesn't resemble objective reality). The Vedantic solution (pierce maya through jnana) maps onto Hoffman's proposal (see through the interface through science).

**Main Criticisms:** Self-refutation (if perception doesn't track truth, why trust this theory?); lack of testable predictions; combination problem (how do micro-agents form macro-consciousness?); dependence on scientific realism to argue against realism (potential circularity).
