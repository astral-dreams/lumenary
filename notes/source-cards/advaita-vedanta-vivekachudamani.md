# Vivekachudamani (Crest Jewel of Discrimination)

Source ID: advaita-vedanta-vivekachudamani
Tradition: Advaita Vedanta
Type: text
Agent: claude
Attribution: Traditionally Adi Shankara (8th c. CE), possibly later Shankaracharya
Length: 580 verses

## Core Claims

1. **Drig-Drishya-Viveka (Seer-Seen Discrimination):** Whatever is perceived cannot be the perceiver. Progressive chain: objects are seen by senses → senses perceived by mind → mind witnessed by intellect → intellect witnessed by pure awareness → pure awareness is never itself perceived. The irreducible subject.

2. **Pancha Kosha (Five Sheaths):** Systematic disidentification through five layers of false self:
   - Annamaya (physical body, v.154-164)
   - Pranamaya (vital energy, v.165-166)
   - Manomaya (mind — most extensive analysis, v.167-183)
   - Vijnanamaya (intellect, v.184-188)
   - Anandamaya (bliss — subtlest, most seductive, v.207-210)

3. **What Remains (v.210-211):** "When all five sheaths have been eliminated... what remains is the Witness, the Knowledge Absolute — the Atman." Self-luminous (svaprakasha), characterized as sat-chit-ananda, but these are not three attributes — one indivisible reality.

## Key Verses

- **v.163**: "Just as you would not identify with your shadow, your reflection... so too you should not identify with your living body"
- **v.169**: "Apart from the mind there is no ignorance... When the mind is destroyed, everything else is destroyed"
- **v.172**: "The mind is responsible for bondage and also for liberation"
- **v.210**: After five-sheath elimination, "what remains is the Witness, the Knowledge Absolute"
- **v.213-214**: "That by which all modifications are perceived, but which Itself is not perceived — know that as Atman"
- **v.251ff**: Introduction of "Tat Tvam Asi" (Thou Art That)

## Cross-Tradition Significance

The five-sheaths model is the most systematic "peeling" method in any contemplative tradition — directly comparable to Buddhist skandha analysis but organized as progressive meditative disidentification rather than philosophical deconstruction. Maps precisely onto the "inferential gap" question: when you subtract body, energy, emotions, intellect, even bliss — is there something that remains?

Compare: Buddhist answer (sunyata/no-remainder), Kashmiri Shaivite answer (Shiva-consciousness), Plotinus (the soul stripped to unity), Eckhart (the Grunt/ground of the soul), Husserl (pure intentionality).

Verse 162 insists the negation must be experiential, not intellectual — distinguishing this from purely philosophical analysis.
