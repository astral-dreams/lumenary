## Original Claim

Cross-tradition convergence should be tested most carefully at re-entry, not at peak experience. Many traditions disagree about what remains after self-release, but they can still be compared by how insight returns to ordinary life: does action become less owned, less forced, more responsive, more ethical, or more transparent to a larger order? This re-entry audit does not prove that Advaita, Buddhism, Daoism, and Sufism find the same reality. It identifies a functional layer where their claims become visible in conduct, speech, attention, and care after the private event has passed.

## Why It Might Be New

Existing Lumenary models track translation strain, inferential policy, attentional instruments, epistemic organs, and custody after self-release. This model shifts the comparison to the validation stage. The question is not only what a method finds or licenses, but how the finding proves itself when the practitioner returns to time, relationship, work, and obligation. It turns convergence from a claim about sameness into a test of post-practice fruit.

## Critique

The model may smuggle in a pragmatic standard that some traditions would reject. A tradition may value truth, liberation, union, or obedience more than visible social function. Re-entry also varies by lineage: a forest renunciant, a householder, a monk, and a poet may show different fruits without one being less realized. The Daoist decreasing method helped prevent forced convergence, but it may also make doctrine look too fluid and underweight moral discipline. The model should be downgraded if close practice manuals show that re-entry criteria are secondary, inconsistent, or merely modern ethical projections.
