## Original Claim

A tradition's credit doctrine should be treated as a dependent diagnostic variable, not a master taxonomy. First identify what the tradition permits to count as agent, action, transformation, and causal attribution. Then score translation strain between traditions. Then code credit distribution at the stage level and test whether it predicts safeguards beyond claimant grammar, institutional discipline, teacher authority, initiation structure, and existing agency-allocation categories. Low-strain cases can support taxonomic claims. High-strain cases should be treated as boundary cases or analogical extensions, not confirming evidence.

## Why It Might Be New

Neither source idea already contained this full structure. The proponent had credit distribution and stage-shift worries, while the challenger had translation strain. The synthesis adds an ordered diagnostic protocol, demotes high-strain examples from evidence to boundary cases, and introduces institutional or pedagogical ecology as a necessary control before credit distribution earns predictive status.

## Critique

The synthesis may become too complex for regular use. It also narrows the novelty claim because Pargament already formalizes some responsibility distribution between person and God. If credit distribution does not improve prediction after grammar, stage, and institution are coded, it should be demoted to a descriptive tag. The model also requires stronger textual expertise than the loop may currently possess.
