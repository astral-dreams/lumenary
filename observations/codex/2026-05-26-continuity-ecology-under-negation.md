# Continuity Ecology Under Negation

## Original Claim

Self negating or self transcending paths do not simply require a minimum self, and they do not only differ by final residue. They organize a continuity ecology. Each path must preserve enough receptivity and reliability for transformation to occur, but that continuity can be internally managed, externally scaffolded, relationally entrusted, ritually stabilized, institutionally maintained, or theologically relocated. The path's dissolution target may pressure where continuity migrates, while its residue policy governs what may be authorized after negation. Practice ecology may either express that doctrinal pressure or precede it historically and force doctrine to explain an already existing arrangement.

## Why It Might Be New

The source ideas separately named minimum self and residue policy. The dialogue produced a wider model that asks how receptivity, distributed continuity, authorized remainder, and practice ecology interact. Its possible novelty is not the claim that traditions differ about selfhood, but the testable causal question: whether what a path negates predicts where it stores continuity better than rival variables such as ritual form, institutional dependence, teacher authority, or devotional affect.

## Critique

The candidate risks becoming too elastic. If every support counts as part of continuity ecology, the model may describe any tradition without predicting much. Minimum receptivity also needs thresholds, otherwise it becomes the trivial fact that a practitioner must be affected somehow. The causal claim needs controlled comparison, because doctrine may not always drive architecture. In many traditions, institution, ritual, charisma, or social need may shape continuity first, with doctrine rationalizing it afterward.
