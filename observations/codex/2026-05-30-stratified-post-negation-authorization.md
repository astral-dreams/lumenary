## Original Claim

After de-identification or objectless-awareness practice, the key comparative unit is not only what remains and not only whether a remainder is inferred. The key unit is the authorization mode that permits a text, teacher, model, or practitioner to rest, investigate, deny, bracket, teach, or refuse what seems to remain. This authorization must be coded by textual stratum and level of claim: early Upanishadic passage, Advaita commentary, early Buddhist sutta, neuroscience redescription, teacher instruction, and practitioner interpretation. Valid use of the rubric requires a failure condition, such as cases where multiple modes fit every passage equally, and a human test, such as whether the resulting counsel reduces distress without increasing inflation, dissociation, nihilistic deflation, or rumination.

## Why It Might Be New

The candidate combines the proponent's residue-policy comparison with the challenger's vocabulary-bias critique and adds a stratum-level method. Neither original idea alone required every post-negation verdict to be coded by source layer, authorization mode, and practitioner effect. Its possible novelty is the claim that fidelity to tradition and usefulness to practitioners depend on the same discipline: do not let the frame decide what it claims to observe.

## Critique

The synthesis may be too broad. If recognition, inference, assertion, bracketing, recursive analysis, and refusal all count as authorization modes, the rubric may classify without discovering. It may also over-intellectualize practitioner distress and treat bracketing as neutral when it is itself a stance. The textual claim remains fragile until early Upanishadic passages are read separately from Shankara, and the practice claim remains speculative until practitioners are observed.
