# Agency-Authority Calibration at the Handoff

## Original Claim

Transformative practices should first be read through the practitioner's starting agency profile, then through staged handoff events in which capacities are confirmed, ownership is canceled, support is entrusted, or the claimant frame dissolves. The sequence matters: it should predict the practice's warnings, safeguards, repair practices, and non-fit cases. For over-claiming strivers, a practice may need to cancel ownership of a real capacity. For under-claiming people, especially those shaped by coercion, trauma, or denied authority, the first ethical move may be to confirm perception, rebuild boundaries, and permit agency before any surrender or dissolution language is introduced.

## Why It Might Be New

The proponent supplied a three-operation vocabulary, but located it too strongly at completion. The challenger supplied a staged handoff rubric, but did not fully develop cohort-gated agency calibration as the prior safety condition. The dialogue produced a candidate synthesis that treats authority transfer as one part of a broader agency-calibration grammar, with predictive and therapeutic constraints. This still needs an originality audit before any promotion.

## Critique

The synthesis may be too absorptive if every practice can be coded after the fact. Authority transfer may not be central to practices whose main work is nervous-system regulation, social belonging, habit repair, or basic agency building. The three operations may be too coarse, especially for entrusting, repairing, boundary-making, and communal identity formation. The main safety risk is applying surrender, cancellation, or claimant dissolution to people whose wound is denied agency.

## Next Research Directions

- Pre-register a coding rubric that starts with agency profile, then predicts handoff sequence, warnings, safeguards, repair practices, and non-fit cases before reading target manuals.
- Test sequence predictions: cancel-then-confirm should produce passivity or quietism safeguards, while confirm-then-cancel should produce pride, grasping, or spiritual possession warnings.
- Code contemplative, recovery, therapy, and grief cases separately, then compare whether the same handoff grammar survives across domains.
- Interview dual-trained practitioners and recovery participants about permitted first-person grammar: cultivation, entrusting, recognition, service, surrender, or ownership.
- Run a safety test with under-claiming cohorts, including survivors of spiritual abuse or coercive control, to see when cancellation language harms or must be delayed.
- Compare the model against religious coping styles, locus-of-control research, ego strength before no-self, trauma-informed therapy, and agency restoration literature.
