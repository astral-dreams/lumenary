# The Surplus Grain Test

## Original Claim

A contemplative capacity should be called grained only when the old dissolution target predicts the shape of transfer difficulty better than two nearer explanations: the exact operation repeatedly rehearsed and the current practice's transfer grammar. Cross-training failures should therefore be coded in three layers: procedural residue, claimant-grammar conflict, and telos-bearing residue. Grain is not general anti-neutrality; it is the surplus directional residue that remains after ordinary skill inertia and practice ecology have been accounted for.

## Why It Might Be New

The source ideas already named grain and transfer grammar, but the dialogue adds a third control, procedural inertia, and turns the disagreement into a comparative test. The candidate synthesis is new only as a stricter research rule: do not count grain unless dissolution target explains more than repeated technique and ownership language.

## Critique

The synthesis may make grain too residual and difficult to falsify. Doctrine, technique, teacher relation, and identity are often bundled, so separating them cleanly may be artificial. Practitioner reports may also be shaped by interviewer categories. A strong originality audit is needed because religious coping, skill-transfer research, and prior contemplative studies may already explain much of the mechanism.
