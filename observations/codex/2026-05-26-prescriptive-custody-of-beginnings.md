# Prescriptive Custody of Beginnings

## Original Claim

Transformative paths begin by placing unstable attention, trust, agency, and interpretation under some form of custody before the practitioner can fully authorize or understand the path. Traditions then give that custody an entry grammar. In inherited, apprenticeship, ritual, and gradual habituation contexts, the grammar may be mostly retrospective, naming a process already underway. In explicit incapacity traditions, the grammar can become prescriptive: it forbids some forms of self-initiation, effort, authority, or verification, and authorizes others. The beginning of a path is therefore not only a first break and not only social induction. It is a contested custody arrangement whose doctrine may be either narration or control surface.

## Why It Might Be New

The source ideas separately treated entry circularity and post-negation attentional ownership. The dialogue adds a third layer: the custody of the beginning itself, before stable agency or understanding is available. The prescriptive versus retrospective distinction gives the model a way to avoid universalizing first-break doctrine while still preserving cases where doctrine reorganizes practice. This synthesis appears stronger than either original idea because it explains why some traditions can begin through ordinary participation while others must regulate the very possibility of beginning.

## Critique

The candidate may still compress a messy causal ecology into one elegant frame. Entry doctrine, custody arrangements, institutional form, and sectarian identity often mature together, so the model could mistake co-development for doctrinal causation. Its key examples, such as Jodo Shinshu versus Jodo Shu or Dzogchen versus gradual Tibetan paths, are promising but not clean controls. The language of custody may also impose modern psychological categories on traditions whose primary terms are grace, liberation, realization, obedience, practice, or union. This candidate should remain research-level until an originality audit and process-tracing tests are completed.

## Next Research Directions

- Run an originality audit on the prescriptive custody frame, searching for close prior arguments in initiation theory, conversion studies, situated learning, theology of grace, Buddhist transmission studies, and anthropology of apprenticeship.
- Build a coding rubric that separates lived induction, entry doctrine, custody arrangement, effort theory, authority structure, verification method, and characteristic warning language.
- Use matched comparisons, especially Jodo Shinshu versus Jodo Shu, Dzogchen versus gradual Tibetan sutra-path practice, and explicit grace traditions versus neighboring cooperation traditions.
- Add negative cases where explicit incapacity doctrine exists without major institutional divergence, and cases where institutional divergence exists without explicit incapacity doctrine.
- Design practitioner interviews that ask what held attention, trust, and agency before the practitioner could explain the path, then compare those reports with later doctrinal interpretation.
