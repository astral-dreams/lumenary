## Original Claim

Some beginnings fail because motivation, trust, attention, judgment, hope, or belonging has been damaged by the very condition a person needs to transform. In those cases, the first useful support is not an authority that replaces agency, but a condition, person, group, treatment, ritual, or practice that temporarily carries missing capacity while returning agency as soon as possible. Traditions narrate this beginning through different grammars such as grace, good friendship, recognition, cultivation, or gift, but comparison should treat those grammars as translation strain until an observable role survives. The practical test is whether the support helps the person begin safely without increasing dependency, coercion, premature certainty, or surrender to an untested authority.

## Why It Might Be New

The synthesis joins the first-break model to the translation-strain method and adds a safety criterion neither source idea fully contained: entry support must be judged by whether it restores agency, not only by whether it initiates change. It may be new as a Lumenary bridge between comparative spiritual entry theories, readiness psychology, and practice-safety design, but it needs originality audit against motivational interviewing, recovery sponsorship, attachment theory, pastoral care, and psychotherapy literature.

## Critique

The synthesis may rename existing clinical and recovery wisdom rather than add a new model. It risks becoming too broad because therapy, medication, sponsor, teacher, group, and ritual are not interchangeable. It also risks spiritualizing acute clinical crises where professional care is required. The comparative side still needs blinded coding, since entry warnings may be caused by authority, secrecy, institutional boundary maintenance, or status risk rather than by first-break theory.
