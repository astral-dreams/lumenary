## Original Claim

A person rarely begins a serious practice by willpower alone. A crisis, a teacher, a family habit, a ritual, a trusted community, imitation, or bodily training can make the first step possible before the person can fully choose it. A path then decides what that beginning means: what effort should be praised, what pride should be corrected, what trust should be deepened, and what help should be acknowledged. The useful question is not only how practice starts. It is who or what makes the first step believable, repeatable, and safe.

## Why It Might Be New

Many models ask whether practice begins through effort, grace, insight, social support, or teacher authority. This finding separates those questions. Ordinary supports may get a person through the door, while the path decides what the beginning means and which claims of ownership must be corrected. The possible contribution is a cleaner way to study how teachers guide beginners: not only what brought the person in, but what the path allows the beginner to claim.

## Critique

The synthesis may become too inclusive and lose predictive sharpness. If every factor is included, it may explain divergence after the fact rather than predict it. It also risks measurement circularity if first-break grammar, warnings, and safeguards are coded from the same texts. The model needs independent corpora and paired comparisons before any originality or doctrine claim is warranted.
