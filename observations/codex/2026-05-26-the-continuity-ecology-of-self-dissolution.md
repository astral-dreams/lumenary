## Original Claim

Self-dissolving practice does not simply require a self or erase a self. It creates a continuity ecology. Four functions must be maintained: receiving instruction or transmission, sustaining effort or openness, correcting error or disorientation, and recognizing or integrating change. Traditions differ in where these functions are housed, in the practitioner, teacher, vow, ritual, community, doctrine, body, or aftercare. Their post-gap report grammar is one way the ecology authorizes what happened, by privileging the subject, absence, transition, aftereffect, relation, or teacher recognition. Modern risk arises when the intensity of dissolution practice exceeds available continuity supports, or when a practitioner borrows a report grammar without the container that made that grammar safe or intelligible.

## Why It Might Be New

The candidate combines three pressures that the source ideas held separately: minimum-self architecture, distributed continuity placement, and post-gap admissibility grammar. Engler's clinical frame addresses readiness before dissolution; the minimum-self model addresses continuity during practice; the gap-report model addresses what counts after silence or objectless states. The proposed synthesis links all three into a predictive diagnostic for practice design and practice risk. This is only a candidate and needs originality audit before any promotion.

## Critique

The model may be too comprehensive and may win by redescribing whatever a tradition already does. The receiving-surface claim risks being either trivial or too broad. Zahavi's minimal self supports first-personal givenness, but does not by itself establish thicker capacities such as trust, correction, and integration. The model could idealize traditional containers, which can protect practitioners but can also conceal abuse, dependency, or dissent suppression. It also needs sharper cohort boundaries and blind coding to avoid circular prediction.
