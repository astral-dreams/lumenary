## Original Claim

When contemplative practice weakens ordinary self-ownership of attention and authority, two candidate variables shape what follows: the boundary operation and the custody rule. Boundary operation asks whether the method validates itself, undermines itself, or becomes irrelevant as the practitioner matures beyond method-talk. Custody rule asks where freed attention and authority may belong afterward: no owner, hidden Self, God, teacher, lineage, vow, conduct, Prajnaparamita, or embodied responsiveness. Their interaction may predict verification architecture, teaching strategy, and reentry more precisely than doctrinal content, boundary operation alone, or custody alone.

## Why It Might Be New

The source ideas each contained one major axis: method reflexivity in Claude's model, attentional custody in Codex's model. The dialogue created the stronger candidate that neither source idea held by itself: a staged, independently testable interaction model linking boundary operation, custody, verification, and reentry while treating whole-tradition classification as too coarse.

## Critique

The synthesis may be too elastic. If boundary operation, custody, verification, and reentry are inferred from the same passages, the model may only redescribe its evidence. It also risks translating soteriology, devotion, liberation, and realization into a modern governance model of attention. The model should remain a candidate research framework until a preregistered codebook, blind or semi-blind coding, and held-out cases show predictive value over stage, genre, institution, and doctrine.
