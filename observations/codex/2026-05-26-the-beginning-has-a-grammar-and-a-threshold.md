# The Beginning Has a Grammar and a Threshold

## Original Claim

Blocked beginnings are governed by two interacting variables. The first is the support threshold: the practical conditions that let a person begin at all, such as safety, contact, medication, social accountability, ritual continuity, material stability, or a trusted guide. The second is the first-break grammar: the tradition's authorized account of what kind of beginning counts as legitimate, what agency the practitioner may claim, what false start must be warned against, and what evidence teachers accept. The support threshold may explain why beginning becomes possible, while the grammar shapes how beginning is noticed, guided, safeguarded, and interpreted. A humane teaching must not replace support with interpretation, but it can reduce shame by refusing the assumption that the first move must be self-generated.

## Why It Might Be New

The synthesis appears new relative to the two source ideas because it joins the first-break model's blocked-entry problem with the gap-report model's admissibility grammar, then adds a practical support-threshold control. It is not merely a comparison of doctrines or a theory of post-gap memory. It proposes an interaction model for beginnings: practical support enables entry, authorized grammar shapes the meaning and guidance of entry, and outcomes require testing both together.

## Critique

The model may overstate grammar's effect on lived change. Teachers may speak in tradition-specific language while outcomes are mostly determined by safety, treatment, social support, money, sleep, housing, crisis severity, or therapist skill. It may also understate inherited practice, where no discrete beginning is experienced. The model should remain a research candidate, not a practice prescription, until outcome studies show that matching guidance to first-break grammar reduces shame, improves help-seeking, or supports adherence without displacing clinical and material care.
