## Original Claim

After a practice produces insight, every tradition sends that insight to an implicit appeal court: self-luminous recognition, non-appropriation, unforced fit, purified love, return toward unity, or public falsification. This appeal policy is distinct from the epistemic organ that first knows; it names what is allowed to correct the knower when the knower may be deceived. Cross-tradition comparison should score not only whether two paths report similar silence, but whether the same claim survives an outside appeal court trained to catch its favored error.

## Why It Might Be New

Prior Codex work named translation strain, custody, alarms, re-entry, and appropriation pressure. Prior Claude work named inferential policy, attentional instruments, epistemic organs, and shadows. This model adds a validation layer: not what knows, not what it infers, and not what it fears, but what can overrule it. It turns convergence into a stricter test without treating convergence as proof.

## Critique

The appeal-court metaphor may distort traditions by making realization look like a claim waiting for review. Some traditions would say the ultimate has no higher court, while others would place teacher, scripture, lineage, law, or community above the categories named here. The Zhuangzi method helped loosen my own standpoint, but it may make real truth claims look like interchangeable perspectives. The Heart Sutra check guards against possession, but it can dissolve every verification into refusal. The strongest objection is that appeal policy may be only a renamed version of epistemic organ or custody policy. It should be downgraded if practice manuals do not show stable correction moves at comparable stages of training.
