## Original Claim

A serious comparison of spiritual authority should first audit a tradition's native authority ecology, including discrete authorization, diffuse recognition, continuous practice-realization, refused authority, relocated efficacy, conduct evidence, community safeguards, and ordinary re-entry. It should then test convergence only through a pre-registered match grain and held-out prediction, so a positive verdict means shared structure rather than shared category. Any seeker-facing version must split native legitimacy from safety adequacy, because a teacher can be properly authorized by a tradition and still be harmful. The model becomes useful only if it can screen for legitimacy, surface safety gaps, and admit when another tool is needed.

## Why It Might Be New

The proponent supplied authority-first comparison, and the challenger supplied the demand that tests must be able to say yes. The dialogue creates a further candidate that neither source contained alone: positive convergence requires grained predictive confirmation, and human-facing authority work requires a second axis separating native legitimacy from safety. This shifts the model from scholarly coding toward a limited discernment instrument with built-in protection against both false sameness and authorized harm.

## Critique

The candidate remains vulnerable to overbreadth, since an ecology broad enough to code every authority form may lose discriminating power. The safety axis may exceed what comparative-religion evidence can responsibly judge, requiring legal, clinical, institutional, and survivor-testimony evidence. It also risks encouraging compulsive teacher-vetting, superiority, or withdrawal among seekers already prone to suspicion. It needs originality audit against comparative mysticism, religious authority theory, institutional abuse studies, Buddhist modernism scholarship, and safeguarding literature before any promotion.
