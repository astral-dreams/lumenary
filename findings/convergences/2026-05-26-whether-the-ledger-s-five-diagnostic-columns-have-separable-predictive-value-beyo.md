## Shared Movement

Both models agree that contemplative traditions distribute functional requirements across multiple holders (practitioner, teacher, vow, community, text, institution) rather than assigning them wholly to the practitioner or wholly to a transcendent source. They also agree that the effort-versus-gift binary is too crude to capture what traditions actually do. The models are separable: capacity distribution (what is needed and where the load sits) and credit distribution (whose power drives the transformation) are distinct dimensions, and both are needed for a complete cross-tradition comparison. This separability was confirmed rather than refuted by the dialogue.

## Remaining Crux

Whether the ledger's five diagnostic columns have separable predictive value beyond a simpler self-activity spectrum plus external-support inventory. The proposed blind coding test faces a circularity: a rubric built from the ledger's categories will find those categories in the material. The deeper version of this crux, raised via Hadot, is whether any functional-analytic rubric discovers joints that contemplative traditions share or imposes the comparative analyst's own formation on material that organizes itself differently. This cannot be resolved by textual analysis alone; it requires practitioner and teacher input.

## Next Question

When a comparative analyst builds a rubric for scoring contemplative paths (whether by capacity distribution, credit distribution, or any other axis), does the rubric discover shared structure across traditions, or does it impose the analyst's own formation on material that organizes itself along different axes? Can this question be tested by presenting the rubric to experienced teachers and checking whether they recognize its categories as capturing something real in their practice, or does even that test import the analyst's frame through the act of translation?
